## Introduction

So you're expecting a baby — congratulations! But between the nursery planning and hospital bag packing, there's one family member who has no idea their world is about to change: your dog.

Dogs thrive on routine. They know when walks happen, where they sleep, who sits where on the sofa. A new baby turns all of that upside down — overnight. The good news? With proper preparation, most dogs adjust brilliantly. Many go on to become fiercely protective, gentle companions who form incredible bonds with their tiny humans.

This guide walks you through everything: what to do during pregnancy, how to handle the first meeting, and how to navigate the tricky first year when your dog and baby are learning to coexist.

> **Important:** This article provides general guidance based on advice from Dogs Trust, the RSPCA, and veterinary behaviourists. If your dog has a history of anxiety, reactivity, or aggression, consult a qualified behaviourist (ABTC-registered) before your baby arrives.

## Part 1: During Pregnancy (Start as Early as Possible)

The key principle: **introduce changes gradually, long before the baby arrives.** If your dog associates all the disruption with the baby's arrival, they're more likely to struggle.

### 1. Adjust Your Dog's Routine Now

Think honestly about how life will change:

- **Walks may be shorter** — especially in the first few weeks postpartum
- **Attention will be divided** — you'll have less time for play and cuddles
- **Access may be restricted** — the nursery, certain furniture, or rooms

Start making these changes *now*, not when you're sleep-deprived with a newborn.

**Practical steps:**
- If walks are currently 60 minutes, gradually reduce to 40 minutes and supplement with enrichment activities (snuffle mats, Kongs, puzzle feeders)
- Practice giving your dog less constant attention — build their independence with settle training
- If you'll restrict nursery access, install baby gates early and reward your dog for staying calm on the other side

### 2. Set Up Baby Equipment Early

Prams, Moses baskets, bouncer chairs, play gyms — they all look and smell weird to your dog. Dogs Trust recommends introducing these items **months** before your due date.

**How to do it right:**
- Bring items out one at a time over several weeks
- Let your dog approach and investigate at their own pace
- Pair new equipment with positive experiences (treats, chews, calm praise)
- Don't tell your dog off for sniffing — curiosity is healthy

**Pro tip:** Set up the pram and practice walking with it and your dog together. This is much harder than it sounds (trust us), and you'll want the logistics figured out before you're also juggling a baby.

### 3. Play Baby Sounds at Home

Crying, babbling, squealing — baby sounds are unlike anything your dog has heard before. Dogs Trust offers free [sound therapy clips](https://www.dogstrust.org.uk/dog-advice/understanding-your-dog/sound-therapy-for-pets) specifically designed to prepare dogs for new babies.

**The method:**
1. Start playing baby sounds at **very low volume** while your dog is relaxed
2. Pair the sounds with something your dog enjoys (chewing a Kong, mealtime)
3. Gradually increase the volume over weeks
4. If your dog shows any anxiety (panting, lip-licking, turning away), reduce the volume and slow down

### 4. Introduce Baby Smells

Ask friends or family with babies for unwashed blankets, muslins, or babygrows. Place these around your home so your dog becomes familiar with "baby smell" before the real thing arrives.

### 5. Brush Up on Basic Training

Your dog doesn't need to be Crufts-ready, but these skills become essential with a baby:

- **Settle/place command** — going to their bed and staying there calmly
- **Leave it** — for when baby drops food (this will happen A LOT)
- **Gentle/easy** — taking treats softly, being calm around small humans
- **Not jumping up** — critical when you're carrying a baby

If your dog doesn't have these nailed, now is the time to invest in training. The [ABTC](https://abtc.org.uk/) can help you find a qualified trainer near you.

### 6. Visit Your Vet

Book a health check before the baby arrives. Mention the upcoming arrival and discuss:

- Whether your dog would benefit from an [Adaptil](https://www.adaptil.com/uk) pheromone diffuser (clinically proven to reduce stress in dogs)
- Any behavioural concerns
- Ensuring vaccinations and parasite treatments are up to date
- Whether your dog's microchip details are current

## Part 2: When the Baby Arrives

### 7. The Hospital Stay: Introduce the Scent

If you're staying in hospital after the birth, ask your partner or a family member to bring a blanket or muslin with the baby's scent home to your dog. Place it where your dog can investigate it naturally — don't force it on them.

This bridges the gap between "unknown baby smell" and "oh, I know that scent."

### 8. The Homecoming: Keep It Calm

Your dog will be excited to see you — especially if you've been away for a day or two. Dogs Trust recommends:

1. **Have someone else hold the baby** while you greet your dog first
2. Let your dog's excitement settle before any introductions
3. Keep your tone calm and relaxed — dogs pick up on your energy
4. Don't make a huge fuss about the baby in front of the dog initially

### 9. The First Meeting: Step by Step

**What you'll need:**
- A helper (partner, family member)
- Treats for rewarding calm behaviour
- Your dog's lead (optional, depending on temperament)

**The process:**
1. Hold the baby and **stand up** (you can move away quickly if needed)
2. Let your dog approach naturally — don't force them
3. Allow a gentle sniff (feet first is a good starting point)
4. Praise calm behaviour immediately and have your helper give treats
5. If your dog loses interest and walks away, that's perfect — reward it
6. Keep the first meeting **short** (a few minutes is fine)

**Watch your dog's body language:**
- ✅ **Good signs:** Relaxed body, gentle sniffing, loose wagging tail, choosing to walk away
- ⚠️ **Caution signs:** Stiff body, hard stare, tucked tail, lip licking, whale eye (showing whites of eyes)
- 🚫 **Stop immediately:** Growling, lunging, snapping, raised hackles

### 10. The Golden Rule: Never Leave Them Alone Together

This cannot be stressed enough. **Never leave your dog and baby unsupervised, even for a moment.** This applies regardless of how gentle your dog seems.

Even the most placid dog can be startled by a sudden movement or cry. Baby gates, playpens, and closed doors are your best friends.

## Part 3: The First Year Together

### 11. Maintain Your Dog's Routine

It's easy to let your dog's needs slip when you're exhausted. But maintaining some consistency helps them feel secure:

- **Keep walk times as regular as possible** — even if they're shorter
- **Protect their safe space** — their bed/crate should be a baby-free zone
- **Schedule one-on-one time** — even 10 minutes of focused play makes a difference

**Tip:** If you can't manage long walks, use enrichment to tire your dog mentally. Frozen Kongs, lick mats, scatter feeding in the garden, and snuffle mats all provide excellent mental stimulation.

### 12. Create Positive Associations

You want your dog to think: "When the baby is around, good things happen to me."

- Give treats and attention when the baby is present (not only when baby is asleep)
- Include your dog in baby activities where safe — walks with the pram, family time in the living room
- Never punish your dog for showing interest in the baby — redirect instead

### 13. Baby Milestones That Affect Your Dog

Each developmental stage brings new challenges:

| **Baby Stage** | **What Changes for Your Dog** | **What to Do** |
|---|---|---|
| **0-3 months** | Crying, disrupted sleep, lots of visitors | Sound prep pays off now. Give dog escape routes from noise |
| **4-6 months** | Grabbing, reaching, rolling | Start teaching baby-free zones. Supervise closely |
| **6-9 months** | Crawling, pulling up | Critical phase — baby moves toward dog. Increase supervision |
| **9-12 months** | Walking, throwing food, loud play | Ensure dog has retreat space. Train "leave it" for dropped food |
| **12+ months** | Running, hugging, tail-pulling | Teach your child gentle hands. Never allow riding, hugging, or cornering the dog |

### 14. Teaching Your Child Dog Safety

As your baby grows into a toddler, they need to learn:

- **Gentle hands** — no pulling ears, tails, or fur
- **Dog's bed is their safe space** — never disturb a sleeping dog
- **No taking food or toys** from the dog
- **No cornering** — dogs should always have an escape route
- **No hugging** — most dogs tolerate it but don't enjoy it (despite what Instagram suggests)

## Part 4: Specific Advice for Common UK Breeds

### Labradors and Retrievers
Generally excellent with babies due to their gentle temperament. Watch for: over-enthusiasm (tail-wagging can knock a baby over), and they'll try to eat *everything* the baby drops.

### Spaniels (Cockers, Springers)
Usually adaptable and affectionate. Some can be resource-guarders — manage food and toys carefully around the baby.

### Staffies and Bull Breeds
Often incredibly gentle and devoted to children (historically called "nanny dogs"). Their strength means supervision is especially important during play.

### Terriers (Jack Russells, Yorkies, Westies)
Can be more reactive to sudden movements and noises. Extra sound preparation is worthwhile. Ensure they have plenty of escape routes.

### Older Dogs (8+ years)
May struggle with disruption to routine and could be less tolerant of grabbing/noise due to pain (arthritis, etc.). A vet check is especially important.

## Part 5: When to Get Professional Help

Seek help from an [ABTC-registered behaviourist](https://abtc.org.uk/find-a-clinical-animal-behaviourist/) if your dog:

- Shows aggression towards the baby (growling, snapping, lunging)
- Becomes excessively anxious (hiding, not eating, excessive panting)
- Guards resources more intensely
- Shows significant behaviour changes (destructiveness, house soiling)
- Has a bite history of any kind

**Don't wait.** Early intervention is always more effective. Your vet can provide a referral.

### Rehoming: The Last Resort

If your dog genuinely cannot adapt, Dogs Trust and the RSPCA can provide support and advice. Rehoming is always a last resort, but it's better than an unsafe situation for either your child or your dog.

## Final Thoughts

Bringing a baby home to a house with a dog is one of life's great adventures. Yes, it takes preparation. Yes, you'll be juggling a lot. But watching your child and your dog grow up together — developing that special bond that only dogs and kids share — is worth every bit of effort.

Start early, stay patient, and remember: your dog isn't being "naughty" when they struggle. They're adjusting to a seismic shift in their world. With your help, they'll get there.

And one day, you'll look over and see your toddler curled up next to your dog watching CBeebies, and you'll know it was all worth it. 🐾
