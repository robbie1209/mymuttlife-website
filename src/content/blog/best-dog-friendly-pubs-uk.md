## Why Dog-Friendly Pubs Are Having a Moment

There's something uniquely British about a good pub. Add a dog curled up by the fire, and you've got perfection.

The dog-friendly pub scene has exploded in recent years. With over **13 million dogs** in UK homes (PDSA PAW Report 2024) and a post-pandemic generation of owners who refuse to leave their pets behind, pubs have cottoned on. Many now offer **doggy menus**, treat jars on the bar, water bowls at every table, and even **"puppuccinos"** (frothed milk for your four-legged friend).

Websites like DoggiePubs.org.uk now list **over 5,000 dog-friendly pubs** across the UK — all added by recommendation, not paid listing. That's a lot of Sunday roasts you can enjoy with your best mate beside you.

But not all dog-friendly pubs are created equal. Some merely tolerate dogs in the beer garden; others roll out the red carpet with blankets, biscuit jars, and dedicated doggy areas. Here's our pick of the best.

## Best Dog-Friendly Pubs by Region

### 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 South West England

**1. The Pandora Inn, Falmouth, Cornwall**
Nestled on Restronguet Creek with its own pontoon, The Pandora is the kind of pub that makes you question every life decision that didn't lead you to Cornwall sooner. Dogs are welcomed in the bar and terrace, and there's direct access to creek-side walks. The thatched roof and flagstone floors are pure pub perfection.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in bar and terrace areas
- **Walks nearby:** Restronguet Creek coastal path, Carrick Roads estuary
- **Top tip:** Arrive by boat if you can — yes, really

**2. The Trout Inn, Lechlade, Gloucestershire**
On the banks of the Thames (near its source), The Trout is a Cotswolds gem with a massive beer garden that slopes down to the water. Dogs can splash about while you work through their local ale selection.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome throughout, water bowls provided
- **Walks nearby:** Thames Path, Buscot Park
- **Top tip:** Great stop on a Thames Path walking weekend

**3. The Hive Beach Café, Burton Bradstock, Dorset**
Technically more café than pub, but they serve local ales and cider with some of the best views on the Jurassic Coast. Dogs welcome inside and out. The beach is directly accessible — perfect for a post-lunch paddle.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome everywhere
- **Walks nearby:** South West Coast Path, Chesil Beach
- **Top tip:** Check seasonal beach dog restrictions (typically May–September on some sections)

### 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 South East England

**4. The Royal Oak, Fritham, New Forest, Hampshire**
A proper no-frills New Forest pub with no kitchen — just ploughman's lunches, local ales, and a massive garden where your dog can roam. Surrounded by open forest, it's the ideal pit stop on a New Forest walk. Ponies and donkeys may wander past. This is the real deal.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome throughout (it's the New Forest — dogs are part of the furniture)
- **Walks nearby:** Fritham Plain, Eyeworth Pond, Islands Thorns Inclosure
- **Top tip:** Cash only. No food service — bring your own or enjoy their legendary ploughman's

**5. The Anchor Bleu, Bosham, West Sussex**
Right on Bosham Harbour with views across Chichester Harbour. Dogs welcome in the bar area. The village is famously where King Canute tried to hold back the tide — your Labrador will almost certainly try the same.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in bar area and garden
- **Walks nearby:** Bosham shoreline walk, Chichester Harbour AONB
- **Top tip:** Check tide times — the car park floods at high tide (not a joke)

### 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 The Midlands

**6. The Old Gate Inn, Brassington, Derbyshire**
A 17th-century pub in the Peak District with stone floors, real ales, and a genuinely warm welcome for dogs. The kind of place where the landlord knows every regular dog's name (and possibly forgets the owners').

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in bar and snug areas
- **Walks nearby:** High Peak Trail, Carsington Water
- **Top tip:** Try the steak and ale pie — widely regarded as one of the Peak District's best

**7. The Fleece Inn, Bretforton, Worcestershire**
A National Trust-owned medieval pub with pewter tankards hanging from the ceiling and a tradition of asparagus auctions. Dogs are welcome in the bar, and the Cotswold/Vale of Evesham walking is superb.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in bar area
- **Walks nearby:** Bredon Hill, Broadway Tower
- **Top tip:** Visit during the asparagus season (April–June) for the annual auction

### 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿 North of England

**8. The Tan Hill Inn, Swaledale, North Yorkshire**
Britain's highest pub at 1,732 feet above sea level. If your dog can handle the walk up (they'll love it), you'll be rewarded with roaring fires, hearty food, and views that go on forever. They even host a dog-friendly music festival.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome throughout — they're a rural pub, dogs are expected
- **Walks nearby:** Pennine Way, Swaledale valley walks
- **Top tip:** Book accommodation if you want to stay — it's remote and glorious

**9. The Drunken Duck, Ambleside, Lake District**
Tucked away above Ambleside with its own microbrewery (Barngates Brewery), The Drunken Duck offers spectacular fell views and a warm welcome for dogs in the bar. The surrounding walks are world-class.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in the bar area
- **Walks nearby:** Loughrigg Fell, Tarn Hows, Ambleside circular walks
- **Top tip:** The beer is brewed on-site — try the Tag Lag or Cracker Ale

**10. The Philharmonic Dining Rooms, Liverpool**
Grade I listed (one of only two pubs in the UK with that distinction), the Phil is an architectural marvel. Dogs are welcome in the bar area, and it's a must-visit for the ornate interior alone — marble, mosaics, and copper panels. Your dog won't appreciate the décor, but you will.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in bar area
- **Walks nearby:** Sefton Park (15 min walk), Liverpool waterfront
- **Top tip:** Even if you're not drinking, pop in to see the famous Gents' toilets (Grade I listed in their own right)

### 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 Scotland

**11. The Clachaig Inn, Glencoe, Scottish Highlands**
After a day walking in Glencoe (quite possibly the most dramatic landscape in Britain), The Clachaig is where you collapse into a chair with your dog, a dram, and a deep sense of satisfaction. Dogs welcome in the Boots Bar — named because you can walk in straight from the hills.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in Boots Bar (muddy boots and dogs equally welcome)
- **Walks nearby:** Lost Valley, Signal Rock, Buachaille Etive Mòr
- **Top tip:** The Boots Bar has over 300 whiskies. Pace yourself.

**12. The Drovers Inn, Inverarnan, Loch Lomond**
Established in 1705, this is possibly Scotland's most atmospheric pub. Stuffed animals line the walls, kilted staff serve you, and your dog will fit right into the slightly chaotic, wonderfully eccentric vibe.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome throughout — the pub's own dog probably greets yours
- **Walks nearby:** West Highland Way (it's literally on the route), Loch Lomond
- **Top tip:** Stay overnight in one of the rooms — some are allegedly haunted

### 🏴󠁧󠁢󠁷󠁬󠁳󠁿 Wales

**13. The Felin Fach Griffin, Brecon, Powys**
A gastropub with rooms in the Brecon Beacons (now Bannau Brycheiniog). Dogs welcome in the bar, and the food is exceptional — locally sourced, seasonally driven. This is where you come when you want a proper foodie pub day with your dog.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome in bar and some accommodation rooms
- **Walks nearby:** Pen y Fan, Brecon Beacons circular walks
- **Top tip:** Book a dog-friendly room and make a weekend of it

**14. Tŷ Coch Inn, Porthdinllaen, Gwynedd**
One of the most photographed pubs in Wales, sitting directly on the beach of a tiny fishing village accessible only on foot. Dogs are welcome, and the setting is absolutely extraordinary — sand between your toes, a pint in hand, and your dog digging holes beside you.

- **Dog policy:** Welcome inside and on the beach
- **Walks nearby:** Llŷn Peninsula coastal path
- **Top tip:** Walk in from Morfa Nefyn golf club car park (20 minutes). No road access.

## Dog Pub Etiquette: The Unwritten Rules

Taking your dog to the pub should be relaxing for everyone. Here's the etiquette that'll make you a welcome regular:

### Before You Go

- **Check the policy first.** "Dog-friendly" can mean anything from "dogs welcome everywhere" to "dogs in the garden only." Phone ahead or check their website.
- **Make sure your dog is pub-ready.** A dog that sits calmly under a table is a pub dog. A dog that barks at every passing plate of chips is a park dog (for now).
- **Time it right.** If your dog gets anxious in crowds, avoid peak Friday/Saturday evening. Quiet Tuesday lunch? Perfect.

### What to Bring

Every good pub dog owner packs a **pub bag**. Here's what goes in:

- **[Collapsible water bowl](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B07WDKQ7BN?tag=mymuttlife-21)** — Many pubs provide bowls, but don't rely on it
- **[Long-lasting chew or treat](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B0BM3Y9VX3?tag=mymuttlife-21)** — A stuffed Kong or Yakers chew buys you at least one course
- **Lead** — Even if your dog has perfect recall, pubs mean on-lead
- **[Travel blanket or mat](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B08H1YZ1YX?tag=mymuttlife-21)** — Gives your dog a familiar "spot" to settle on
- **Poo bags** — Obviously, but you'd be surprised
- **[Dog towel](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B0C1K3KZZN?tag=mymuttlife-21)** — If there's been a muddy walk beforehand

### At the Pub

✅ **Do:**
- Keep your dog on a lead or under close control at all times
- Choose a spot out of the main walkway (corner tables are gold)
- Ask staff where dogs are allowed — don't assume
- Clean up immediately if there's an accident
- Bring your dog's water rather than asking for a kitchen bowl

❌ **Don't:**
- Let your dog approach other dogs or diners without asking
- Put your dog on furniture (even if they give you *that look*)
- Feed your dog pub food — onion gravy, garlic bread, and chocolate desserts are all toxic to dogs
- Let them beg at other tables (train a solid "place" command first)
- Tie them up outside unsupervised

## The Legal Bit: Are Dogs Actually Allowed in Pubs?

Quick answer: **it's up to the pub.**

UK law (Food Safety Act 1990 and Food Hygiene Regulations) states that dogs are **not allowed in areas where food is prepared**. But bars, lounges, beer gardens, and even dining rooms? That's entirely at the landlord's discretion.

The Food Standards Agency guidance says: *"It is for the food business operator to decide whether to allow customers' dogs access to certain parts of their premises."*

**Key points:**
- **Assistance dogs** have a legal right of access everywhere under the Equality Act 2010 — pubs cannot refuse them
- **Pet dogs** can be refused at the pub's discretion
- **Beer gardens** are almost always fine
- **Kitchen areas** are always off-limits (for obvious reasons)
- Most pubs that welcome dogs will have a clear policy — if in doubt, just ask

## Training Your Dog for Pub Visits

Not every dog is born pub-ready. Here's how to build up to it:

### Stage 1: Settle Practice at Home
Teach a solid "place" or "mat" command. Your dog should be able to lie on their bed/blanket calmly for 30+ minutes before you try a pub.

**What you need:**
- [Portable dog mat](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B08H1YZ1YX?tag=mymuttlife-21) — This becomes their "pub spot"
- High-value treats for rewarding calm behaviour
- A long-lasting chew for distraction

### Stage 2: Café Test
Dog-friendly cafés are lower-stakes than pubs. Less noise, shorter visits, easier to leave if it goes wrong. Try 20-minute coffee stops first.

### Stage 3: Quiet Pub Visit
Choose a quiet weekday lunchtime. Sit in a corner, give your dog their mat and a chew, and keep it to 45 minutes max. Reward calm behaviour heavily.

### Stage 4: Graduation
Once your dog can settle happily in a quiet pub, gradually increase the challenge — busier times, longer visits, different venues. You'll know they've graduated when they curl up and fall asleep within five minutes of sitting down.

**Breeds that typically take to pubs naturally:** Labradors, Golden Retrievers, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels, Greyhounds (champion pub dogs — they sleep 18 hours a day anyway), Whippets, and most older dogs.

**Breeds that may need more training:** High-energy working breeds (Collies, Spaniels), anxious breeds, reactive dogs, and puppies under 6 months.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I take my dog to Wetherspoons?
Most Wetherspoons allow dogs in outdoor areas only, but policies vary by location. Check the individual pub's page on the Wetherspoons website or call ahead.

### Do dog-friendly pubs charge for dogs?
Very rarely for day visits. Some pub accommodation charges £10–20 per night per dog for overnight stays. Day visits are almost always free.

### What age can I take my puppy to a pub?
Once they've had their full vaccinations (usually around 12 weeks) and are comfortable around people and noise. Start with short, quiet visits and build up gradually.

### Are all beer gardens dog-friendly?
Most are, but not all. Some pubs with food-focused beer gardens restrict dogs. Always check, especially in summer.

### Can pubs refuse my assistance dog?
No. Under the Equality Act 2010, assistance dogs have a legal right of access to all areas. If a pub refuses your assistance dog, this is a legal matter — contact the Equality Advisory Support Service.

### What if my dog has an accident in the pub?
It happens. Clean it up immediately and thoroughly. Apologise to staff. If your dog isn't reliably toilet-trained, stick to beer gardens for now. Most pub staff are understanding — it's the owners who leave it that get banned.
