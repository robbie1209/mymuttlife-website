## Introduction

Your dog communicates constantly. The question is whether you're listening.

Most owners focus on the obvious — a wagging tail must mean happy, right? But a wagging tail is one signal in a whole conversation. Dogs communicate through the position of their ears, the tension in their body, the direction of their gaze, the curve of their spine, and dozens of micro-signals that most people miss entirely.

Missing those signals isn't just about misunderstanding your dog. It can lead to preventable bites, anxiety that goes unaddressed for years, and a relationship where your dog feels chronically unheard.

This guide covers the science of dog body language — from calming signals to play invitations, from early stress indicators to the warning signs that precede a bite. We'll also look at how breed differences affect communication, and how to tell a happy dog from one that's just about coping.

*Sources used: Dogs Trust, PDSA, Blue Cross, Association of Pet Behaviour Counsellors (APBC), British Veterinary Behaviour Association (BVBA), Dr Sophia Yin (Low Stress Handling), Turid Rugaas (On Talking Terms with Dogs).*

## The Foundation: Dogs Communicate in Full Sentences

Norwegian dog trainer and author Turid Rugaas coined the term "calming signals" in the 1990s — over 30 behaviours she identified as dogs' way of communicating stress, deescalating conflict, and reassuring themselves and others.

Since then, the science has expanded significantly. We now understand that dogs use:

- **Calming signals** — to reduce tension and avoid conflict
- **Stress signals** — involuntary physical responses to anxiety
- **Fear/threat signals** — to signal discomfort or warn others off
- **Appeasement signals** — to communicate submission or non-threat
- **Play signals** — invitations to interact

Reading your dog means combining all these signals, not cherry-picking one.

## Part 1: The Tail — Much More Than "Wagging = Happy"

### Tail Position

The tail's *resting position* varies dramatically by breed — a Pug's tail is naturally curled, a Greyhound's naturally low. Always assess tail position relative to that dog's neutral baseline, not against a universal standard.

| Tail Position | What It Usually Means |
|--------------|----------------------|
| High, stiff, flagging (fast, rigid) | High arousal — confidence, alertness, or threat |
| Mid-height, soft wag | Relaxed, happy, content |
| Level with spine, soft | Engaged but calm |
| Low, loose wag | Tentative, slightly uncertain |
| Tucked between legs | Fear, submission, pain |
| Slowly wagging low | Appeasement or anxiety |

### The Direction of the Wag Matters

Research from the University of Trento (2013, Current Biology) found that dogs wag more to the *right* when they see something positive (their owner), and more to the *left* when they see something threatening. This reflects lateralisation of the brain — similar to human handedness.

You probably won't be able to spot this in real time, but it's a useful reminder that tail wagging is complex, directional, and context-dependent.

### Common Misreadings
- ❌ "He's wagging, so he's fine" — a high, stiff, rapid wag can precede a bite
- ❌ "Tail between legs means guilty" — this is fear or submission, not guilt (dogs don't experience guilt the way we do)
- ✅ Look for softness: a soft, full-body wag that includes the hips is almost always a good sign

## Part 2: Ears — The Mood Barometer

Again, breed baseline matters enormously. A Basset Hound's ears hang naturally; a German Shepherd's stand erect. Read ears relative to *that dog's normal*.

| Ear Position | What It Usually Means |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Erect, forward-facing | Alert, interested, focused |
| Erect, rotated back slightly | Conflict — alert but uncertain |
| Relaxed, slightly to the side ("airplane ears") | Happy, calm |
| Pinned flat against the head | Fear, appeasement, submission |
| One ear forward, one back | Mixed feelings — ambivalence |

### Key Signal: Ear Tension
It's not just position but *tension*. Relaxed ears — even upright ones — have a softness to them. Tense ears are held rigidly, often with facial tension (furrowed brow, tightened muscles around the eyes).

## Part 3: Eyes — Windows Into Emotional State

### Pupil Size
Dilated pupils signal arousal — which can mean excitement *or* fear. You can't tell which from pupils alone, but combined with other signals, dilation is a useful indicator.

### The Whale Eye
"Whale eye" describes the crescent of white sclera visible at the corner or edge of the eye — the dog is turning their head away but keeping their gaze fixed. This is a significant stress/warning signal often seen just before a bite.

A dog should never be approached when showing whale eye.

### Soft Eyes vs Hard Eyes
- **Soft eyes:** slightly squinted, relaxed, blinking naturally — relaxed, comfortable dog
- **Hard eyes:** wide, fixed, unblinking stare — threat display or intense focus. A hard stare is a warning. Do not stare back.

### The "Look Away"
Direct eye contact is challenging in dog communication. When a dog breaks your gaze, looks away, or blinks slowly, they are communicating appeasement or discomfort. Humans instinctively meet a dog's gaze — but many dogs find prolonged eye contact deeply uncomfortable.

*Dogs Trust guidance:* When greeting an unfamiliar dog, avoid direct eye contact. Look slightly to the side.

## Part 4: Calming Signals — The Language of Deescalation

Turid Rugaas documented over 30 calming signals. These are the signals dogs use to tell you, another dog, or themselves: *"I want to reduce tension here."*

They appear when a dog is:
- Mildly stressed
- Attempting to calm down an approaching dog or person
- Feeling conflicted or uncertain
- Trying to stop an interaction they don't want

### The 12 Most Common Calming Signals

**1. Yawning**
Not because they're tired. A yawn in a tense situation is almost always a calming signal. Dogs yawn at vets, during training when frustrated, and when an unfamiliar person approaches.

**2. Lip Licking / Tongue Flick**
A quick flick of the tongue toward the nose. Easily missed — it happens in milliseconds. One of the earliest signs of discomfort.

**3. Looking Away / Turning the Head**
Deliberately breaking gaze. A dog that turns their head away from a staring human or an approaching dog is asking for space.

**4. Turning the Body / Showing the Side**
A full turn away, or showing their broadside rather than facing directly. Conflict-reducing — "I'm not a threat."

**5. Sitting or Lying Down When Approached**
When another dog rushes toward them, the calming response is to sit or drop to the ground. It's the equivalent of taking the heat out of a situation.

**6. Sniffing the Ground**
When a dog suddenly becomes very interested in the floor mid-approach, they're not distracted — they're deescalating. This is one of the most common calming signals and one of the most frequently ignored by owners who interpret it as "bad recall" or "not listening."

**7. Walking in a Curve**
Dogs naturally approach each other in arcs — a direct, straight-line approach is rude in dog language. A dog that curves their path when approaching you or another dog is being polite.

**8. Blinking / Squinting Slowly**
Soft, slow blinks communicate non-threat and calmness. You can use this yourself: a slow blink at a stressed dog can genuinely help.

**9. Freezing**
Temporary stillness — a pause in motion. At low intensity, this is a calming signal; at high intensity, it precedes an escalation. Context matters.

**10. Pawing / Gentle Touch**
A dog that reaches out to paw gently at you or another dog is often making a soft contact request — "can we interact gently?"

**11. Play Bow**
Front legs extended, rear end up, tail wagging. This one most people know — it's an explicit invitation to play. Importantly, it also appears during rough play to reset the interaction and signal "this is still play."

**12. Moving Slowly**
Deliberate slowing of pace when approaching. A dog that slows down as another dog approaches is communicating non-aggression.

## Part 5: Stress Signals — What Chronic Stress Looks Like

Unlike calming signals (which are deliberate communication), stress signals are often *involuntary physical responses*. If you see these regularly, your dog is experiencing more stress than they're showing you through overt behaviour.

### Physical Stress Signals

| Signal | What It Looks Like |
|--------|-------------------|
| Shedding | Sudden hair loss mid-interaction (you'll see fur float off the dog) |
| Panting | When not hot — panting at the vet, in the car, during greeting |
| Drooling | Excessive salivation without food present |
| Sweating | Wet paw prints on the exam table (dogs sweat through paws) |
| Trembling | Shaking when not cold |
| Digestive upset | Loose stools, vomiting in association with a stressor |
| Increased heart rate | Visible pulse in the flank or temple |

### Behavioural Stress Signals

- **Excessive self-grooming** — licking paws, flanks, or genitals
- **Pacing** — repeated route-walking when unable to settle
- **Displacement behaviours** — sniffing, scratching, yawning in inappropriate contexts
- **Hypervigilance** — constantly scanning the environment, unable to relax
- **Loss of appetite** in a dog that normally eats enthusiastically
- **Excessive vocalisation** — whining, barking at low-level stimuli
- **Attention-seeking** or sudden clinginess

**If you see three or more of these regularly, speak to your vet.** Chronic stress has real health consequences — it suppresses the immune system, disrupts gut flora, and correlates with a shortened lifespan. Your vet can refer you to an APBC-accredited behaviourist.

## Part 6: The Escalation Ladder — Understanding Bite Prevention

Dogs rarely bite without warning. The problem is that the warnings are often subtle, misread, or punished out of the dog over time (which is dangerous — a dog that's been corrected for growling may skip the warning and go straight to biting).

The **Ladder of Aggression** (originally developed by Kendal Shepherd) describes the escalating sequence of signals a dog displays when uncomfortable:

### The Full Ladder (Low to High)

1. Yawning, blinking, nose licking
2. Turning head away
3. Turning body away, sitting, pawing
4. Walking away
5. Creeping, ears back, tail low
6. Standing tall, staring, tail high
7. **Growling**
8. **Snapping — biting the air**
9. **Biting, leaving no mark**
10. **Biting, bruising**
11. **Biting, puncturing**
12. **Repeated biting, shaking**

### What This Means in Practice

- A dog that is repeatedly put in situations where they must escalate to step 7+ will eventually become a dog that bites
- Punishing growling removes a warning signal — it does not remove the underlying discomfort
- The goal is to notice steps 1–5 and *change the situation* before escalation
- Children, who tend to approach dogs directly and make sustained eye contact, are the most common bite victims — not because dogs "go for children" but because children are poor at reading early signals

*PDSA advice:* Never punish growling. A growl is communication, not aggression. Respond by removing the stressor.

## Part 7: Play Signals — What Play Actually Looks Like

Play has a different texture to conflict — but rough play can look alarming to owners who don't know what to look for.

### Signs of Genuine Play
✅ **Play bow** — front down, rear up, tail wagging
✅ **Role reversal** — dogs swap being "on top" and "underneath"
✅ **Exaggerated, bouncy movements** — movements are sloppy and theatrical
✅ **Self-handicapping** — the bigger/stronger dog lets themselves be caught or pinned
✅ **Voluntary returns** — both dogs choose to return to each other after breaks
✅ **Relaxed, open mouth** — loose jaw, sometimes panting
✅ **Reciprocal** — both dogs are engaged, not just one chasing the other

### Signs Play Is Becoming a Problem
⚠️ One dog consistently trying to leave and being stopped
⚠️ Play bows stop — interaction becomes linear/one-directional
⚠️ Vocalisations shift from play growls (rhythmic, medium pitch) to sharp, high yelps
⚠️ One dog showing stress signals (lip licking, whale eye, ears back)
⚠️ "Checking in" stops — neither dog pauses to look back at owners

**The 3-Second Rule:** In a play session, interrupt and pause every 3 seconds. A dog that wants to continue will rush back to play — a dog that needed a break will walk away. This is an easy way to assess consent on both sides.

## Part 8: Breed-Specific Differences in Communication

Not all dogs communicate the same way. Selective breeding has altered body language capacity across breeds — sometimes with serious consequences.

### Flat-Faced Breeds (Brachycephalics)
**Breeds:** French Bulldog, English Bulldog, Pug, Boston Terrier

These dogs have physically compromised signals. Shortened muzzles mean reduced capacity for facial expression. Other dogs often misread brachycephalic dogs as threatening or challenging — because their face doesn't give the soft, rounded signals other dogs expect.

> Research note: A study in Applied Animal Behaviour Science (2018) found that brachycephalic dogs received more aggressive responses from other dogs than medium-faced dogs displaying identical behaviours — attributed to the unintentional "hard stare" their facial anatomy creates.

**Practical advice:** Supervise interactions with unfamiliar dogs carefully. Don't assume another dog being wary of your French Bulldog is "unfriendly" — it may be responding to signals your dog is inadvertently sending.

### Herding Breeds
**Breeds:** Border Collie, German Shepherd, Belgian Malinois, Australian Shepherd

These breeds were bred with an intense, forward-fixed stare as part of their working drive. The herding "eye" (stalking stare) can be misread by other dogs as a threat display. Interactions with unfamiliar dogs benefit from managed, calm introductions.

Border Collies in particular often show stalking body posture — low, creeping approach — which is normal working behaviour but can alarm both dogs and owners who don't recognise it.

### Greyhounds and Sighthounds
**Breeds:** Greyhound, Lurcher, Whippet, Saluki

Sighthounds communicate largely through movement. Their prey drive activates through movement-triggered instinct — it's not aggression. However, their body language in high-arousal states can look alarming (hard stare, forward posture, rigid body) before they switch from "watched" to "chased" mode.

Most retired racing Greyhounds are calm, gentle dogs — but their low body fat, short coat, and deep chest make physical stress signals (shivering, muscle tremors) more visible. This is normal thermoregulation, not necessarily anxiety.

### Heavy-Coated Breeds
**Breeds:** Chow Chow, Akita, Malamute, Newfoundland

Dense, heavy coats mask many body language signals. Ear position, tail position, and coat-level muscle tension are harder to read. These breeds require closer attention to eye signals, breathing rate, and movement quality.

### Docked or Bob Tails
Dogs born with or bred for short tails (Pembroke Welsh Corgi, Australian Shepherd, Boxer) have a reduced tail communication repertoire. Research from the University of Victoria (2007) showed that dogs with shorter tails received more negative approaches from other dogs than long-tailed dogs displaying identical body language.

## Part 9: Reading Your Dog in Everyday Situations

### At the Vet
Almost all dogs find vets stressful. Look for:
- Lip licking and yawning in the waiting room (stress)
- Whale eye during examination
- Paw sweating on the table
- Excessive shedding during handling

**What helps:** Ask your vet about "Low Stress Handling" (as developed by Dr Sophia Yin). Many UK vets now use this approach. Fear Free certified vets are listed at fearfreepets.com.

**Products that can help:**

- [Adaptil Transport Spray](affiliate link) — spray on bedding in carrier 15 minutes before travel

### Meeting New Dogs
✅ Allow approach in an arc — never direct
✅ Let both dogs sniff briefly, then move on
✅ Watch for both dogs' calming signals — and respect them
✅ Keep leads loose — a tight lead communicates tension to the dog and restricts communication
❌ Don't allow one dog to bowl straight into another
❌ Don't hold your dog up (makes them more vulnerable and prevents normal communication)
❌ Don't force interaction — if your dog walks away, the other dog must not follow

### Meeting New People
Many dogs are uncomfortable with strangers who approach directly, make eye contact, and reach toward their face. The correct way to greet an unfamiliar dog:
1. Ask the owner if it's okay
2. Crouch sideways — don't stand directly facing the dog
3. Look to the side, not at the dog
4. Let the dog approach you — never reach toward the dog
5. Pet under the chin first, not over the head (patting the head is threatening from a dog's perspective)

### Greeting After Time Apart
A dog that greets you frantically every time you return may not be expressing joy — they may be expressing relief from anxiety. True separation anxiety involves distress *during* your absence, not just at reunion. If your dog is destructive, vocalises excessively, or eliminates indoors while you're gone, speak to your vet.

If reunion greetings are becoming over-the-top, calmly ignore until your dog settles, then greet quietly. This reduces the emotional charge of departure and return.

## Part 10: The Happy Dog — What Contentment Looks Like

After all the warnings, it's worth describing what a genuinely settled, happy dog looks like:

✅ **Body:** Loose, slightly wiggly — no rigidity. Muscles are soft.
✅ **Tail:** Mid-height, full-body wag or gentle sway
✅ **Eyes:** Soft, slightly squinted, normal pupil size, blinking naturally
✅ **Mouth:** Relaxed, slightly open, lips soft — often looks like a "smile"
✅ **Ears:** In neutral position for their breed — not pinned or rigidly forward
✅ **Movement:** Easy, bouncy, approaches people with a curved, wiggly path
✅ **Breathing:** Relaxed — not panting unless post-exercise
✅ **Engagement:** Checks in with you, responds to their name, comfortable being approached

A dog can be excited *and* happy — or excited *and* anxious. The key marker is **muscle tension and facial softness**. Excited-and-happy is loose and wiggly. Excited-and-anxious is tighter, harder, with more controlled movement.

## When to Get Professional Help

If your dog regularly shows:
- Growling, snapping, or biting
- Extreme fear (hiding, trembling, inability to function)
- Reactivity to other dogs or people that's getting worse
- Suspected separation anxiety

You need a qualified behaviourist — not a "dog trainer." Look for:

- **APBC accredited** (Association of Pet Behaviour Counsellors) — apbc.org.uk
- **ABTC registered** (Animal Behaviour and Training Council) — abtc.org.uk
- **CCAB** (Certificated Clinical Animal Behaviourist)

Avoid anyone who talks about "dominance," uses prong collars, e-collars, or punishment-based methods. The evidence base firmly supports reward-based methods for reducing fear and anxiety-related behaviour.

**A vet referral is often required** before seeing a behaviourist — your vet will also rule out pain or medical causes for behavioural changes.

## FAQs

**Q: My dog wags their tail but snaps at children — why?**
A: As explained above, tail wagging alone doesn't indicate happiness. High, stiff wagging with a tense body and direct stare is a warning sign, not a friendly signal. Watch for the full picture — and never leave children unsupervised with any dog.

**Q: My dog shows calming signals during training. Does that mean training is stressful?**
A: Some lip licking or yawning during training is normal — it's often frustration or the dog working through uncertainty. If it's constant or accompanied by other stress signals, reduce the difficulty level, shorten sessions, and increase reinforcement rate. Training should feel easy most of the time.

**Q: Is my dog showing guilt when he puts his tail down after doing something wrong?**
A: No. Research by Dr Alexandra Horowitz (2009) showed that the "guilty look" is a response to *your* body language and tone — not to what the dog did. Dogs that had done nothing wrong showed the same "guilty" posture when their owner acted angry. What you're reading is appeasement — your dog sensing you're upset and trying to reduce conflict.

**Q: My dog growls when I touch their food bowl. Is this aggression?**
A: It's resource guarding — a normal instinct, but one that needs management. Never punish growling (see above). Speak to your vet or a qualified behaviourist. A simple starting approach: teach "trade" — offer a high-value treat as you approach, so the approach predicts good things, not removal.

**Q: How do I know if my dogs are playing or fighting?**
A: See Part 7. The key markers are role reversal, voluntary returns, bouncy/exaggerated movement, and the play bow. If one dog is trying to leave and being prevented, it's no longer play.

**Q: My dog seems happy at home but nervous in public. Is this normal?**
A: Yes — many dogs are well-adjusted at home but find the outside world overwhelming. This is often undersocialisation during the critical period (3–12 weeks). A qualified behaviourist can help with a structured desensitisation programme.

## Summary

Reading your dog is a learnable skill — and it changes your relationship profoundly. When you can spot a lip lick at the vet, a whale eye during a greeting, or a calming signal on a walk, you stop reacting to what your dog does and start *responding* to what they need.

The principles:
- **Read the whole dog**, not one signal
- **Breed baseline matters** — always assess relative to their normal
- **Calming signals are communication** — respect them
- **Stress signals are often involuntary** — if you see them regularly, something needs to change
- **Never punish growling** — it's a warning, not a crime
- **Get professional help early** — behavioural problems get harder to address over time

The more fluent you become in dog body language, the better the life you can build for your dog — and the stronger the bond between you.
