*Spring is here — and if your dog is leaving tufts of fur on every surface in your home, you're not alone. As the days lengthen and temperatures rise, most dogs "blow" their winter coat in a dramatic explosion of shedding. It's the perfect time to get on top of your dog's grooming routine — and this guide will show you exactly how.*

Whether you've got a slobbery Labrador, a fluffy Poodle mix, or a wire-haired Border Terrier, grooming your dog at home is one of the best habits you can build. It saves money, deepens your bond, and — perhaps most importantly — lets you spot health issues before they become expensive vet bills.

This is your complete UK dog grooming guide for 2026: practical, breed-specific, and written for real dog owners, not professionals.

## Why Groom Your Dog at Home?

The average full groom at a professional salon in the UK costs £30–£80. For many breeds, that's every 6–8 weeks. Over a year, that's up to £600 or more — before you've even bought food or toys.

But home grooming isn't just about money. According to the [PDSA](https://www.pdsa.org.uk/), regular grooming is a vital part of responsible pet ownership. It:

- **Removes dead hair and prevents matting** — mats pull on skin and can cause serious discomfort
- **Keeps skin and coat healthy** — brushing distributes natural oils and improves circulation
- **Reduces shedding around the house** — especially important during spring coat-blowing season
- **Helps you spot problems early** — lumps, skin conditions, parasites, ear infections, overgrown nails
- **Builds trust and calm handling** — a dog used to being touched is easier for vets to examine

The [Blue Cross](https://www.bluecross.org.uk/) notes that dogs who are regularly groomed from puppyhood tend to find veterinary check-ups far less stressful. That's a win for everyone.

## How Often Should You Groom Your Dog?

Grooming frequency depends almost entirely on your dog's coat type. Here's the definitive breakdown:

### 🐶 Short-Haired Breeds (Labs, Staffies, Bull Terriers, Boxers, Beagles)

**At home:** Weekly brush
**Professional grooming:** Every 8–12 weeks (bath, nail trim, ears)

Short-haired dogs are the lowest maintenance on the grooming front — but don't skip it. A weekly once-over with a rubber curry brush or bristle brush removes dead hair before it ends up on your sofa, and gives you a chance to check for any skin changes or lumps.

> **💡 Breed Spotlight — Labrador Retriever**
> Don't be fooled — Labs have a dense double coat and shed *heavily*, especially in spring. During shedding season, upgrade from once a week to daily brushing with a deshedding tool. You'll be amazed how much comes out.

---

### 🐶 Medium-Haired Breeds (German Shepherds, Cocker Spaniels, Golden Retrievers, Border Collies)

**At home:** 2–3 times per week
**Professional grooming:** Every 6–8 weeks

Medium-coated breeds typically have a double coat with a dense undercoat. Without regular brushing, they tangle easily — particularly around the ears, collar area, and behind the legs. Spaniels in particular need special attention to their silky ears and feathering.

> **💡 Breed Spotlight — German Shepherd**
> GSDs are often called "German Shedders" for good reason. They have a thick double coat that blows twice a year. Invest in a quality undercoat rake and use it religiously during spring. The [Kennel Club](https://www.thekennelclub.org.uk/) recommends daily brushing during heavy shedding periods.

---

### 🐶 Long-Haired Breeds (Poodle Mixes, Lhasa Apso, Shih Tzu, Yorkshire Terrier, Afghan Hound)

**At home:** Daily brushing (non-negotiable)
**Professional grooming:** Every 4–6 weeks

Long-haired dogs require the most commitment. Without daily brushing, their coats mat quickly — and mats are more than unsightly. They trap moisture and bacteria against the skin, restrict blood flow, and can become genuinely painful. Once a mat becomes severe, the only humane option is often to clip the coat short, which is distressing for many owners.

> **💡 Breed Spotlight — Cockapoo/Cavapoo/Labradoodle**
> The Poodle cross explosion in the UK has brought a grooming challenge with it. These dogs often inherit the Poodle's curly or wavy coat — which doesn't shed much (great!) but mats incredibly easily (not great). Daily brushing, monthly baths, and professional grooming every 4–6 weeks is the minimum. Don't let the "low shedding" claim fool you into thinking they're low maintenance.

---

### 🐶 Wire-Haired Breeds (Border Terriers, West Highland White Terriers, Schnauzers, Wire Fox Terriers)

**At home:** 2x weekly
**Professional grooming / hand-stripping:** 2–3 times per year

Wire-haired breeds have a unique double coat with a harsh outer wire layer and a soft undercoat. This coat is designed to be "hand-stripped" — a technique where the dead outer coat is pulled out by hand to allow new growth — rather than clipped. Clipping a wire coat softens it, loses the colour, and can change the texture permanently.

Many owners opt for a specialist groomer who understands hand-stripping for these breeds. The [Animal Trust](https://www.animaltrust.org.uk/) recommends finding a groomer experienced with your specific breed type.

## Step-by-Step Home Grooming Guide

### Before You Start: Setting Your Dog Up for Success

Grooming should always be a positive experience. If your dog associates the brush with stress, every session becomes a battle.

**Tips for building positive associations:**
- Start when they're calm and slightly tired (post-walk is ideal)
- Keep sessions short initially — 5–10 minutes is enough for puppies or nervous dogs
- Use high-value treats liberally. Lick mats with peanut butter (xylitol-free) are excellent for keeping dogs distracted during grooming
- Never restrain forcefully. If your dog is struggling, stop, calm them down, and try again
- Always end on a positive note — play, a treat, or praise

If your dog has never been groomed before (or has had a bad experience), work up gradually. Touch their paws, ears, and mouth daily — before you even introduce tools — so they learn that handling is normal and pleasant.

---

### Brushing Technique by Coat Type

**Short coats:** Work in the direction of hair growth, covering the whole body. A 5-minute weekly brush is all most short-coated dogs need.

**Medium coats:** Brush in sections. Lift the top coat and work the brush through from root to tip, tackling one area at a time. Pay special attention to the neck, behind the ears, armpits, and groin — all prime areas for matting.

**Long coats:** Brush from the skin outward — not just the surface. Many owners make the mistake of brushing only the top layer, leaving tangles underneath. Use a pin brush or slicker and work through small sections methodically. Finish with a steel comb to check no tangles remain.

**Wire coats:** Brush in the direction of hair growth with a slicker. For hand-stripping, either learn the technique from a breed specialist or have it done by a professional.

**🚫 If you find a mat:** Never try to brush through a solid mat — it's extremely painful. Instead, try to work the mat apart with your fingers first, then use a detangler spray and a wide-toothed comb to tease it apart from the outside in. If the mat is too severe to detangle without causing pain, take your dog to a groomer.

---

### Bathing Your Dog at Home

**How often:** Most dogs need a bath every 4–6 weeks. Over-bathing strips natural oils from the skin and coat. Some dogs with skin conditions may need medicated baths more frequently on vet advice.

**Water temperature:** Lukewarm — similar to a comfortable baby bath. Never hot. Many dogs are nervous about baths, and cold water makes this worse.

**The process:**
1. Brush thoroughly *before* bathing — wet hair tangles and mats even more easily
2. Wet the coat completely, working from the neck back (avoid getting water in ears and eyes)
3. Apply shampoo and lather well, massaging into the skin
4. Rinse thoroughly — leftover shampoo residue causes itching
5. Apply conditioner if needed (long-coated or dry-coated dogs benefit from this)
6. Rinse again completely

**Drying:** Towel dry first, absorbing as much water as possible. For long-haired dogs, a pet dryer (or a human hair dryer on its lowest, coolest setting) is advisable — allowing them to air dry while still damp can cause matting and, in cold weather, can chill your dog. Keep the dryer moving and at a safe distance from the skin.

### Nail Clipping: A Step-by-Step Approach

Many owners are scared of clipping nails, and understandably so — the "quick" (the blood vessel inside the nail) can bleed if cut. But with the right technique, it's straightforward.

**Identifying the quick:**
- On white/clear nails: You can see a pink area — that's the quick. Cut just below it.
- On black nails: Clip a little at a time. When you see a white or grey circle in the cross-section, you're close — stop there.

**The process:**
1. Pick up one paw gently and hold it steady
2. Clip just the tip of each nail — small cuts are always safer
3. Do a little at a time over several sessions if your dog is nervous
4. Have styptic powder (available from pet shops) on hand in case you catch the quick — it stops bleeding quickly
5. Reward after each paw — or even after each nail if your dog is particularly anxious

**How often:** Every 3–4 weeks for most dogs. Dogs who walk regularly on pavements will naturally wear their nails down and may need less frequent clipping.

---

### Ear Cleaning

Check ears weekly. Healthy ears should be pale pink inside, not red, and shouldn't smell. A little light wax is normal.

**To clean:**
1. Squeeze a few drops of ear cleaner into the ear canal
2. Gently massage the base of the ear for 20–30 seconds — you'll hear a squelching sound
3. Let your dog shake their head (have a towel ready!)
4. Wipe away any debris from the outer ear with a cotton pad

**⚠️ Never use cotton buds in the ear canal.** If ears are very dirty, red, smelly, or your dog is pawing at them, see a vet — don't try to clean further at home.

---

### Teeth Cleaning

Dental disease affects the majority of dogs over three years old, according to the PDSA. Brushing your dog's teeth 3–4 times per week significantly reduces this risk.

- Use a dog-specific toothpaste (human toothpaste contains xylitol, which is toxic to dogs)
- Start with your finger, building up to a finger brush, then a toothbrush
- Focus on the outer surfaces — where plaque builds up most

Dental chews, water additives, and certain toys can supplement brushing but shouldn't replace it.

---

### The Eye Area

Check eyes daily for discharge. A little clear or slightly brownish discharge in the corner is normal. Clean it away gently with a damp cotton pad, wiping from the inner corner outward.

**Watch for:** Yellow or green discharge, excessive tear staining, redness, squinting or pawing at the eye. All of these need a vet check.

Long-haired breeds around the face (Shih Tzu, Lhasa Apso, Maltese) may need the hair around their eyes trimmed regularly to prevent irritation. This is fiddly work — if you're unsure, ask your groomer to do it.

## Spring Shedding Season: Extra Care Guide

### Why Does Spring Shedding Happen?

Twice a year — in spring and autumn — dogs "blow" their coat. The process is triggered by changing daylight hours (not temperature, as is commonly thought), signalling to your dog's body to swap its winter undercoat for a lighter summer one.

The result? *Tufts of undercoat appearing across your home in quantities that seem physically impossible given your dog's size.*

The spring shed is typically the more dramatic of the two, as dogs lose the thick, insulating undercoat they built up over winter. For double-coated breeds, this can last 2–4 weeks.

---

### Which Breeds Shed the Most?

Double-coated breeds are the biggest shedders in spring:

| Breed | Shedding Level | Notes |
|-------|---------------|-------|
| Siberian Husky | 🔴 Extreme | Can be dramatic for 3–4 weeks |
| German Shepherd | 🔴 Extreme | Year-round shedders, worse in spring |
| Labrador Retriever | 🟠 Heavy | Dense undercoat, often surprises owners |
| Golden Retriever | 🟠 Heavy | Long hair means more visible on carpets |
| Border Collie | 🟠 Heavy | Needs daily attention during peak shed |
| Pomeranian | 🔴 Extreme | Small dog, huge shedder |
| Corgi (Pembroke/Cardigan) | 🟠 Heavy | Very dense double coat for their size |

---

### Managing Spring Shedding

**Daily brushing during peak shedding:** Even if your dog normally only needs 2–3 brushes per week, step this up to daily during the coat blow. Focus on the FURminator or undercoat rake rather than just the surface slicker brush.

**Groom outside when possible:** On a dry day, take your grooming session into the garden. You'll see just how much undercoat is coming out — and it won't be on your carpet. Birds often appreciate the loose fur for nesting material.

**Managing hair in the home:**
- Use a rubber-backed doormat at key entrances to trap loose hair
- A damp rubber glove or rubber squeegee works brilliantly on upholstered furniture
- Vacuum more frequently with a pet-specific attachment (most modern pet-friendly Dysons and Sharks handle this well)
- Consider a robot hoover to run daily during peak shed season
- Wash bedding and soft furnishings more frequently

**Diet matters:** A coat in good condition sheds more cleanly and is easier to manage. Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) are widely recommended by vets to support skin and coat health during shedding season.

**What about coat-blowing supplements?** Some owners swear by supplements containing biotin, zinc, and omega-3s during spring. Speak to your vet before adding any supplements to your dog's diet.

## Signs Your Dog Needs Professional Grooming

Home grooming has its limits. Here's when it's time to call in the professionals:

### Severe Matting
If you can't detangle mats with your fingers and a detangling spray, stop. Forcing through severe mats is painful for your dog and can damage the skin. A professional groomer can either carefully remove the mat or, if necessary, clip the coat short and start fresh.

### Skin Issues
If you notice redness, sores, scabs, unusual dandruff, a rash, or bald patches, visit your vet before attempting to groom. Some skin conditions are painful, and grooming over them can make things worse.

### Nail Overgrowth
If your dog's nails have grown so long they're curling, or the quick has grown so far down that you can't safely clip to a normal length, a vet or groomer can help. In extreme cases, nails may need to be professionally cut under sedation.

### Anal Glands
Anal gland expression is a common service offered by groomers and vets. If your dog is scooting their bottom along the floor, licking excessively at their rear end, or has a fishy smell, their anal glands may need expressing. This is not a home grooming job — it requires training to do safely.

### Gland and parasite checks
During your home grooming sessions, get in the habit of checking for fleas (look for "flea dirt" — tiny black specks — in the coat, especially around the base of the tail and belly), ticks (run your hands through the coat after walks in woodland or long grass), and lumps or bumps that warrant a vet check.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: How do I get my dog to enjoy being groomed?

Start slowly and make it rewarding. Begin by just touching your dog with the brush for a few seconds, treating immediately. Gradually increase the duration. Never push past the point of clear stress — always end on a positive moment. For very nervous dogs, ask your vet about whether a calming supplement might help during grooming sessions.

---

### Q: Can I use baby shampoo on my dog if I've run out of dog shampoo?

Baby shampoo is much gentler than adult human shampoo, but it's still formulated for human skin pH. In an emergency, a small amount of baby shampoo won't cause lasting harm — but make it a one-off. Dog-specific shampoos are inexpensive and much better for your dog's skin in the long run.

---

### Q: My dog's coat is dull and dry — what am I doing wrong?

A few possible causes: over-bathing (stripping natural oils), using the wrong shampoo, not rinsing thoroughly (shampoo residue causes dryness), or diet (look at whether your dog is getting adequate omega-3 fatty acids). If the issue persists, a vet can check for underlying skin conditions or nutritional deficiencies.

---

### Q: How do I know if I've cut the quick?

You'll know immediately — the nail will bleed and your dog will likely yelp and pull away. Keep styptic powder (or if you don't have it, cornflour) handy. Press it firmly onto the bleeding nail for 30 seconds. The bleeding should stop quickly. Give your dog lots of reassurance and take a break from nail clipping for the day.

---

### Q: My dog hates having their paws touched — how do I get them used to it?

Paw sensitivity is very common and needs to be worked on gradually. Start by just picking up the paw for a second, treating, and releasing. Progress to holding it for longer, then introducing the clippers (turned off, just placed near the paw). Only when your dog is calm with each step do you move to the next. This can take weeks, and that's completely fine.

---

### Q: How often should I groom a puppy?

Start immediately — even if there's nothing to actually groom yet. The goal at puppy stage is familiarisation, not cleaning. Brush gently, handle their paws, ears, and mouth, and make every experience positive. A puppy who grows up comfortable with grooming is an adult dog who is easy to groom, easy for vets to examine, and easier to handle in general. This is one of the highest-value habits you can build in those early weeks.

## Final Thoughts

Dog grooming doesn't need to be a chore — or an expensive trip to the salon every few weeks. With the right tools, a bit of technique, and a positive approach, most of your dog's grooming needs can be met at home.

Spring is the best time to get into a routine, because that's when your dog needs you most. Whether you're dealing with the great coat blow of a Husky, keeping a Doodle's curly coat tangle-free, or just giving your Staffie a weekly once-over, the investment of time is always worth it.

Your dog will look better, feel better — and you'll catch problems early, saving yourself expensive vet bills down the line.
