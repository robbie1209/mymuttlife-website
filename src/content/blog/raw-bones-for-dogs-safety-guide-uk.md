## Introduction

Raw bones. Few topics in dog nutrition generate more passionate debate — and more genuine confusion — than this one.

Ask your vet and they might wince. Ask a raw feeding enthusiast and they'll tell you bones are nature's toothbrush and an essential part of a dog's diet. Ask online and you'll get a thousand conflicting opinions before lunch.

The truth, as with most things in dog health, is more nuanced than either camp admits.

Raw bones *can* be a safe, beneficial addition to your dog's life. They can also cause serious injury if you get it wrong. This guide gives you the complete, honest picture — what's safe, what isn't, what the science says, and how to feed bones responsibly if you choose to.

> **Quick disclaimer:** Always consult your vet before making significant changes to your dog's diet, especially if they have existing dental or digestive conditions.

---

## Raw Bones vs Cooked Bones: The Most Important Rule

Let's start with the single most important fact about feeding dogs bones:

**Never feed cooked bones. Ever.**

This isn't a precaution — it's a hard rule backed by every major veterinary body in the UK, including the British Veterinary Association (BVA) and the British Small Animal Veterinary Association (BSAVA).

Here's why cooking matters:

| Bone state | What happens | Risk |
|------------|--------------|------|
| **Raw** | Bone is pliable, bends under pressure | Lower fracture and splinter risk (though not zero) |
| **Cooked** | Collagen structure breaks down, bone becomes brittle | Splinters into sharp shards that can perforate the gut |
| **Smoked/dried** | Similar to cooked — dehydration makes them brittle | High risk — avoid |
| **Boiled** | Worst of all — all moisture and pliability removed | Extreme splintering risk |

Cooked chicken bones, pork bones, lamb chops — these are the bones that end up in emergency vets on Christmas and Easter. The horror stories you've heard about dogs and bones almost always involve cooked bones, not raw ones.

If you take nothing else from this guide: **raw only**.

## What Are the Benefits of Raw Bones for Dogs?

When fed correctly, raw bones offer several genuine benefits:

### 1. Dental Health
Chewing raw meaty bones is one of the most effective ways to reduce plaque and tartar buildup. The mechanical scraping action — combined with enzymes naturally present in raw meat — breaks down dental deposits that even daily brushing can miss.

A 2009 study published in the *Journal of Veterinary Dentistry* (Knaus et al.) found that dogs fed raw meaty bones had significantly lower calculus (tartar) scores than those fed exclusively on commercial dry food.

The PDSA PAW Report 2024 found that 4 in 10 dogs show signs of dental disease — making this far from a trivial concern.

### 2. Mental Stimulation and Enrichment
Chewing is a deeply satisfying behaviour for dogs. It releases endorphins, reduces stress hormones, and provides prolonged focus that depletes mental energy. A raw bone on a Sunday afternoon is the canine equivalent of a good book.

For dogs prone to anxiety, boredom destruction, or excessive barking, structured chewing time can be genuinely therapeutic.

### 3. Nutritional Value
Raw bones — particularly meaty bones with soft tissue attached — provide calcium, phosphorus, and trace minerals in bioavailable form. Raw bone marrow is rich in fat-soluble vitamins and healthy fats.

Important caveat: bones should never be the *primary* source of these nutrients. A complete, balanced diet comes first — bones are a supplement, not a replacement.

### 4. Natural Jaw Exercise
The jaw is a muscle group that benefits from appropriate resistance work. Regular chewing builds jaw strength and satisfies natural predatory behaviours, particularly in working breeds and terriers.

## The Risks: What Can Actually Go Wrong

Honesty matters here. Raw bones are not risk-free. Understanding the risks is what separates responsible feeding from dangerous feeding.

### 1. Choking and Blockages
The most serious risk. Pieces of bone that are swallowed whole — or bones that splinter into sharp fragments — can cause intestinal obstruction. This is a veterinary emergency requiring surgery, and in severe cases, can be fatal.

**Most at risk:** Aggressive chewers, food-motivated breeds (Labradors, Beagles, Spaniels), dogs who have previously swallowed objects.

### 2. Fractured Teeth
Bones that are too hard can fracture teeth — particularly the carnassial (shearing) teeth, which are expensive and painful to treat. 

A vet dental procedure on a fractured tooth can cost **£400–£1,200** in the UK, depending on the treatment required.

**Most at risk:** Weight-bearing bones (femur, knuckle joints from large animals), dried/smoked bones presented as "natural chews."

### 3. Digestive Issues
Rich marrow, fat, and bone meal can cause gastrointestinal upset — loose stools, vomiting, pancreatitis in susceptible dogs. Dogs not accustomed to raw bones should be introduced very gradually.

**Most at risk:** Dogs with sensitive stomachs, pancreatitis history, or dogs transitioning from kibble for the first time.

### 4. Bacterial Contamination
Raw meat carries bacteria including *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, and *E. coli*. Most healthy adult dogs handle this well — their gut pH and digestive speed is adapted for raw protein. However:

- Dogs with weakened immune systems are at higher risk
- Bacteria shed in faeces can pose a risk to humans in the household
- Children, elderly people, and immunocompromised adults should not handle raw bones without careful hygiene

The UK Food Standards Agency advises thorough handwashing after handling raw meat, including raw bones for pets.

### 5. Constipation
Too much bone — particularly edible bone rather than recreational bone — can cause chalk-white, crumbly, difficult-to-pass stools. This is the most common complaint from new raw feeders.

## Types of Raw Bones: A Practical Guide

Not all bones are created equal. The key distinction is between **recreational bones** (for chewing, not eating) and **edible bones** (softer, meant to be consumed as part of a raw diet).

### Recreational Bones (Chew, Don't Eat)

These are large, dense bones that your dog will gnaw on but ideally shouldn't consume. Think of them as a long-lasting chew rather than a food.

| Bone | Size guidance | Notes |
|------|--------------|-------|
| **Beef marrow bone (cut/cross-section)** | Large dogs | The classic. Excellent for enrichment. Remove when gnawed down to a nub to prevent tooth fracture on the hard ends. |
| **Beef knuckle bone** | Medium–large dogs | Very hard — some vets caution against these as they can fracture teeth. Monitor closely. |
| **Lamb leg bone** | Medium dogs | Softer than beef. Good choice for medium breeds. Supervise carefully as smaller dogs may break off larger pieces. |
| **Bison/buffalo rib** | Large dogs | Less common but excellent option. Durable but not as tooth-threatening as dense knuckle bones. |

### Edible Bones (Part of a Raw Diet)

These are softer bones that dogs can and should consume. They're an important part of balanced raw feeding, providing calcium and phosphorus alongside meat and offal.

| Bone | Dog size | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| **Raw chicken wings** | Small–medium | Classic edible bone. Raw only — see above. Excellent for dental health. |
| **Raw chicken necks** | Small–medium | Softer than wings. Often recommended as a starter edible bone for dogs new to raw feeding. |
| **Raw duck necks** | Medium–large | Similar to chicken neck but larger. Good calcium source. |
| **Raw turkey necks** | Large dogs | Meaty and satisfying. Excellent for large breeds who need more calcium in their raw diet. |
| **Raw lamb ribs** | Small–large (size-matched) | Soft enough to eat but meaty enough to be rewarding. Good starter bone for new raw feeders. |
| **Raw rabbit** | Small–large | Whole rabbit or rabbit pieces are one of the most complete edible bone sources — soft bones throughout. |
| **Raw salmon/fish frames** | All sizes | Fish frames (carcasses after filleting) are excellent and provide omega-3s alongside calcium. |

### Bones to Avoid Entirely

These bones carry high risk regardless of your dog's size or experience with raw feeding:

- ❌ **Any cooked bone** — all types, without exception
- ❌ **Pork bones** — prone to splintering even when raw
- ❌ **Rib bones from beef** (small cross-sections) — can become rigid and splinter
- ❌ **T-bone or steak bones** — the T-shaped centre can cause palate or throat injury
- ❌ **Weight-bearing leg bones from large animals** — dense enough to fracture carnassial teeth
- ❌ **Dried, smoked, or processed "natural chews" sold as bones** — despite being marketed as safer, the drying process makes them brittle. Bully sticks are the safer alternative in this category.

## Breed-Specific Considerations

### Large & Working Breeds (Labradors, German Shepherds, Rottweilers, Huskies)
Generally well-suited to raw bones. Their jaw strength means they'll consume edible bones efficiently. The risk is their tendency to eat too fast — consider large recreational bones that they can't swallow whole, and always size up.

### Medium Breeds (Spaniels, Collies, Setters, Staffies)
Raw feeding community's sweet spot. Chicken necks, duck necks, and lamb ribs are ideal edible bones. Staffies have particularly strong jaws for their size — choose accordingly.

### Small Breeds (Jack Russells, Chihuahuas, Shih Tzus, Miniature Schnauzers)
Raw chicken wings and rabbit pieces are the safest options. Avoid anything large enough to risk impaction if swallowed. Small dogs can benefit enormously from raw bone dental action — the dental disease burden in small breeds is disproportionately high (PDSA, 2024).

### Brachycephalic Breeds (French Bulldogs, Pugs, Bulldogs, Shih Tzus)
Exercise particular caution. Brachycephalic dogs have shorter jaws, misaligned teeth, and often different swallowing mechanics. They're at higher risk of choking. If you choose to feed raw bones, consult your vet first and supervise all sessions without exception.

### Puppies (Under 6 Months)
Many raw feeding vets advise starting edible bones from 8–10 weeks as part of a raw diet — soft chicken necks or wings work well. However, puppy bones are developing, and getting the calcium:phosphorus ratio right matters more at this stage. Avoid hard recreational bones entirely until adulthood.

### Senior Dogs
Dental disease is extremely common in senior dogs — raw bones can genuinely help. But ageing dogs often have worn teeth that are more prone to fracture. Opt for softer edible bones (chicken, rabbit, duck neck) and avoid hard recreational bones.

### Dogs with Medical Conditions
- **Pancreatitis history:** Marrow bones are very high in fat. Avoid or remove marrow before giving.
- **Dental disease/broken teeth:** Consult your vet before introducing any bones.
- **Kidney disease:** Calcium levels matter. Discuss with your vet before adding bone to the diet.
- **Immunocompromised dogs:** The bacterial risk is higher. Cooked (yes, cooked) bones remain too dangerous — this is one situation where appropriate dental chews may be preferable to raw bones entirely.

## How to Feed Raw Bones Safely: The 7 Rules

### Rule 1: Always Supervise
Never leave a dog alone with a bone. This is the single most important rule. Supervision allows you to intervene if a large piece breaks off, if your dog begins to choke, or if they lose interest and try to bury it somewhere it will become a bacterial hazard.

### Rule 2: Size Matters — Bigger Than Their Head
The bone should always be larger than your dog's head. This sounds dramatic, but it's a practical rule: if a dog can fit the entire bone in their mouth, they can potentially swallow it. A bone they have to work against is a safe bone.

### Rule 3: Fresh Only — Time Limit of 20 Minutes
Raw bones should be fresh. Don't allow your dog to chew a bone for hours, return to it across multiple sessions, or bury it and dig it up days later. Bacteria multiply rapidly in ambient temperatures. A 15–20 minute session is plenty, then remove and refrigerate (use within 24 hours) or discard.

### Rule 4: Feed After a Meal, Not on an Empty Stomach
A dog fed raw bones on an empty stomach may eat too fast and too aggressively. Feeding after a meal reduces the feeding frenzy instinct and promotes calmer, more controlled chewing.

### Rule 5: Introduce Gradually
If your dog has never had raw bones, start with something soft — a raw chicken wing or neck — and observe for the first few sessions. Some dogs, particularly those transitioning from kibble, may experience loose stools while their gut adjusts. Go slowly.

### Rule 6: Give One at a Time, Separate Multiple Dogs
Resource guarding is real, and bones are a prime trigger. If you have multiple dogs, feed raw bones in separate spaces where dogs cannot see or smell each other's bone. Never allow dogs to compete over bones.

### Rule 7: Know the Warning Signs
Seek emergency veterinary care if you see any of the following after feeding a bone:

- Retching, gagging, or attempting to vomit without success
- Lethargy, reluctance to move
- Swollen or painful abdomen
- Signs of distress: pacing, whining, unable to settle
- Blood in stool
- Constipation lasting more than 24 hours after bone feeding
- Broken tooth (visible fracture or sudden reluctance to eat)

## What the Vets Say: The Official UK Position

It's fair to say the UK veterinary establishment is cautious about raw bones, but not uniformly opposed to them.

**The British Veterinary Association (BVA)** advises against feeding raw bones due to the risks of bacterial contamination and physical injury. Their guidance prioritises public health alongside animal health.

**The Raw Feeding Veterinary Society (RFVS)**, a UK-based group of vets who support raw feeding, takes a different view — arguing that bones are an integral part of canine evolutionary nutrition and that the risks are manageable with proper education.

**The Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons (RCVS)** does not take a blanket position on raw feeding but emphasises that vets should advise clients based on individual animal circumstances.

**The PDSA** recommends dental chews over raw bones as a safer alternative for dental health.

The reality: most vets fall somewhere in the middle. Many will acknowledge that a large, raw, meaty bone fed under supervision is unlikely to cause harm to a healthy adult dog — while remaining cautious about recommending it publicly due to the risks in less controlled situations.

**Our recommendation:** Have an honest conversation with your own vet about your dog specifically. A vet who knows your dog's health history, jaw strength, and temperament is better placed to advise you than any general guide.

## Raw Bones vs Dental Chews: Which Is Better?

This comes up constantly in online forums. Here's a practical comparison:

| Factor | Raw meaty bones | Dental chews (e.g., Whimzees, Lily's Kitchen) |
|--------|----------------|------|
| **Dental effectiveness** | Very high (mechanical + enzymatic) | Moderate (mechanical only) |
| **Safety** | Moderate if done right | Higher — designed to dissolve |
| **Cost** | Low (chicken necks from butchers ~£1–2) | Moderate (£0.50–£1.50 per chew) |
| **Supervision required** | Yes, always | Recommended but lower risk |
| **Vet-recommended** | Depends on the vet | Generally yes |
| **Enrichment value** | Very high | Moderate |
| **Bacterial risk** | Present (manageable) | Minimal |
| **VOHC approved** | No (UK doesn't have raw bone VOHC trials) | Some products are |

**Verdict:** Dental chews are safer and easier. Raw bones are more enriching and potentially more effective for dental health. For most dog owners, a combination works well — dental chews for day-to-day maintenance, an occasional supervised raw bone for enrichment and deeper dental benefit.

Your local butcher is the best source. Ask for raw chicken carcasses, chicken wings, lamb ribs, or marrow bones. Make sure they know it's for a dog — you want fresh, human-food-grade product without seasoning or marinades.

## Hygiene: Keeping Your Household Safe

Raw meat hygiene matters — not just for your dog, but for you and your family.

**After handling raw bones:**
- Wash hands thoroughly with soap and warm water for at least 20 seconds
- Use a separate dedicated board or tile if you handle the bone (don't use your food prep surfaces)
- Clean and disinfect the area where your dog chewed the bone

**Storing raw bones:**
- Keep refrigerated (below 5°C) and use within 24 hours
- Freeze if not using within 24 hours — frozen bones keep for up to 3 months

**If you have young children, elderly relatives, or immunocompromised people in the household:**
- Extra caution is warranted — consider whether raw bones are appropriate for your household situation
- The FSA advises that raw pet food (including bones) poses a small but real bacterial risk to vulnerable humans

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can dogs eat chicken bones raw?
Yes — raw chicken wings and necks are among the most commonly recommended edible bones for dogs, and have been fed to dogs safely for generations. The danger is cooked chicken bones, which splinter. Raw chicken bones are softer and more pliable. That said, supervise all bone feeding and size-match to your dog.

### Are raw bones safe for puppies?
Soft edible bones like raw chicken wings and necks are widely used in puppy raw feeding from around 8–10 weeks. Avoid hard recreational bones (marrow, knuckle) until adulthood. Consult your vet or a raw feeding nutritionist if you're introducing bones to a puppy diet.

### How often should I give my dog a raw bone?
Most raw feeders offer edible bones 2–3 times per week as part of a complete raw diet. Recreational chewing bones (marrow, large recreational cuts) are typically given once a week or less. Too much bone leads to calcium excess and constipation.

### My dog ate a bone and seems fine — do I need to call the vet?
If your dog ate a raw bone (or a piece of one) and is behaving normally — eating, drinking, active — monitor closely for 24–48 hours. Look for the warning signs listed above (gagging, distress, bloating, constipation). If any appear, call your vet immediately. If your dog consumed a *cooked* bone, call your vet the same day, even without symptoms.

### Can dogs eat pork bones?
Raw pork bones are generally not recommended. Pork bones tend to splinter even when raw, and carry a higher risk of *Trichinella* (a parasitic worm) compared to chicken and lamb. Most raw feeding guides suggest avoiding pork bones specifically, even in raw form.

### Are smoked or dried bones safe?
No. Despite being sold as "natural dog chews," smoked and dried bones are brittle for the same reason cooked bones are — the cooking/drying process destroys the collagen that makes raw bone pliable. These are responsible for a large number of veterinary emergencies involving what pet owners thought were safe products. If you want a long-lasting chew with a similar profile, choose a quality bully stick instead.

## Final Thoughts

Raw bones occupy a frustrating middle ground in dog nutrition: genuinely beneficial if done right, genuinely dangerous if done wrong. This guide exists because the answer isn't "never" and it isn't "always" — it's "here's how to do it safely."

The dogs most likely to benefit from raw bones are healthy adult dogs whose owners have taken the time to understand the risks, choose the right type, supervise sessions, and introduce them gradually. The dogs most likely to end up at the emergency vet are those given cooked bones, left unsupervised, or given something too small to be safe.

Whichever route you take — raw bones, dental chews, or a combination — your dog's dental health is worth paying attention to. An estimated 80% of dogs over the age of three show signs of dental disease (PDSA PAW Report). That's a problem worth solving.
