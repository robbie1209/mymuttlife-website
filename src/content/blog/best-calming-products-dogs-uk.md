## Which Type of Calming Product is Right for Your Dog?

With so many options, choosing can feel overwhelming. Here's a quick guide based on your dog's situation:

### Pheromone Products (Adaptil Diffuser, Adaptil Collar)
**Choose if:** Your dog has general anxiety at home, separation anxiety, or is settling into a new environment. Pheromones are backed by the most clinical research and are completely odourless.

### Daily Supplements (YuCalm, Zylkene)
**Choose if:** Your dog has ongoing, chronic anxiety and you want to build long-term resilience. These work best given consistently over several weeks. Think of them as the "foundation" of a calming routine.

### Fast-Acting Supplements (NutraCalm)
**Choose if:** You need targeted relief for specific events ‚Äî vet visits, car journeys, visitors. Give them 1‚Äì2 hours before the stressful event.

### Herbal Remedies (Dorwest, Bach Rescue Remedy)
**Choose if:** You prefer a natural/herbal approach. Dorwest is particularly effective for noise phobias and has VMD licensing. Bach Rescue Remedy suits very mild stress only.

### Sprays & Diffusers (Pet Remedy)
**Choose if:** You want room-wide calming or a quick spritz for situational stress. Great for multi-pet homes and very easy to use.

### Pressure Wraps (ThunderShirt)
**Choose if:** Your dog has noise phobias (fireworks, thunder) or situational anxiety. Drug-free, works immediately, and it's a one-off purchase.

### Combining Products

Many vets and behaviourists recommend combining different *types* of calming product for the best results. For example:

- **Adaptil Diffuser** (background calm at home) + **YuCalm** (daily supplement for long-term resilience) + **ThunderShirt** (for fireworks/storms)
- **Pet Remedy Plug-In** (room diffuser) + **NutraCalm** (before vet visits)

Always introduce one product at a time so you can gauge what's working.

## Common Signs of Anxiety in Dogs

Not sure if your dog is anxious? Look for these signs:

- **Excessive panting or drooling** when there's no obvious cause
- **Pacing and restlessness** ‚Äî can't seem to settle
- **Destructive behaviour** ‚Äî chewing furniture, scratching doors (especially when left alone)
- **Excessive barking or whining**
- **Trembling or shaking** during storms, fireworks, or when visitors arrive
- **Hiding or seeking constant reassurance**
- **Loss of appetite** during stressful periods
- **Toileting indoors** despite being house-trained
- **Lip licking, yawning, or whale eye** (showing the whites of their eyes)
- **Tail tucked between legs** and low body posture

Some of these signs are subtle and easy to miss. If you're seeing several of them regularly, a calming product can help ‚Äî but it's worth understanding the root cause too.

Regular [exercise](/blog/how-much-exercise-does-my-dog-need) plays a huge role in managing anxiety. A well-exercised dog is generally a calmer dog. [Older dogs](/blog/caring-for-senior-dogs-guide) can also develop anxiety as part of cognitive decline, which needs a slightly different approach.

## When to See a Vet

Calming products are brilliant tools, but they're not a replacement for veterinary advice. **Book a vet appointment if:**

- Your dog's anxiety is **severe** ‚Äî causing self-harm, extreme destruction, or complete refusal to eat
- Symptoms have **suddenly appeared** or worsened ‚Äî there could be a pain or medical cause
- You've tried calming products for **4‚Äì6 weeks with no improvement**
- Your dog is showing **aggression** linked to fear or anxiety
- The anxiety is seriously affecting **your dog's quality of life** or yours

Your vet may recommend prescription medication (such as fluoxetine or clomipramine), a referral to a certified clinical animal behaviourist (look for ABTC-registered practitioners), or a combination of medication and calming products.

There's no shame in seeking help. Anxiety is a welfare issue, and your dog deserves to feel safe.
