## Introduction (150 words)

You leave for work, and five minutes later your neighbour sends a video of your dog howling. Or you come home to a shredded sofa cushion, a puddle by the door, and a dog who greets you like you've been gone for six months.

Separation anxiety is one of the most distressing — and misunderstood — problems in dog ownership. It's not your dog being naughty. It's not "attention-seeking." It's a genuine panic response, and it deserves a genuine solution.

The good news: it's one of the most successfully treated behaviour problems in dogs, when handled correctly. The bad news: most of the advice floating around — crating your dog, ignoring the behaviour, "just leaving them to cry" — can actively make it worse.

This guide covers everything: how to tell if your dog genuinely has separation anxiety, how to assess severity, and the step-by-step protocol that actually works.

## 1. What Is Separation Anxiety?

Separation anxiety (SA) is a clinical behaviour condition where a dog experiences genuine distress when separated from their attachment figure — usually one specific person, sometimes two.

It is not:
- A dog being "spoilt"
- Boredom (a bored dog isn't panicking, they're just looking for something to do)
- Spite or revenge for being left
- A training problem in the traditional sense

It is:
- A fear response — the canine equivalent of a panic attack
- Often rooted in insecure attachment and unpredictable early experience
- Physiologically measurable: cortisol spikes, heart rate elevation, panting begin within minutes of departure

The condition was formally recognised in the AVSAB (American Veterinary Society of Animal Behaviour) position statement in 2008 and is now well-established in UK clinical practice. The ABTC (Animal Behaviour and Training Council) classifies it as a welfare concern requiring professional assessment in moderate to severe cases.

## 2. True SA vs. Pseudo-Separation Anxiety — The Difference Matters

Before starting any protocol, it's critical to distinguish between these two very different presentations:

### True Separation Anxiety
- Dog is panicking. The behaviour starts immediately (within 30 seconds to 5 minutes of departure).
- Physical symptoms: trembling, hypersalivation, vomiting, self-injury from escape attempts
- Directed at a specific person — the dog may settle fine if left with anyone else
- Does NOT respond well to basic training alone; needs a systematic desensitisation programme
- In severe cases, medication is required before behavioural work can begin

### Pseudo-Separation Anxiety (Simulated SA)
- Dog is frustrated or under-exercised, not panicking
- Behaviour often starts later (10–30 minutes in)
- Less intense — may bark on and off, destroy things selectively, but settles eventually
- Responds well to increased exercise, enrichment, and basic alone training

**How to tell the difference:** Set up a camera and leave. Watch the footage. True SA dogs are typically distressed within the first 5 minutes. Their body language is unmistakably anxious — panting, pacing, whining, inability to settle. Pseudo-SA dogs may bark for a while, then settle or find something to chew.

This distinction changes everything about your approach.

## 3. Signs and Symptoms: What to Look For

### Before You Leave (Pre-Departure Signals)
Many dogs with SA learn to recognise departure cues — keys rattling, shoes going on, bags being packed — and begin to show distress before you've even opened the front door.

**Watch for:**
- Shadowing you from room to room as routine changes
- Panting or yawning when you pick up your keys
- Refusing breakfast on "leaving" mornings
- Whining or barking before you've even left
- Climbing on you or blocking the door

### While You're Gone (The Core Symptoms)
- Vocalisation: whining, howling, barking (often described as "frantic" by neighbours)
- Destructive behaviour — particularly around exits (doors, windows, skirting boards)
- House soiling in a dog that is fully toilet trained
- Self-harm: scratching, chewing paws, hair loss from repetitive licking
- Pacing, circling, inability to settle
- Refusing food (even a Kong left stuffed with their favourite treats)
- Hypersalivation (wet patches on bedding, floors)
- Vomiting in severe cases

### When You Return (Post-Return Symptoms)
- Excessive, frantic greeting (sustained beyond 5 minutes)
- Immediate need for physical contact — leaning, climbing
- Signs of physical fatigue (shaking, exhausted appearance) suggesting a sustained stress episode

## 4. Severity Scale: Mild, Moderate or Severe?

Knowing where your dog sits on the scale helps you choose the right approach.

| Level | What It Looks Like | Typical Camera Footage | Approach |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|----------|
| **Mild** | Whines for 10–20 min, settles, may pace occasionally | Dog settles within 30 min, normal thereafter | DIY desensitisation protocol, enrichment, management |
| **Moderate** | Sustained distress for 30–60+ min, some destruction or soiling | Pacing, vocalising, unable to settle most of the absence | DIY protocol + ABTC behaviourist guidance; consider vet consult |
| **Severe** | Distress from the moment of departure, sustained throughout entire absence, physical symptoms | Constant vocalisation, escape attempts, visible panic | Vet appointment first — medication almost always required before behavioural work can succeed |

## 5. How to Assess Your Dog (The Homealone Test)

Before starting any protocol, you need footage. You cannot treat what you haven't observed.

**Step 1: Set up a camera**
A cheap home security camera (Ring, Blink, Tapo) or even an old phone propped up works fine. Position it to capture the door area and your dog's usual spots.

**Step 2: Do a short absence**
Go through your normal departure routine. Leave for 30 minutes. Come back and review the footage.

**Step 3: What to note**
- How quickly did distress start? (Within 5 min = likely true SA)
- Did it escalate, plateau, or peak then reduce?
- Was there a clear trigger (a specific noise, your car leaving)?
- Did the dog eat the Kong or treat you left?

**Step 4: Log it**
Keep a simple log: date, duration of absence, distress start time, peak behaviour, how the dog was when you returned. You'll use this to track progress.

## 6. Common Causes and Risk Factors

Separation anxiety doesn't have one cause. It's typically a combination of factors:

**Genetics and early experience**
- Dogs from uncertain or traumatic early environments are at higher risk
- Some dogs are genetically predisposed to anxious temperaments

**Attachment style**
- Dogs allowed or encouraged to follow their owners everywhere, sleep on the bed, be in constant physical contact — not causes in themselves, but they can intensify SA when it exists

**Life changes**
- A new baby or partner changing the household routine
- Moving house
- Owner returning to work after a long period at home (huge post-COVID driver — see section 14)
- Loss of another pet
- A traumatic event while alone (fireworks, a break-in, a fire alarm)

**Medical conditions**
- Pain can cause distress when alone (dog can't self-soothe)
- Cognitive Dysfunction Syndrome in older dogs often presents similarly to SA
- Hyperthyroidism has been linked to anxiety-type behaviours

**Always rule out medical causes first**, especially in dogs who develop SA suddenly in middle age or older.

## 7. Breed-Specific Risk Table

While any dog can develop separation anxiety, some breeds are statistically higher risk due to their breeding history for close human partnership.

| Breed Category | Risk Level | Why |
|----------------|-----------|-----|
| Hungarian Vizsla | 🔴 Very High | Bred for constant handler proximity; clinically one of the most commonly affected breeds |
| Border Collie | 🔴 Very High | Intense human focus, needs constant mental engagement |
| Labrador / Golden Retriever | 🟠 High | Highly social, strong attachment; working/field lines tend to cope better than show lines |
| German Shepherd | 🟠 High | Loyal, protective breed; can form intense single-person attachments |
| Springer Spaniel | 🟠 High | Energetic, people-focused; especially show-bred lines |
| French Bulldog | 🟠 High | Brachycephalic breeds show higher anxiety rates; breathing difficulties amplify stress |
| Bichon Frise / Maltese / Shih Tzu | 🟡 Moderate-High | Companion breeds; often live in close owner proximity |
| Greyhound / Sighthound | 🟡 Moderate | Sensitive temperaments; ex-racing dogs often have additional adaptation challenges |
| Independent breeds (Basenji, Shiba Inu, Chow Chow) | 🟢 Lower | Generally more tolerant of alone time, though individual variation applies |

*Note: Mixed breeds can inherit any combination of traits. Early assessment is the only reliable guide.*

## 8. The Desensitisation Protocol (Step-by-Step)

This is the gold-standard approach, adapted from the work of Malena DeMartini (author of *Separation Anxiety in Dogs*) and endorsed by ABTC accredited practitioners in the UK.

**The core principle:** You build your dog's tolerance for alone time incrementally, never pushing past the threshold at which they become anxious. Every session must end before distress begins.

**What you'll need:**
- A camera or monitor (essential — you cannot do this blind)
- A filled Kong or high-value chew for each session
- 10–20 minutes per day, 5–7 days per week
- Patience — this takes weeks, not days

---

### Phase 1: Foundation (Days 1–7)

**Goal:** Establish that short alone periods are safe and end in good things.

**Step 1.1 — The 30-second departure**
Give your dog a Kong. Step outside the door. Come back in 30 seconds. Don't make a fuss on return.

**Step 1.2 — Vary the duration randomly**
10 seconds. 45 seconds. 20 seconds. 1 minute. Random variation is important — it prevents the dog learning that each absence will be longer than the last (which can itself trigger anticipatory anxiety).

**Step 1.3 — Monitor constantly**
Watch the camera. The moment you see pre-distress signals (stops eating, looks up and stiffens), you've hit threshold. Come back now — don't wait for full panic.

**Step 1.4 — End every session before distress**
This is the non-negotiable rule. A session that ends in panic is a setback, not progress.

---

### Phase 2: Building Duration (Weeks 2–4)

Once your dog is reliably settling for 1–2 minutes, begin extending in small, random increments.

**Example week 2 session plan (in minutes):**
`1 → 2 → 0.5 → 3 → 1 → 4 → 2 → 5`

Never increase by more than 30–60 seconds in any single jump at this early stage.

**Signs you're moving too fast:**
- Dog stops eating the Kong immediately on your departure
- Pacing starts within the first minute
- Barking or whining appears earlier than in previous sessions

If this happens: step back. Run 3–4 successful shorter sessions before trying the longer duration again.

---

### Phase 3: Real-World Absences (Weeks 4–8+)

By this point, your dog should be managing 15–30 minutes comfortably. Now you begin practising in more realistic scenarios:

- Different departure routes (back door vs front door)
- Getting in the car and coming back (many dogs find the car departure more triggering)
- Leaving during different times of day
- Varying the pre-departure routine

**The 2-hour milestone**
Most dogs need 6–12 weeks to reach 2 hours. Some take longer. Do not rush this stage — regression from a bad session at week 8 is demoralising and genuinely sets progress back.

## 9. Pre-Departure Cue Training

If your dog is already anxious before you've left (showing pre-departure stress when you pick up your keys), you need to address the cues first.

**Step 1: Identify the cues**
Common triggers: keys jangling, bag being packed, shoes being put on, coat being taken off the hook, alarm being set on phone.

**Step 2: Desensitise each cue separately**
Pick up your keys. Sit back down. Watch TV. Repeat 10× daily until your dog stops reacting to the keys.

Put your shoes on. Sit at your desk. Work for an hour. Repeat until your dog no longer reads shoes = departure.

**Step 3: Mix the cues**
Once individual cues are neutral, combine them in different orders. Sometimes wear your coat and not leave. Sometimes leave without your coat.

This stage alone can take 1–2 weeks but dramatically smooths the main protocol.

## 11. Enrichment Strategies for Alone Time

For mild-moderate cases where the dog can settle, enrichment extends positive alone time:

**Feeding enrichment (makes meal times last 20–40 minutes)**
- Scatter feed in garden rather than bowl-feeding
- Stuffed, frozen Kong (freeze 24 hours for maximum duration)
- Snuffle mat with breakfast kibble
- LickiMat with peanut butter, banana, or wet food (check no xylitol)

**Foraging games**
- Hide treats in cardboard boxes (cut holes, let them sniff them out)
- Muffin tin puzzle (tennis balls over each cup, kibble underneath)
- DIY bottle spinner (bottles on a wooden rod)

**Sensory enrichment**
- Dog Calm Spotify playlist or Through a Dog's Ear recordings (shown to reduce heart rate in some studies)
- Leaving a worn T-shirt near their bed (your scent is calming)
- White noise machine if external noise is a trigger

**Important:** None of these will stop true SA on their own. They're tools for building positive associations with alone time once baseline tolerance exists.

## 12. Medication: When and What?

This section is informational only — all decisions about medication must be made with your vet.

For moderate-severe separation anxiety, medication is often necessary before behavioural work can succeed. A dog in full panic cannot learn. The medication lowers the baseline anxiety enough for the dog to absorb the desensitisation work.

**Long-term medications (daily, prescribed by vet)**

| Drug | Type | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| Fluoxetine (Prozac) | SSRI | Most commonly prescribed for SA in the UK; takes 4–6 weeks to reach full effect; no sedation |
| Clomipramine (Clomicalm) | Tricyclic antidepressant | Licensed for SA in dogs in UK; effective; requires cardiac screening in some dogs |
| Sertraline | SSRI | Off-label but increasingly used; good tolerability |

**Situational medications (as-needed, prescribed by vet)**

| Drug | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Trazodone | Anti-anxiety; used for predictable high-stress events; gives 4–8h of reduced anxiety |
| Gabapentin | Increasingly used in combination; off-label |
| Alprazolam / Diazepam | Short-acting benzodiazepines; rarely used as primary treatment now |

**The honest take:** Medication is not a permanent crutch. It's a scaffold that enables behavioural change. Many dogs are successfully weaned off medication once the desensitisation programme is complete. The goal is always behavioural independence — the medication just makes it reachable.

## 13. When to Call a Behaviourist

Consider professional help if:

- Your dog is at moderate-severe level (suffering is a welfare issue — don't delay)
- You've been working the protocol for 4+ weeks without clear progress
- Your dog is causing noise complaints or damaging property
- You're struggling with consistency or need a personalised plan
- Your vet recommends medication alongside behaviour support

**How to find a qualified behaviourist in the UK:**

Always look for ABTC accreditation. The ABTC (Animal Behaviour and Training Council) is the government-recognised register of animal behaviour practitioners in the UK.

- **Clinical Animal Behaviourist (CAB)** — highest level; can work alongside vets on complex cases involving medication
- **Animal Training Instructor (ATI) with behaviour endorsement** — can support mild-moderate cases

🔗 Find a CAB near you: [abtc.org.uk/registers](https://abtc.org.uk)
🔗 APBC (Association of Pet Behaviour Counsellors): [apbc.org.uk](https://apbc.org.uk)

**⚠️ Red flags when hiring a behaviourist:**
- Uses or advocates punishment, choke chains, prong collars, or e-collars for anxiety
- Claims to "cure" separation anxiety in a single session
- Won't tell you their qualifications
- Recommends flooding (forcing the dog to stay alone until they "give up" — this is harmful)

## 14. Working From Home Dogs: The New Problem Nobody's Talking About

Since COVID, millions of UK families got dogs while working from home. Now, with return-to-office mandates and hybrid working patterns changing, a generation of dogs who have never spent significant time alone are suddenly being left for 6–8 hours.

This is a genuine welfare crisis. The RSPCA reported a significant rise in behaviour referrals from 2022 onwards, with SA-type presentations a leading complaint.

**If this is your situation:**

**Start now, before you go back**
The longer a dog goes without alone training, the harder it becomes. Begin the desensitisation protocol even while you're still home — micro-absences, room separation, building independence.

**Use room separation as practice**
Put a baby gate across the hall. Work in your office with the dog on the other side. Start with 5 minutes, build gradually. This teaches the dog that your absence from their immediate vicinity is normal.

**Don't cold-turkey it**
If you're returning to office next Monday and your dog has never been alone for more than an hour, you cannot fix this over the weekend. Arrange childcare for the dog (a trusted friend, a dog daycare, a dog walker) while you work the protocol over the following weeks.

**Doggy daycare is not a cure**
It manages the problem. It doesn't treat it. The dog will still panic when left alone on weekends or in any other context. Use it as management while the protocol runs.

## 15. Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will getting a second dog fix separation anxiety?**
A: Rarely. True SA is about separation from the attachment person — another dog doesn't replace them. Some dogs with milder pseudo-SA do better with canine company, but it shouldn't be your primary strategy. It also introduces the complexity of two dogs, one of whom may develop anxiety.

**Q: Is it cruel to crate a dog with separation anxiety?**
A: If your dog already has SA, crating can make things significantly worse — it adds confinement to panic, which can lead to injury as they try to escape. Many dogs with SA cause more harm when crated. Unless your dog has a genuinely positive association with their crate (positive confinement training from puppyhood), don't use it as an SA management tool.

**Q: My neighbour says my dog barks all day. Could it be territorial, not anxiety?**
A: Possibly. Territorial or alarm barking tends to be reactive — triggered by specific stimuli (people walking past, delivery vans) and stops once the trigger moves on. SA barking tends to be more continuous, especially at the start of the absence, and is accompanied by other distress signals on camera footage. Review your footage carefully.

**Q: My dog is fine when I go on holiday and leave them with my mum. Does that mean they don't have SA?**
A: Possibly — or your mum has become a secondary attachment figure. True single-attachment SA (attached to one person exclusively) is more severe but less common. Multi-attachment SA (attached to the household generally, can include grandparent when the family isn't there) is different again. The key question: is your dog distressed when home alone or only when *you* specifically are gone?

**Q: How long does the protocol take?**
A: Realistically, 6–16 weeks for mild-moderate cases when worked consistently. Severe cases with medication can take 3–6 months before reliable multi-hour absences are possible. This is a long game — there are no shortcuts.

**Q: Can I hire a dog trainer to fix this?**
A: Only if they're qualified specifically in behaviour. SA is a clinical behaviour condition — not a training problem. A standard dog trainer who focuses on obedience won't have the skills for this. Look for an ABTC-accredited Clinical Animal Behaviourist or a trainer who specifically lists SA as a specialism.
