## What to Look for in an Outdoor Dog Kennel

Before jumping into specific recommendations, here's what separates a good kennel from a bad one:

### 1. Size (This Is Non-Negotiable)

Your dog needs to be able to:
- Stand up fully without ducking
- Turn around comfortably
- Lie stretched out in any direction

Use this as a starting guide:

| Dog Size | Typical Breeds | Minimum Kennel Size (L x W x H) |
|----------|---------------|----------------------------------|
| Small (up to 10kg) | Chihuahua, Shih Tzu, Miniature Dachshund | 60cm x 55cm x 65cm |
| Medium (10–25kg) | Cocker Spaniel, Beagle, Staffordshire Bull Terrier | 90cm x 75cm x 85cm |
| Large (25–40kg) | Labrador, Golden Retriever, Dalmatian | 120cm x 90cm x 100cm |
| XL (40kg+) | German Shepherd, Rottweiler, Great Dane | 150cm x 120cm x 120cm+ |

Always size up if your dog is at the top of a weight bracket or is a long-backed breed like a Greyhound.

### 2. Weatherproofing

The UK is not a warm country. Your kennel needs to handle:
- **Rain** — proper overlapping roof panels, not just a flat sheet
- **Wind** — solid construction that won't shift or rattle
- **Sun** — ventilation to prevent heat buildup in summer
- **Cold** — insulated walls for use through winter months

Look for **tongue-and-groove construction** in wood kennels (far better than standard overlapping boards) and a fully waterproofed roof.

### 3. Insulation

This is where budget kennels fall short. Uninsulated wood kennels can get extremely hot inside in direct sun — dangerously so for dogs. Look for:
- Double-wall construction
- Raised floor (off the ground, prevents cold coming through)
- Separate sleeping area in kennel-and-run combos

### 4. Material

**Timber (Pressure-Treated Pine or Spruce)**
Most outdoor dog kennels are made from FSC-certified softwood. Quality varies enormously. Look for:
- Pressure-treated wood (resists rot for 10–15 years)
- Minimum 18mm board thickness (cheaper kennels use 12mm)
- Smooth internal finish (no splinters)

**Plastic/Resin**
Easier to clean, completely waterproof, and resistant to chewing. Heavier to move. Some dogs find them less comfortable without added bedding.

**Metal**
Mainly used for runs rather than the kennel itself. Galvanised steel is the best option for corrosion resistance.

### 5. Ventilation

Hot spots kill dogs. Any outdoor kennel used in summer must have:
- Vents in the side or rear wall
- A roof design that doesn't trap heat
- A position in the garden away from direct afternoon sun

The [PDSA](https://www.pdsa.org.uk/) advises keeping dogs out of full sun between 11am and 3pm from May–September, regardless of kennel quality.

## Kennel Placement: What the Experts Say

Location matters as much as kennel quality. [Dogs Trust](https://www.dogstrust.org.uk/) and the PDSA advise:

**✅ Good placement:**
- Partially shaded (morning sun only in summer)
- Sheltered from prevailing wind
- Visible from the house (dogs are social and find this reassuring)
- On level ground with good drainage

**❌ Avoid:**
- Full afternoon sun (risks heatstroke May–September)
- Against north-facing walls in winter (excessive cold and damp)
- Isolated corners (increases anxiety)
- Low-lying areas prone to pooling water

## Breed-Specific Recommendations

### Labrador Retriever & Golden Retriever
Labs and Goldens are generally hardy and enjoy outdoor time. They cope well in most kennels, but their love of mud and water means easy-to-clean options are worth prioritising. **Best choice:** Gor Pets Premium (Large or XL).

### German Shepherd
GSD owners need to think about a few things: head height (GSDs have tall, upright ears), chewing (young GSDs can be destructive), and the need for substantial space. **Best choice:** Hartman & Rose Pro or Shire Apex Extra.

### French Bulldog
Frenchies are brachycephalic (flat-faced) and overheat very easily. They should NOT be in an outdoor kennel in direct summer sun. If you do use a kennel, it must be in permanent shade with excellent ventilation. **Best choice:** Trixie Natura Vario (insulated, with vents) — and always monitor.

### Greyhound / Lurcher / Whippet
Sighthounds have very little body fat and a single-layer coat — they feel the cold intensely. Any outdoor kennel must be well-insulated and well-padded. **Best choice:** Trixie Natura Vario with an extra fleece bed inside.

### Border Collie & Working Breeds
Collies need mental stimulation more than physical containment. A kennel-and-run combo allows more movement during outdoor time. **Best choice:** Kerbl Deluxe with run (XL size).

### Dachshund (Standard)
Dachshunds can have back problems; avoid kennels with a steep step-up entry. Look for low threshold designs or add a small ramp. **Best choice:** Merax (for miniature) or Rowlinson Apex (for standard).

## Kennel Maintenance: What to Do & When

Even a pressure-treated kennel needs regular care to reach its 10–15 year lifespan.

**Every 3–6 months:**
- Sweep out and disinfect interior with [Johnsons Kennel Cleaner](#) or diluted F10
- Check for splinters inside and sand smooth if found
- Check hinges and door catch

**Annually:**
- Apply preservative wood treatment to exterior (not creosote — toxic to dogs)
- Check and re-caulk any gaps in the roof panels
- Check floor panel for soft spots (sign of rot)

**After winter:**
- Inspect for water ingress
- Check for mould in corners (use a dog-safe disinfectant)

## What the Law Says

The [Animal Welfare Act 2006](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/45/contents) requires that dogs have:
- A suitable environment (including shelter from extremes of weather)
- The ability to exhibit normal behaviour patterns
- Protection from pain, suffering and disease

Leaving a dog in an inadequate kennel — whether too hot, too cold, too cramped, or without water — can constitute an animal welfare offence. The RSPCA can investigate and prosecute.

**You should never:**
- Leave a dog in a kennel for more than a few hours unsupervised
- Use a kennel as a punishment
- Lock a dog in without access to fresh water
- Leave a dog outside overnight in cold weather without veterinary advice

## Kennel vs Dog Run vs Dog House: What's the Difference?

Confusing terminology is everywhere in this category. Here's a quick clarifier:

- **Dog kennel** — a small wooden or plastic shelter for one dog
- **Dog run** — a secure fenced exercise area (may or may not include a kennel)
- **Dog house** — US term for kennel; used interchangeably in UK
- **Kennel and run combo** — kennel unit connected to a secure exercise pen (best of both worlds)
- **Dog cage/crate** — indoor or transportable metal enclosure (not an outdoor shelter)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it cruel to keep a dog in an outdoor kennel?**
A well-sized, weatherproof kennel used for supervised outdoor time is completely appropriate. Kennels become problematic if dogs are isolated in them for long periods without human contact, in extreme temperatures, or without enrichment. The Dogs Trust is clear: dogs are social animals that need regular company and shouldn't be left alone for more than four hours at a stretch.

**What size kennel do I need for a Labrador?**
A standard Labrador (25–35kg) needs a kennel of at least 120cm x 90cm x 100cm (L x W x H). Always measure your specific dog from nose to tail base and add 30cm for the length, plus add 30cm to shoulder height for the roof clearance.

**Can dogs sleep in an outdoor kennel in winter?**
This depends heavily on the dog, the kennel, and the location. Short-coated breeds, elderly dogs, puppies, and dogs with health conditions should not sleep outdoors in UK winters without heating. Hardy breeds with thick double coats (Huskies, Malamutes, some Collies) can tolerate properly insulated kennels in mild UK winters. Always consult your vet for individual advice.

**How do I stop a kennel getting too hot in summer?**
Position it in shade (east-facing ideally — morning sun, sheltered from afternoon heat). Ensure ventilation openings are clear. Never use a dark-coloured kennel in full sun. Add a cooling mat inside. Provide a constant supply of fresh water. The PDSA advises being especially vigilant between 11am–3pm in warm weather.

**Do wooden kennels need treating?**
Yes. Pressure-treated kennels come factory-treated, but should be re-treated annually with a dog-safe wood preservative. Do not use creosote or oil-based stains — many contain chemicals that are toxic to dogs. Wait 48 hours after treatment before allowing your dog access.

**Should a dog kennel have a floor?**
Always. A kennel without a raised floor sits directly on damp ground, which means cold and moisture will come straight through. If your kennel doesn't include a floor, add a rubber mat or wooden platform underneath. The Gor Pets kennels include a raised floor as standard, which is one reason they're consistently recommended.
