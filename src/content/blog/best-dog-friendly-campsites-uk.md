## Introduction

Camping with your dog is one of life's great pleasures. Fresh air, open fields, campfire evenings — and a tail that doesn't stop wagging for the entire trip. But not every campsite welcomes dogs, and those that do vary wildly in what they offer. Some provide a small patch of grass and a "dogs must be on leads" sign. Others have dedicated dog wash stations, fenced meadows, and dog-friendly pub agreements with the local village.

This guide cuts through the noise. We've researched the best dog-friendly campsites across every region of the UK — from the Scottish Highlands to the Jurassic Coast — covering tent pitches, touring spots, and glamping pods alike. We've included sites that go beyond tolerating dogs to actually *welcoming* them.

**What we looked for:**
- Dogs explicitly allowed (not just "considered on request")
- On-site or nearby off-lead exercise
- Dog-friendly facilities (wash stations, dog bins, etc.)
- Safe, secure pitches without hazards
- Proximity to dog-friendly walks, beaches, or countryside

## Dog Camping Essentials: What to Pack

Before we get to the sites, here's what experienced dog campers bring every time:

**Sleeping & Comfort**
- Dog sleeping bag or insulated mat (nights get cold even in summer)
- Travel crate or familiar bed for den security
- Their regular food + portable storage containers

**Safety & ID**
- Up-to-date microchip details (required by law)
- ID tag with mobile number (phone signal can be poor — give someone else's number too)
- [Tractive GPS Tracker](https://affiliate.link) — essential on unfamiliar ground, especially at night
- First aid kit (see our [Dog First Aid Guide](#))

**Walking & Adventure**
- Well-fitted harness (not just collar on trails)
- Long line (10m) for off-lead training on open ground
- Collapsible water bowl
- Poo bags (more than you think you need — double it)
- Towel for muddy paws and river splashes

**Campfire Evenings**
- [LickiMat](https://affiliate.link) with frozen peanut butter — keeps them calm and settled
- High-value chew for the evening wind-down
- Tether spike if your dog is a roamer

**Tip:** Pack your dog's kit in a separate dry bag. Wet dog gear mixed with your sleeping bag is the worst start to day two.

## UK Dog-Friendly Campsite Rules: What to Expect

Most dog-friendly campsites in the UK operate on similar rules:

- **Maximum dogs per pitch:** Usually 1–2; always check before booking
- **Lead rules:** On leads in the main campsite; many have a dedicated off-lead area
- **Not left unattended:** You cannot leave dogs alone in tents or pods at most sites
- **Up-to-date vaccinations:** Not always asked for, but best practice
- **Seasonal restrictions:** Some sites restrict dogs during peak summer school holidays

*Always book directly and mention your dogs — even at "dog-friendly" sites, some pitches are dog-free zones.*

## National Park Camping: What You Need to Know

The UK's National Parks are prime camping territory for dogs. Here's the quick guide:

### Dartmoor (Wild Camping Legal)
The only National Park in England where wild camping is legally permitted. Dogs can join you — but must be under close control near livestock, and ground-nesting bird restrictions apply April–July in some areas. Keep to the permitted zone (2 nights max, 100m from roads/enclosures).

### Cairngorms (Scotland — Wild Camping Rights)
Under Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003, responsible access is a legal right. Responsible wild camping with dogs is permitted — but the Scottish Outdoor Access Code requires dogs to be under close control around livestock and wildlife.

### Snowdonia, Lake District, Yorkshire Dales, Peak District
Wild camping is NOT a legal right in these parks. You must use designated sites or get landowner permission. However, all these parks have excellent campsite options within their boundaries.

## Booking Tips: Getting the Best Dog-Friendly Pitch

**1. Call before you book**
Online booking forms don't always capture dog requirements. Call the site to confirm your specific pitch is dog-friendly (some pitches in "dog-friendly" sites are dog-free zones).

**2. Book early for summer**
Peak weeks (late July, all of August) book out by January–February at popular dog-friendly sites. If you have school-age children, you're competing for a smaller pool of pitches.

**3. Off-season is better for dogs**
September and October are the sweet spot — school holidays are over, sites are quieter, walks are less crowded, and dogs can often go to beaches that have summer restrictions.

**4. Check breed/size limits**
A handful of sites restrict certain breeds or large dogs in glamping accommodation due to furniture damage concerns. Not common, but worth confirming.

**5. Ask about the local pub**
The best dog-friendly campsites are usually near a dog-friendly pub. Ask the site team — they'll know.

## Dog Safety: Campsite Hazards to Watch For

Campsites aren't entirely risk-free for dogs. Keep an eye on:

**BBQ ash and coals:** Dogs will eat coal ash, which contains toxic compounds and sharp fragments. Ensure used BBQ trays are secured or out of reach.

**Groundwater and stagnant pools:** Blue-green algae risk in summer. If the water smells or looks unusual, keep dogs out. UKHSA updates the National Bloom Alert system — check before camping near lakes.

**Guy ropes and tent pegs:** Dogs (and you) will trip over these in the dark. Glow-in-the-dark pegs and markers are worth it.

**Shared bins:** Campsite bins are often full of food waste — highly attractive to dogs. Keep yours away from communal areas where possible.

**Wild mushrooms:** Autumn campsites have foraging opportunities. Numerous UK mushroom species are toxic to dogs. Train a "leave it" command before camping trips.

**Livestock:** If your dog is not livestock-safe, keep on lead in any fields with sheep, cattle, or horses. A dog worrying livestock can legally be shot by a farmer in England, Wales, and Scotland.

## Travelling with Puppies: Campsite Advice

Camping with a young puppy (under 6 months) comes with additional considerations:

- **Vaccination status:** Your puppy must be fully vaccinated before mixing with other dogs at public sites. Consult your vet on timing.
- **Socialisation opportunity:** Campsites expose puppies to new sounds, smells, people, and dogs — a brilliant (and safe) socialisation environment once vaccinated.
- **Sleep disruption:** Puppies wake up early. Inform neighbours or pick a pitch away from other tents.
- **Cold nights:** Puppies can't regulate temperature as efficiently as adults. Bring extra bedding and consider a puppy-specific sleeping bag.
- **Short trips first:** A weekend trip before a week-long holiday lets you discover what your puppy needs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can dogs go on all campsites in the UK?**
A: No. Many campsites explicitly exclude dogs, particularly in peak summer. Always check before booking. "Pet-friendly" and "dog-friendly" are sometimes used interchangeably but aren't the same — some "pet-friendly" sites mean indoor pets or small animals only.

**Q: Are dogs allowed in UK National Parks for camping?**
A: Dogs are welcome in National Parks, but you must camp on designated sites in most parks (Dartmoor is the exception where wild camping is legally permitted in certain zones). Dogs must be under close control around livestock and wildlife.

**Q: What's the best region for dog-friendly camping in the UK?**
A: Scotland and Pembrokeshire consistently top the list — both offer dog-friendly beaches year-round, extensive off-lead countryside, and genuine welcome for dogs at most sites. Cornwall is excellent in spring and autumn when summer beach bans don't apply.

**Q: Do I need dog insurance for camping?**
A: Your standard dog insurance covers most vet bills including accidents on camping trips. Check your policy for "away from home" coverage. [Petplan](https://affiliate.link) and [ManyPets](https://affiliate.link) both offer policies that cover treatment throughout the UK.

**Q: My dog is reactive — can we still camp?**
A: Yes, with some planning. Choose smaller, quieter sites with good pitch spacing. Avoid bank holiday weekends at popular sites. Bring a pop-up privacy barrier for the pitch entrance. Early evening arrivals mean you can get settled before other dogs are around.

**Q: Are dogs allowed on the beach near campsites in summer?**
A: This is site-specific. Many popular beaches in Devon, Cornwall, and Wales ban dogs from May to September (typically 10am–6pm). Always check the specific beach rules before planning a trip. Some dog-friendly campsites have private beach access that avoids public restrictions.
