## Introduction

*Every vet knows it. Most dog owners don't: by the time your dog is three years old, there's an 87% chance they already have some degree of dental disease.*

Not a small issue. Not a cosmetic one. Dental disease — also called periodontal disease — is the single most common health condition diagnosed in dogs in the UK. Left untreated, it doesn't just cause bad breath and lost teeth. It causes chronic pain, difficulty eating, and has been linked to heart, liver, and kidney disease as bacteria from infected gums enter the bloodstream.

The good news? Most dental disease is preventable. With the right routine, the right products, and — for some dogs — a professional clean, your dog's teeth can stay healthy for life.

This guide covers everything: how to brush your dog's teeth (even if they hate it), which dental products actually work, when professional cleaning is necessary, breed-specific risks, and how to spot the signs of a problem before it gets serious.

## Understanding Dog Dental Disease

### How It Starts: Plaque to Periodontal Disease

Every time your dog eats, bacteria form a sticky film on the teeth called **plaque**. Within 24–36 hours, if plaque isn't removed, it starts to mineralise into **tartar** (also called calculus) — that hard, yellow-brown deposit you see on teeth. Unlike plaque, tartar can't be brushed off at home. Once it's there, only a vet can remove it.

Tartar builds up around and below the gum line, causing **gingivitis** — inflammation of the gums. You'll see this as reddening along the gum edge. At this stage, the process is still reversible with treatment and better home care.

Without intervention, gingivitis progresses to **periodontal disease**: the infection spreads below the gum line and starts destroying the ligaments and bone that hold the teeth in place. Teeth become loose and painful. By this stage, extractions are often unavoidable.

**The four stages of periodontal disease:**

| Stage | What's Happening | Reversible? | Treatment |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|-----------|
| 0 | Healthy teeth and gums | N/A | Maintenance only |
| 1 | Gingivitis only — no bone loss | Yes | Professional clean + home care |
| 2 | Early periodontitis — <25% bone/attachment loss | Partially | Professional clean + possible root planing |
| 3 | Moderate periodontitis — 25–50% loss | No | Professional clean, root planing, possible extractions |
| 4 | Advanced periodontitis — >50% loss | No | Extractions, pain management, antibiotics |

*Source: BSAVA classification, adapted from the Veterinary Oral Health Council*

### Why Dental Disease Matters Beyond the Mouth

Bad breath is the most obvious sign — but the consequences of untreated dental disease go further:

- **Chronic pain:** Dogs are stoic. Many continue eating normally even with severely painful mouths. You won't always know they're suffering.
- **Systemic disease:** Bacteria from periodontal infections can enter the bloodstream. The BVDA notes associations between severe dental disease and changes in heart, liver, and kidney function — though direct causation in dogs is still being researched.
- **Weight loss and malnutrition:** Dogs with painful mouths often struggle to chew, particularly affecting kibble-fed dogs.
- **Behavioural changes:** Irritability, reluctance to be touched around the head, and reduced play can all be signs of dental pain.

## How to Brush Your Dog's Teeth

Brushing is the gold standard. Nothing else — no chew, no water additive, no dental diet — matches the mechanical plaque removal that brushing achieves. The BVDA recommends **daily brushing** for optimal results; brushing every other day still provides significant benefit.

### What You Need

**Toothbrush options:**
- **Finger toothbrush** (silicone, fits over your fingertip) — best for starting out and for small dogs
- **Long-handled dog toothbrush** — better reach for larger dogs; angled heads help with back teeth
- **Double-headed toothbrush** — cleans inner and outer surfaces simultaneously; can be useful for compliant dogs

**Toothpaste — always use dog-specific:**
Human toothpaste contains fluoride, which is toxic to dogs (they swallow toothpaste rather than spitting). It also contains xylitol in some brands — a severe dog toxin. Always use a paste specifically formulated for dogs.

### The 4-Week Introduction Programme

Never try to brush a dog's teeth cold. This is how you create a dog that runs from the bathroom. Instead, spend 4 weeks building positive associations.

**Week 1 — Flavour introduction**
- Dip your finger in the toothpaste
- Let your dog lick it off
- Praise heavily and give a treat
- 1–2 minutes daily; stop before they get bored
- No brushing yet

**Week 2 — Gum touching**
- Let them lick paste off your finger again
- Then gently rub your paste-covered finger along the gum line of the front teeth (canines and incisors)
- Keep sessions short (30–60 seconds)
- End every session with praise and a treat

**Week 3 — Introduce the brush**
- Show them the toothbrush, let them sniff and lick it
- Run the brush along the outer surface of the front teeth only
- Focus on the **gum margin** — this is where plaque accumulates most
- Don't force the mouth open; work with the lips pulled back

**Week 4 — Working toward a full mouth**
- Gradually extend to the back teeth (premolars and molars) — these are the most important and the most neglected
- Aim for the outer surfaces — you don't need to brush the inside surfaces; the tongue does most of the cleaning there
- Build to 2 minutes total

**The correct technique:**
Hold the brush at a **45-degree angle** to the gum line. Use small circular or back-and-forth strokes. Focus time on the back upper teeth (maxillary 4th premolars and 1st molars) — these are where tartar builds fastest, and where periodontal disease is most severe in dogs.

### Tips for Resistant Dogs

- Use a flavour they love — chicken, beef, peanut butter varieties exist
- Brush after exercise when they're calm
- Use a mat with lickable treat paste (LickiMat with Peanut Butter) as a distraction tool
- Try a finger brush first if a toothbrush triggers resistance
- If a dog is genuinely impossible to brush, speak to your vet — some dogs may need sedation for cleaning and a different home care approach

## Dental Products That Work (and Some That Don't)

For dogs who won't tolerate brushing — or as a supplement to brushing — there's a growing range of products. Not all are equal. The Veterinary Oral Health Council (VOHC) maintains a list of products that have passed clinical efficacy trials. Look for the VOHC seal.

### Dental Chews

The most popular category. Work mechanically — the chewing action scrapes plaque. Effectiveness depends heavily on:
- Texture (needs to be firm enough to require sustained chewing)
- Whether the dog actually chews (some swallow whole)
- Daily use

**UK-available options with VOHC approval:**
- **Pedigree Dentastix** (daily, most widely available, VOHC approved for tartar reduction)
- **Hills Prescription Diet Dental Chews** (vet-only, strongest clinical evidence)
- **Purina DentaLife** (VOHC approved)
- **Whimzees** (vegetable-based, good for dogs with meat sensitivities)

**What to watch with dental chews:**
- Calories: Dentastix for a large dog = ~90 kcal. Factor into daily intake.
- Not a substitute for brushing — reduces tartar by ~25–40% vs brushing's ~60–70%
- Size match: always buy the size appropriate for your dog's weight

**The "bend test":** A good dental chew should flex slightly when bent but not snap like a crisp. Too hard = risk of tooth fracture (see below).

### Water Additives

Added to the drinking bowl daily. Most contain enzymes or chlorhexidine that reduce bacterial load in the mouth. VOHC-approved options include **Healthymouth** and **TropiClean Fresh Breath**.

Pros: genuinely easy to use, no cooperation required  
Cons: less effective than brushing or chews; effectiveness reduces if dog drinks from puddles/other sources

### Dental Diets

Specially formulated kibbles with larger pieces (so the dog must chew) and fibre content that creates a mechanical scrubbing effect.

- **Hills Prescription Diet t/d** — strongest clinical evidence; only available via vets
- **Royal Canin Dental** — good supermarket-accessible option
- **Purina Pro Plan Dental** — VOHC approved

Worth considering for dogs that won't tolerate any other home care. Speak to your vet before switching diets.

### Enzymatic Gels and Sprays

Applied directly to the teeth (or to a finger or toy). Work biochemically rather than mechanically.

- **Logic Oral Hygiene Gel** — widely recommended by UK vets; can be applied with a finger if brushing is impossible
- **Tropiclean Fresh Breath Oral Care Gel** — popular, no brushing required
- **Dentisept Chlorhexidine Gel** — prescription-strength; your vet may prescribe post-clean

### Products to Be Cautious About

**Hard chews and bones:** Antlers, nylon bones, hooves, and real bones are hard enough to fracture teeth. The "thumbnail test" from veterinary dentistry: if you press your thumbnail firmly into the object and it doesn't dent, it's too hard for a dog's teeth. Tooth fractures (particularly the carnassial tooth — the large upper 4th premolar) are painful, frequently become abscessed, and expensive to treat. The BVDA advises against all very hard chew items.

**Rawhide:** Not a dental product. Softens quickly, offers minimal mechanical benefit, and carries contamination and choking risks.

**Anesthetic-free dental scaling:** Offered by some groomers and pet shops. The BVDA and RCVS firmly oppose this practice. Scaling without anaesthesia only removes visible tartar from tooth surfaces, does nothing below the gum line (where disease lives), stresses the dog, and risks injury. A proper dental clean requires a general anaesthetic so the vet can probe every tooth and treat below the gum line.

## Professional Dental Cleaning: What to Expect

Even with excellent home care, most dogs benefit from a professional clean (dental prophylaxis) every 1–3 years. For some breeds, annually.

### What Happens at a Dental

1. **Pre-anaesthetic bloods** — especially important for dogs over 7 and those with known health issues
2. **General anaesthesia** — necessary for a safe, thorough clean
3. **Full mouth radiographs** — many practices now take dental X-rays as standard; they reveal what's happening below the gum line (root resorption, periapical abscesses, bone loss) that looks normal on examination
4. **Ultrasonic scaling** — removes tartar above and below the gum line
5. **Subgingival curettage** — removal of infected material below the gum line (required for stage 2+ periodontal disease)
6. **Polishing** — smooths enamel to slow future plaque adhesion
7. **Tooth extractions if required** — performed under the same anaesthetic; your vet will call during the procedure to update you if extractions are needed
8. **Discharge with pain relief** — dogs recover quickly from extractions; most eat normally within 24–48 hours

## Breed-Specific Dental Risks

Some dogs are significantly more prone to dental disease than others — and knowing your breed's risks lets you get ahead of problems.

### Small and Toy Breeds (Highest Risk)

**Chihuahuas, Yorkshire Terriers, Dachshunds, Pomeranians, Maltese, Shih Tzus, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels**

Small breeds are disproportionately affected by dental disease. Several reasons:
- Their adult teeth are the same size as larger dogs' but crammed into a much smaller jaw — crowding causes food and plaque to accumulate in tight spaces
- Many are fed soft food, which doesn't provide any mechanical cleaning
- Their gum tissue is proportionally thinner and more vulnerable
- Owners often underestimate dental disease risk in small dogs

*Recommendation:* Start brushing from puppyhood. Budget for annual professional cleans. Watch for early signs (bad breath, reluctance to chew hard food) from 2–3 years of age.

### Brachycephalic Breeds (High Risk)

**French Bulldogs, Pugs, Bulldogs, Boston Terriers, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels**

Brachycephalic dogs have the same number of teeth as other dogs but compressed, distorted jaws. Teeth are rotated, overlapping, and often retain baby teeth (retained deciduous teeth) that should have been extracted. The result: abnormally tight pockets between teeth that trap bacteria.

*Recommendation:* Discuss dental care with your vet at your puppy's first vaccination appointment. Retained puppy teeth need vet extraction — never try to remove at home. Professional cleaning typically needed from 2–3 years of age.

### Large and Giant Breeds (Moderate Risk)

**German Shepherds, Labrador Retrievers, Golden Retrievers, Great Danes, Boxers**

Better jaw architecture means lower dental disease risk than small breeds — but not zero. Large breeds are more prone to **tooth fractures** (from playing with hard objects), which can lead to pulp exposure and abscess.

*Recommendation:* Use the thumbnail test on all chew toys. Check for fractured teeth (especially the carnassial) regularly. Annual dental exams from 4 years of age.

### Greyhounds and Sighthounds

Particularly prone to severe periodontal disease despite their size. Studies in racing Greyhounds have found near-universal periodontal disease. Believed to be genetic — their immune response to oral bacteria is different.

*Recommendation:* Assume they need more dental care, not less. Start brushing early, clean professionally as needed.

## Spotting Dental Problems: When to See the Vet

Dogs rarely show obvious signs of dental pain. Watch for:

**Signs that warrant a vet appointment:**

- ☐ Bad breath (halitosis) — especially if noticeably worse than before
- ☐ Yellow, brown, or grey buildup on teeth (tartar)
- ☐ Red, swollen, or bleeding gum line
- ☐ Pawing at the mouth or face rubbing
- ☐ Chewing on one side, or reluctance to chew hard food/toys
- ☐ Dropping food from the mouth
- ☐ Visible swelling on the face, especially below the eye (this can indicate an abscess of the carnassial tooth)
- ☐ Blood in the water bowl or food
- ☐ Loose or missing teeth (in an adult dog)
- ☐ Increased drooling
- ☐ Change in appetite or weight loss

**A note on bad breath:** Some doggy breath is normal. Breath that smells rotting, strongly foul, or like nail varnish remover (which can indicate diabetic ketoacidosis) needs investigation.

## Puppy Dental Care: Getting It Right From the Start

**Puppy teeth:** Pups are born toothless. Baby (deciduous) teeth begin erupting at 3–4 weeks. By 6–8 weeks, they have a full set of 28 baby teeth.

**Adult teeth:** Between 3–7 months, adult teeth erupt and push out baby teeth. Most dogs have their full complement of 42 adult teeth by 7 months.

**Teething:** During this period, expect increased chewing. Provide safe chews (not too hard) and chilled toys to soothe sore gums. Cold carrot is a vet-recommended natural option.

**Retained baby teeth:** If a baby tooth doesn't fall out when the adult tooth comes in, two teeth occupy the same space. Food packs between them, accelerating disease. Your vet will check at puppy vaccination appointments and recommend extraction under anaesthetic if needed.

**Starting brushing:** Introduce handling of the mouth from day one. Let your puppy chew and lick your fingers near their teeth. Begin the 4-week introduction to brushing from 8 weeks. If you start when they're young, most dogs tolerate — or even enjoy — brushing as adults.

## Senior Dog Dental Care (7 Years+)

Dental disease accumulates over a lifetime. Senior dogs need more, not less, dental attention.

**What changes with age:**
- Bone density decreases, meaning extraction carries higher fracture risk
- Pre-anaesthetic bloods become more important (checking kidney and liver function)
- Anaesthetic protocols are adjusted for older patients — a good vet will discuss this
- Underlying conditions (Cushing's, diabetes, hypothyroidism) can worsen dental disease

**The anaesthetic risk question:** Many owners avoid dental treatment in older dogs due to anaesthetic concerns. The BVDA's position: untreated dental disease is almost always a greater risk to health and quality of life than a well-managed anaesthetic. Pre-anaesthetic screening significantly reduces risk. Discuss honestly with your vet — but don't let dental disease go untreated out of fear.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My dog's breath smells terrible. Is that normal?**
Some doggy breath is normal — but severely bad breath (halitosis) usually indicates dental disease. It's caused by bacterial activity below the gum line. Don't mask it with breath mints; get your vet to check what's causing it.

**Can I use human toothpaste?**
No. Human toothpaste contains fluoride, which is toxic to dogs (they swallow rather than spit). Some also contain xylitol, which is severely toxic. Always use dog-specific toothpaste.

**My dog has already lost some teeth. Is it too late to start caring for the rest?**
Never too late. Every remaining tooth matters. Even with severe dental disease history, good home care significantly slows further progression. Book a vet dental check-up and start brushing.

**How do I know if my dog has dental pain?**
Dogs rarely vocalise dental pain. Instead: watch for reluctance to eat hard food, chewing on one side, dropping food, pawing at the mouth, or behavioural changes like irritability. Many dogs with severe dental disease show no obvious signs — which is why regular vet checks matter.

**What's the best dental product if my dog won't let me brush?**
Start with enzymatic gel (e.g., Logic Oral Hygiene Gel) — it can be applied with a finger and begins working chemically. Add VOHC-approved dental chews and a water additive. Then slowly work toward introducing a toothbrush using the 4-week programme. No product replaces brushing, but combination approaches help.

**Do raw feeders need to worry about dental health?**
Raw fed dogs typically have lower tartar levels — the mechanical action of chewing raw meat and edible bones (not weight-bearing bones) helps. But it doesn't eliminate dental disease. Raw-fed dogs still need regular dental checks and should have their mouths examined for any issues.

## Key Takeaway

Dental disease isn't inevitable. Start brushing early, use VOHC-approved products, get regular vet dental checks, and don't be scared of professional cleaning when it's needed. Your dog can't tell you their mouth hurts — but they'll show you in other ways. The best thing you can do is not wait until they do.
