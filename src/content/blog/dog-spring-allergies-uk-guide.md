## Introduction

The clocks go forward, the blossom comes out, and your dog starts scratching themselves raw. If this sounds familiar, you're not imagining it — seasonal allergies in dogs are real, increasingly common, and often misunderstood.

Unlike humans, whose hay fever usually means sneezing and watery eyes, dogs express pollen allergy almost entirely through their skin. The result: constant itching, red inflamed paws, recurrent ear infections, and a dog who can't get comfortable no matter what you do.

The good news: spring allergies are very treatable, and with the right management plan, most dogs can get through pollen season in reasonable comfort. This guide walks you through everything you need to know — from identifying whether your dog actually has seasonal allergies (versus food intolerance or something else entirely), to the full range of treatments from paw wipes to prescription medications.

## What Are Dog Spring Allergies?

### The Difference Between Atopy and Other Allergies

Seasonal allergies in dogs fall under the umbrella of **canine atopic dermatitis (atopy)** — an inherited hypersensitivity to environmental allergens. When the immune system of a predisposed dog encounters an airborne allergen (grass pollen, tree pollen, dust mites, mould spores), it mounts an inflammatory response far out of proportion to the actual threat.

This is distinct from:

| Allergy Type | Trigger | Seasonal? | Primary Symptoms |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Atopy (environmental)** | Pollen, dust, mould | Yes — peak spring/autumn | Itching, skin inflammation, ear infections |
| **Food allergy/intolerance** | Protein in diet | No — year-round | GI issues AND skin, often facial |
| **Contact dermatitis** | Grass, plants, carpet | Sometimes | Localised rash where skin met trigger |
| **Flea allergy dermatitis** | Flea saliva | Spring–autumn peak | Rump/tail base scratching |

> **Key point:** Dogs can (and often do) have more than one allergy type simultaneously. If your dog is itchy year-round but significantly worse in spring, they may have both environmental AND food allergies. Separating these out matters for treatment.

### Why Is Spring Particularly Bad?

In the UK, airborne allergen levels follow a predictable calendar:

| Allergen | Peak UK Season | Worst Months |
|---|---|---|
| Tree pollen (birch, oak, ash) | Early spring | March–May |
| Grass pollen | Late spring–summer | May–July |
| Weed pollen (nettle, plantain) | Summer–autumn | June–September |
| Mould spores | Autumn/damp | August–November |
| House dust mites | Year-round | Peaks Oct–Jan |

Many atopic dogs are sensitised to **multiple allergens**, which is why they may start showing symptoms in March (tree pollen), peak in May–June (grass), and have a secondary flare in autumn (moulds). It can feel relentless.

## Recognising Seasonal Allergies in Your Dog

### The Classic Signs

Dogs do not usually sneeze and develop watery eyes from pollen — that's a human response. Instead, watch for:

**Skin and coat:**
- Constant scratching, especially at face, ears, armpits, groin, and between toes
- Rubbing face on carpet or furniture
- Reddened, inflamed skin (particularly on belly and inner legs)
- Hot spots — localised areas of moist, infected skin from overgrooming
- Hair loss or thinning coat in affected areas
- A musty or yeasty skin odour (sign of secondary yeast overgrowth)

**Paws:**
- Licking and chewing paws obsessively (often worse after walks)
- Red-brown saliva staining between toes
- Swollen, inflamed paw pads or interdigital skin

**Ears:**
- Recurrent ear infections (bacteria or yeast) — in atopic dogs, ears are often the first clinical sign
- Head shaking, scratching at ears
- Dark discharge, odour inside ear

**Eyes:**
- Mild redness or discharge (less common than in humans but can occur)

### What Seasonal Allergies Do NOT Usually Cause in Dogs

- Vomiting or diarrhoea (more typical of food allergy)
- Sudden facial swelling (anaphylaxis — veterinary emergency)
- Limping without skin involvement (consider orthopaedic causes)

### The Seasonality Test

If your dog's symptoms:
- Start in March–April and improve by October → strongly suggestive of pollen allergy
- Persist year-round, even in winter → consider house dust mite, food allergy, or both
- Fluctuate regardless of season → elimination diet is the next step

> **The Vet's Rule of Thumb:** If a dog has had three or more ear infections in a year, and there's skin itching involved, atopic dermatitis should be at the top of the differential list. — *BSAVA Small Animal Dermatology Guide*

## Breeds Most Commonly Affected

Atopic dermatitis has a strong hereditary component. The following breeds are significantly over-represented:

| Risk Level | Breeds |
|---|---|
| 🔴 Very High | West Highland White Terrier, Boxer, French Bulldog, English Bulldog, Dalmatian, Golden Retriever |
| 🟠 High | Labrador Retriever, Cocker Spaniel, German Shepherd, Poodle, Bichon Frisé, Staffordshire Bull Terrier |
| 🟡 Moderate | Border Collie, Cavalier King Charles Spaniel, Shih Tzu, Lhasa Apso |
| 🟢 Lower risk | Greyhound, Whippet, Husky, working farm breeds |

> **Note:** Mixed breeds can absolutely develop atopy — these are population-level tendencies, not guarantees.

## Diagnosing Seasonal Allergies

### The Diagnostic Journey

Getting a confirmed diagnosis takes time. There's no single test that says "your dog has grass pollen allergy" — the diagnosis is built from:

**Step 1: Rule out other causes**
Your vet will first exclude:
- Flea allergy dermatitis (even if you haven't seen fleas — they're good at hiding)
- Sarcoptic mange (mites — highly itchy, contagious)
- Ringworm (fungal)
- Food allergy (requires dietary elimination trial)

**Step 2: Clinical history**
Age of onset matters: most dogs with atopy first develop symptoms between **6 months and 3 years**. If a 7-year-old dog suddenly becomes itchy, other causes are more likely.

**Step 3: Intradermal skin testing or serum allergy tests**

| Test Type | What It Does | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intradermal testing** | Injects small amounts of allergens into shaved skin patch, reads wheal size | Gold standard for identifying specific allergens | Requires dermatologist, sedation, expensive £400–800 |
| **Serum (blood) allergy testing** | Measures IgE antibodies in blood | Available via most vets, no sedation | Less specific; some false positives |

> **Important:** Allergy testing is primarily used to design **allergen-specific immunotherapy (ASIT)** — the dog version of allergy shots. It's not required to begin symptomatic treatment.

## Treatment Options: From Mild to Severe

### Tier 1: Environmental Management (All Dogs)

Before reaching for medications, optimise your dog's environment:

**After every walk:**
- Wipe paws with a damp cloth or dog-safe paw wipes — removes surface pollen before it's licked off
- Consider a foot bath with a dog-safe chlorhexidine shampoo if severely affected
- Wipe down belly and legs (grass pollen lands on contact surfaces too)

**At home:**
- Vacuum frequently with a HEPA filter vacuum — reduces indoor pollen and dust mite load
- Wash dog bedding weekly at 60°C
- Keep dogs indoors during high pollen periods (early morning, late afternoon — when counts are highest)
- Check the Met Office pollen forecast: *metoffice.gov.uk/weather/warnings-and-advice/seasonal-advice/pollen-forecast*
- Avoid mowing the lawn yourself, or keep dogs indoors for 2 hours after mowing

**Bathing:**
Regular bathing with a veterinary-formulated shampoo (every 1–2 weeks during peak season) physically removes allergens from the coat and restores skin barrier function. Look for shampoos containing:
- Colloidal oatmeal — soothing, anti-inflammatory
- Chlorhexidine — antibacterial/antifungal (for dogs with secondary infections)
- Ceramides — restore the skin barrier (deficient in atopic dogs)

### Tier 2: Supplements

**Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil)**
The most evidence-supported supplement for atopy. EPA and DHA reduce inflammatory cytokine production in the skin. Studies show significant reductions in itch scores when dosed correctly.

- Dose: ~50–75mg EPA/DHA per kg bodyweight per day
- Source: Marine (fish or krill) oil — NOT flaxseed, which dogs convert poorly
- Takes 6–8 weeks to see benefit — start before pollen season

**Quercetin**

### Tier 3: Antihistamines

Here's the honest truth: **antihistamines work poorly for atopic dogs**. Studies show around 30% of dogs respond meaningfully. However, they're cheap, safe, and worth trying before moving to prescription options.

**Antihistamines safe for dogs in the UK:**

| Drug | Brand (human) | Safe for dogs? | Typical dog dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Chlorphenamine** | Piriton 4mg | ✅ Yes | 4mg per dog (not per kg) up to 3×/day | Causes sedation — can be useful |
| **Cetirizine** | Zirtek 10mg | ✅ Yes | 1mg/kg once daily | Less sedating than Piriton |
| **Loratadine** | Claritin | ✅ Yes | 0.5mg/kg once daily | Non-sedating |
| **Diphenhydramine** | Benadryl | ⚠️ Use with caution | 1mg/kg | Some Benadryl products contain xylitol — ALWAYS check label |
| **Acrivastine** | Some Benadryl variants | ❌ Toxic to dogs | — | Do not use |

> ⚠️ **Always confirm dosing with your vet before giving human antihistamines to your dog.** Xylitol in some formulations is potentially fatal. Check every ingredient.

### Tier 4: Prescription Medications

When environmental management and OTC options aren't enough, modern prescription drugs have transformed atopy management.

**Apoquel (oclacitinib)**
- **How it works:** Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitor — blocks specific inflammatory signalling pathways that cause itch
- **Effectiveness:** Clinically proven; 74% of dogs show significant improvement within 24 hours (*Zoetis clinical data*)
- **Safety:** Generally well tolerated; not for dogs under 12 months or breeding animals; avoid in dogs with cancer history
- **Cost:** ~£2–4 per tablet; daily ongoing treatment during allergy season
- **Availability:** Prescription only — requires vet consultation

**Cytopoint (lokivetmab)**
- **How it works:** Monoclonal antibody that targets and neutralises IL-31 — the key cytokine responsible for itch in atopic dermatitis
- **Effectiveness:** Works in ~80% of dogs; onset within 24 hours; lasts 4–8 weeks per injection
- **Safety:** Excellent — as a biological rather than a drug, has minimal systemic side effects; safe in puppies 12 weeks+, pregnant dogs, dogs on other medications
- **Cost:** ~£60–120 per injection depending on dog size and practice location
- **Availability:** Administered by vet or vet nurse; frequency adjusted to individual dog

**Steroids (prednisolone, methylprednisolone)**
- **How they work:** Suppress the entire immune response (broad)
- **Effectiveness:** Highly effective at reducing itch and inflammation quickly
- **Safety:** Short courses are generally safe; long-term use has significant side effects (Cushing's-like syndrome, muscle wasting, increased infection risk, diabetes)
- **When used:** Short-term rescue during severe flares; bridge while immunotherapy is being developed
- **Cost:** Very cheap — often <£10 for a short course

**Atopica (ciclosporin)**
- **How it works:** Calcineurin inhibitor — moderates T-cell activity
- **Effectiveness:** Comparable to steroids in long-term control without steroid side effects
- **Safety:** Can cause GI upset, especially early; requires blood monitoring
- **Onset:** Slower — 4–6 weeks to see full effect
- **Cost:** ~£50–100/month depending on dog size

### Tier 5: Allergen-Specific Immunotherapy (ASIT)

The only treatment that changes the underlying allergy rather than managing symptoms. Also called hyposensitisation or "allergy shots."

**How it works:**
After intradermal or blood allergy testing identifies the specific allergens your dog reacts to, a customised vaccine is formulated. Small, gradually increasing doses are injected subcutaneously (under the skin). Over 6–12 months, the immune system "learns" to tolerate the allergen.

**Effectiveness:**
- ~50–75% of dogs show significant improvement
- Takes patience — full effect often takes 9–12 months
- Once successful, ongoing maintenance doses (monthly or less frequent) may be all that's needed long-term

**Cost:**
- Initial testing (dermatologist): £400–800
- Immunotherapy course: £100–200 for formulation, ongoing maintenance variable

**Is it worth it?**
If your dog has severe atopy requiring daily Apoquel or quarterly Cytopoint injections, immunotherapy has the potential to reduce or replace ongoing drug costs while addressing the root cause. For young dogs likely to need lifelong management, it's worth discussing with a veterinary dermatologist.

## Managing Secondary Infections

Atopic dogs scratch. Scratching breaks the skin barrier. Broken skin lets bacteria and yeast in. **Secondary infections are one of the most underappreciated parts of atopic dermatitis** — they significantly worsen itch and can make a mild allergy feel catastrophic.

### Signs of Bacterial Skin Infection (Pyoderma)
- Pustules (small raised spots), crusts
- Red, inflamed patches with yellowish discharge
- Strong odour
- Hot spots — moist, hairless, oozing areas

### Signs of Yeast (Malassezia) Overgrowth
- Greasy or waxy skin
- Dark brown debris in skin folds or ears
- Musty, cheesy odour
- Brown staining between toes (from saliva AND from yeast)

### Treatment
Infections require specific treatment (antibiotics for bacterial, antifungal for yeast) — they do not resolve with allergy drugs alone. Your vet will use skin cytology (a cotton swab from the skin examined under a microscope) to identify which organism is present before prescribing.

## Breed-Specific Advice

### French Bulldogs and Brachycephalic Breeds
Frenchies are among the most commonly affected breeds. The skin folds (facial folds, tail pocket, vulvar fold, body folds) create warm, moist environments ideal for yeast and bacterial overgrowth — fold dermatitis often co-exists with atopy and needs to be managed separately with daily fold cleaning. Use a dog-safe wipe or dilute chlorhexidine solution.

### West Highland White Terriers
Westies so commonly develop atopy that "Westie itch" is a recognised term in veterinary circles. Early ASIT is worth discussing at first diagnosis given the typical severity and lifelong trajectory.

### Labrador and Golden Retrievers
Often develop atopy between 1 and 3 years. Ear infections are frequently the presenting complaint before skin involvement is noticed. Regular ear cleaning during pollen season is important.

### Staffordshire Bull Terriers
Highly predisposed. Skin is often thin and easily traumatised by scratching. Hot spots are common. Regular baths with barrier-restoring shampoo are beneficial from early spring.

## When to See a Veterinary Dermatologist

Your GP vet is well-equipped to start atopy management, but consider a dermatology referral if:

- Symptoms are severe or unresponsive to first-line treatments
- Multiple medications are being cycled without adequate control
- You want allergen-specific immunotherapy (ASIT)
- There's uncertainty about whether it's atopy, food allergy, or both
- Recurrent infections are requiring frequent courses of antibiotics

**Finding a dermatologist in the UK:**
- [RCVS Find a Specialist](https://www.rcvs.org.uk/find-a-registrant/) — search "dermatology"
- Many referral centres offer video consultations for ongoing management

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I give my dog Piriton for itching?**
A: Yes — chlorphenamine (Piriton 4mg tablets) is generally safe for dogs at 4mg per dog (not per kg) up to three times daily. However, always consult your vet first and never use products that contain xylitol. Antihistamines work in about 30% of atopic dogs, so manage expectations.

**Q: My dog is only itchy in spring — does that definitely mean pollen?**
A: It's strongly suggestive, but not definitive. Spring also brings increased flea activity (check for flea dirt), more time outdoors on grass (possible contact dermatitis), and more mould after winter damp. Your vet can help distinguish between causes.

**Q: Is there a cure for dog seasonal allergies?**
A: Allergen-specific immunotherapy (ASIT) is the closest thing — it modifies the immune response rather than just managing symptoms. It works in around 50–75% of dogs and can significantly reduce or eliminate the need for ongoing medication. Other treatments manage the condition effectively but don't address the underlying cause.

**Q: How much does seasonal allergy treatment cost per year?**
A: It varies considerably:
- Antihistamines + omega-3: ~£10–30/month
- Apoquel (daily): ~£60–120/month depending on dog size
- Cytopoint: £60–120 per injection, every 4–8 weeks
- ASIT (after initial testing): £100–200 for vaccine formulation, then maintenance doses

**Q: My dog scratches all year, not just spring. Could it still be pollen?**
A: Possibly — some dogs are sensitised to so many allergens (pollens, dust mites, storage mites, moulds) that they never have a truly symptom-free period. Year-round scratching is more likely to involve house dust mites, food components, or a combination. A proper diagnostic workup is valuable if symptoms are year-round and severe.

**Q: Are some walks worse than others for allergic dogs?**
A: Yes. Pollen counts are highest in the early morning (6–10am) and on warm, dry, breezy days. Walking in the rain or on wet days tends to be much better for atopic dogs — pollen gets weighed down. Open grassland is more problematic than sealed surfaces, woods, or urban streets.

## Summary: Managing Your Dog's Spring Allergies

| Severity | Recommended Approach |
|---|---|
| **Mild** | Paw wipes, regular bathing, omega-3 supplements, antihistamines, reduce outdoor exposure during peak hours |
| **Moderate** | All of the above + vet consultation, consider Apoquel or Cytopoint, treat any secondary infections |
| **Severe** | Veterinary dermatologist referral, ASIT workup, long-term management plan, consider immunotherapy |

Spring allergies are one of the most manageable chronic conditions in dogs — the range of effective treatments available in 2027 is genuinely excellent. The key is recognising the signs early, starting environmental management before pollen peaks, and working with your vet to find the right treatment tier for your dog's individual severity.
