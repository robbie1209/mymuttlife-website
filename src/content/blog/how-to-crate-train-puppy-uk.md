# How to Crate Train Your Puppy: The Complete UK Guide (2026)

*Everything you need to know about crate training — from choosing the right crate to sleeping through the night, backed by UK vet and behaviourist advice.*

---

**Last updated:** March 2026 | **Reading time:** ~12 minutes

Crate training gets a bad reputation in the UK. Mention it in a park and you'll get at least one disapproving look. But done properly, crate training isn't about locking your puppy away — it's about giving them their own safe space. A den they choose to go to, not one they're forced into.

The RSPCA, Dogs Trust, PDSA, and Blue Cross all support crate training when done correctly. The key word is *correctly*. Get it wrong and you'll create anxiety, not comfort.

This guide walks you through everything: choosing the right crate, the step-by-step training process, night-time routines, common mistakes, and what to do when things don't go to plan.

## What Is Crate Training (And What It Isn't) {#what-is-crate-training}

Crate training teaches your puppy to see their crate as a positive, safe space — like a bedroom. Dogs are naturally den animals. In the wild, they seek out enclosed, secure spaces to rest. A crate taps into that instinct.

**What crate training IS:**
- A safe den your puppy voluntarily retreats to
- A tool for house training (puppies rarely soil where they sleep)
- A secure space for car travel, vet visits, and recovery from surgery
- Temporary scaffolding — most dogs graduate to free roaming

**What crate training is NOT:**
- A punishment or time-out zone
- A babysitter (never leave a puppy crated for hours)
- A permanent living arrangement
- A substitute for exercise, enrichment, or companionship

> **UK welfare law:** The Animal Welfare Act 2006 requires that dogs have their needs met, including a suitable environment. A crate used excessively or punitively could breach this. The RSPCA advises that puppies should not be crated for more than 2 hours during the day and never as punishment.

## Benefits of Crate Training {#benefits}

### 1. Faster House Training
Puppies instinctively avoid soiling their sleeping area. A correctly sized crate (big enough to stand, turn, and lie down — not bigger) uses this instinct to your advantage. Combined with a consistent toilet schedule, most puppies house-train weeks faster with crate support.

### 2. Safety When You Can't Supervise
Puppies chew. Everything. Electrical cables, shoes, skirting boards, the kitchen table leg. A crate keeps them safe during the brief moments you can't watch them — cooking, showering, answering the door.

### 3. Travel Safety
The Highway Code recommends dogs be suitably restrained in vehicles. A travel crate in the boot is the safest option and familiar to a crate-trained dog. The Dogs Trust reports that an unrestrained 30kg dog in a 30mph crash generates a force equivalent to a small rhino.

### 4. Vet and Emergency Preparedness
If your dog needs cage rest after surgery (cruciate ligament repair, spinal issues), a dog that's never been crate-trained will find enforced rest extremely stressful on top of their recovery. Dogs Trust recommend crate training all puppies partly for this reason.

### 5. A Retreat from Chaos
New baby? House party? Fireworks? A crate gives your dog somewhere to go where they feel genuinely safe. This is particularly valuable when [introducing your dog to a new baby](/how-to-introduce-dog-to-new-baby-uk-guide).

### Where to Put the Crate

- **Daytime:** In a room where the family spends time — kitchen or living room. Your puppy shouldn't feel isolated.
- **Night-time:** In your bedroom initially (yes, really — more on this below).
- **Away from:** Radiators, direct sunlight, draughts, and washing machines/dryers (the noise can be unsettling).

## Step-by-Step Crate Training Plan {#step-by-step}

This plan follows the PDSA and Dogs Trust recommended approach. Every puppy is different — some breeze through in a week, others need a month. **Never rush it.**

### Day 1–3: Introduction (Crate = Good Things)

**Goal:** Puppy voluntarily enters the crate. Door stays OPEN.

1. Place the crate with the door open (or removed entirely). Add bedding and a blanket over the top.
2. Drop treats around the crate, then just inside the entrance, then further in.
3. When your puppy goes in, praise calmly. Don't squeal with excitement — keep it cool.
4. Feed meals inside the crate with the door open. Place the bowl near the front at first, then gradually further back.
5. Drop a stuffed Kong inside and let them discover it.
6. **Do NOT close the door yet.** Seriously. Patience here saves weeks later.

> **PDSA tip:** If your puppy won't go near the crate, try placing a worn t-shirt of yours inside. Your scent makes it less intimidating.

### Day 3–5: Brief Closures

**Goal:** Puppy relaxed with door closed briefly.

1. While your puppy is eating or working on a Kong inside, gently push the door closed. Don't latch it.
2. Stay right next to the crate. Chat normally, don't make a fuss.
3. Open the door before they finish eating — you want to open it before they want out.
4. Gradually extend the time the door is closed: 30 seconds → 1 minute → 3 minutes → 5 minutes.
5. If they whine, wait for a pause in whining (even 2 seconds), then open the door. Never open it *while* they're whining — this teaches "whining = door opens."

### Day 5–10: Building Duration

**Goal:** 15–30 minutes in the crate with door closed, you in the room.

1. Give your puppy a stuffed Kong, close the door, and sit nearby doing something normal (reading, watching TV, working on your laptop).
2. Occasionally drop a treat through the bars to reinforce that crate = rewards.
3. Start moving around the room while they're crated. Pop to the kitchen. Come back.
4. Build up to 15 minutes, then 20, then 30.
5. Always let them out when they're calm, not when they're fussing.

### Day 10–14: Short Absences

**Goal:** Puppy calm in crate while you leave the room for up to 30 minutes.

1. With your puppy settled in the crate with a Kong, leave the room briefly (30 seconds). Return calmly — no big reunion.
2. Extend: 1 minute, 3 minutes, 5 minutes, 10 minutes, 20 minutes.
3. Vary it — don't always increase. Do 10 mins, then 5, then 15, then 3. Keeps them guessing (in a good way).
4. When you return, wait for calm before opening the door. No excited greetings at the crate.

### Day 14+: Real-World Use

**Goal:** Puppy comfortable being crated for up to 1–2 hours during the day, and overnight.

1. Start leaving the house briefly while they're crated. Pop to the bins. Get the post. Walk to the shop.
2. Leave a radio or TV on for background noise.
3. Build up gradually. Most adult dogs can manage 3–4 hours, but puppies under 6 months should not exceed 2 hours.
4. Always exercise and toilet your puppy before crating.

### Maximum Crate Times by Age

| Age | Max Daytime Crate Time | Overnight (with toilet break) |
|-----|----------------------|------------------------------|
| 8–10 weeks | 30–60 minutes | 3–4 hours then toilet break |
| 10–12 weeks | 1–2 hours | 4–5 hours then toilet break |
| 3–4 months | 2–3 hours | 5–6 hours then toilet break |
| 5–6 months | 3–4 hours | 6–7 hours |
| 6+ months | 4 hours max | 7–8 hours |

> **Important:** These are maximums, not targets. Less is better. A crate supplements your puppy's life — it shouldn't dominate it.

## The Night-Time Routine {#night-time}

The first few nights are the hardest. Your puppy has just left their mum and siblings. They're in a strange place. They're going to cry. Here's how to handle it:

### The Setup

1. **Move the crate into your bedroom** for the first 1–2 weeks. This isn't spoiling them — it's pragmatic. They can hear you breathing, smell you, and feel less alone. You'll also hear when they need the toilet.
2. Last toilet trip right before bed. Take them outside, wait, praise when they go.
3. Pop a warm (not hot) water bottle wrapped in a towel in the crate — simulates sibling warmth.
4. A ticking clock nearby can mimic a heartbeat. Or try a [Snuggle Puppy](https://amzn.to/example) (£25–£35) — a soft toy with a heat pack and heartbeat simulator. Sounds gimmicky, works brilliantly.

### When They Cry

- **First 2–3 nights:** Expect crying. Place your hand against the crate so they can smell you. Whisper reassuringly. Don't take them out unless you think they need the toilet.
- **Toilet or distress?** If they've been settled and suddenly start crying after a few hours, they probably need the toilet. Take them out silently, straight to the garden, wait, praise, straight back in. No play, no fuss, lights off.
- **After 3–5 nights:** Most puppies settle into the routine. The crying reduces dramatically.
- **By week 2–3:** Gradually move the crate toward its permanent daytime position. Move it a foot or two each night.

### Common Night-Time Questions

**Q: Should I set an alarm for toilet breaks?**
Yes, for puppies under 12 weeks. Set it for 3–4 hours after bedtime. Better to wake them before they cry than to have them cry because they're desperate.

**Q: My puppy sleeps fine in my bed — do I need the crate?**
That's your choice, but the crate teaches independence that pays off long-term. Dogs that only sleep in beds can develop [separation anxiety](/separation-anxiety-dogs-complete-uk-guide) when circumstances change.

**Q: They fell asleep on the sofa — should I move them to the crate?**
Gently, yes. Carry them to the crate while drowsy. They'll learn the crate is where sleep happens.

## Crate Training an Adult Dog {#adult-dogs}

Missed the puppy window? No problem. Adult dogs can absolutely learn to love a crate — it just takes longer and more patience, especially if they've had negative crate experiences (rescue dogs, ex-breeding dogs).

### Key Differences

- **Go slower.** Double the time at each stage compared to the puppy plan.
- **Never force them in.** Drop treats, use Kongs, be patient. They may take days to voluntarily enter.
- **Watch for trauma signs.** Excessive drooling, panting, or destructive behaviour when crated suggests a previous negative association. Consult an ABTC-registered behaviourist.
- **Consider the reason.** Are you crate training for travel? Recovery? A new baby? Understanding the end goal helps you train specifically for that scenario.

### Rescue Dogs

Many rescue organisations (Dogs Trust, Battersea, Blue Cross) will tell you about a dog's crate history during the adoption process. Ask specifically:
- Were they crated in their previous home?
- Were they crated at the shelter?
- Any known reactions to crates or confinement?

If a rescue dog has severe crate anxiety, a playpen or puppy-proofed room might be a better alternative.

## Breed-Specific Considerations {#breed-specific}

Not all breeds respond to crate training equally. Here's what to know:

| Breed Type | Crate Notes | Tips |
|------------|------------|------|
| **Labradors & Retrievers** | Generally take to crates well. Food-motivated = easy to lure in. | Use Kong stuffing as primary reward. Watch for chewing the crate itself (boredom). |
| **Spaniels** | Can be velcro dogs — may whine more initially. | Bedroom crate essential early on. Don't rush the separation stages. |
| **Terriers** | Independent but can be destructive chewers. | Wire crates over fabric. Ensure plenty of enrichment toys inside. |
| **Collies & Shepherds** | Intelligent, need mental stimulation. May find crating boring. | Puzzle toys inside. Keep sessions shorter. Pair crating with physical exercise. |
| **Bulldogs & Brachycephalic** | Overheat easily. | Wire crates only (ventilation). Never cover fully. Keep crate in cool area. |
| **Sighthounds (Greyhounds, Whippets)** | Often ex-racing dogs already crate trained. | May actually prefer the crate. Ensure big enough for their long legs. |
| **Giant Breeds (Great Danes, Newfies)** | Need enormous crates. May outgrow standard sizes. | Custom or XL crates. Consider furniture-style crates that double as tables. |
| **Toy Breeds (Chihuahua, Pom)** | Can feel swamped by standard crates. | Smaller crates. Line of sight to owner is important. |

## Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them) {#common-mistakes}

### ❌ Mistake 1: Using the Crate as Punishment
"Go to your crate!" after they've chewed your shoe. This destroys the positive association instantly. The crate should never be associated with your anger or their misbehaviour.

**Fix:** If they've done something wrong, redirect them. The crate is for rest, safety, and choice — not consequences.

### ❌ Mistake 2: Too Much, Too Fast
Locking the door on day one because you've got a Zoom call in 10 minutes. Your puppy isn't ready. You'll create a negative experience that sets training back by weeks.

**Fix:** Follow the timeline. If you need to confine your puppy before they're crate-ready, use a playpen instead.

### ❌ Mistake 3: Letting Them Out When They Cry
This is the hardest one. Your puppy cries, your heart breaks, you open the door. You've just taught them that crying = freedom.

**Fix:** Wait for even a 2-second pause in crying, then open the door. Mark the quiet moment, not the noisy one. If they've been crying for more than 10 minutes continuously, you've pushed too far too fast — go back a stage.

### ❌ Mistake 4: Crating for Too Long
A puppy crated for 8 hours while you're at work is not crate-trained — they're confined. This causes distress, can lead to toileting in the crate (breaking the instinct you're relying on), and may create long-term anxiety.

**Fix:** If you work full-time, arrange puppy daycare, a dog walker, or come home at lunch. Puppies under 6 months should not be crated for more than 2 hours during the day.

### ❌ Mistake 5: Making Departures and Returns a Big Deal
"Oh my GOODNESS, Mummy's HOME! Did you MISS me? You're SO BRAVE!" This creates arousal around the crate and makes your departure more stressful by contrast.

**Fix:** Leave and return calmly. Boring is good. Let them out, give a quiet "good dog," and carry on with life.

### ❌ Mistake 6: Buying the Wrong Size
A crate that's too big defeats the house-training benefit (they'll toilet in one corner and sleep in the other). Too small is uncomfortable and unfair.

**Fix:** Use a crate with a divider. Adjust it as they grow. The right space is stand-turn-lie-down — no more.

## When Crate Training Isn't Working {#when-its-not-working}

Sometimes, despite doing everything right, your puppy just doesn't take to the crate. Signs it's not working:

- Persistent distress (not just grumbling — actual panic) after 2+ weeks of patient training
- Toileting in the crate regularly despite correct sizing and frequent breaks
- Self-harm: scratching at the door until paws bleed, bending wire bars, breaking teeth
- Excessive drooling, panting, or trembling

### What to Do

1. **Go back to basics.** Return to Day 1. Door open, treats scattered, no pressure.
2. **Try a different crate type.** Some dogs prefer enclosed plastic crates; others want the visibility of wire.
3. **Rule out medical issues.** Urinary tract infections can cause frequent toileting. Pain can cause restlessness. See your vet.
4. **Consider alternatives:**
   - **Playpen:** More space, still contained. Good for puppies who panic in small spaces.
   - **Puppy-proofed room:** Baby gates across a kitchen or utility room.
   - **Exercise pen + crate:** Connect an exercise pen to an open crate, giving them the choice to enter.
5. **Get professional help.** An ABTC-registered clinical animal behaviourist can assess whether there's an underlying issue. Find one at [abtc.org.uk](https://abtc.org.uk/). Costs typically £150–£300 for an initial consultation.

## Crate Training and Separation Anxiety {#separation-anxiety}

Crate training and separation anxiety are related but separate issues. A crate-trained dog can still develop separation anxiety, and a dog with separation anxiety may panic more in a crate.

### Key Points

- Crate training done right **reduces the risk** of separation anxiety by building your puppy's confidence to be alone.
- Crate training done wrong **can cause** separation anxiety if the puppy associates the crate with isolation and distress.
- If your dog already has separation anxiety, **do not** start crate training without professional guidance — the confinement can intensify panic.

For a comprehensive guide on this topic, see our article: [Separation Anxiety in Dogs: Complete UK Guide](/separation-anxiety-dogs-complete-uk-guide).

### Preparing for a New Baby

If you're crate training in preparation for a baby arriving, start at least 2–3 months before the due date. You want crate training to be completely established and positive before the upheaval of a new family member. Your dog's crate becomes their calm retreat when things get noisy and chaotic.

## FAQs {#faqs}

### How long does crate training take?
Most puppies are comfortable with the crate within 2–4 weeks. Some confident puppies crack it in a week; nervous or rescue dogs may need 6–8 weeks. The key is never rushing through the stages.

### At what age should I start crate training?
As soon as you bring your puppy home — typically 8 weeks. The earlier you start, the easier it is. But adult dogs can be crate-trained at any age with patience.

### Should I cover the crate at night?
Yes, most dogs settle better with a cover (blanket or fitted cover) that creates a darker, more den-like environment. Leave the front partially open for airflow. Brachycephalic breeds need more ventilation — don't cover fully.

### Can two puppies share a crate?
No. Each puppy needs their own crate. Sharing can cause resource guarding, prevent independent sleeping, and makes house training much harder. If you have two puppies, place the crates next to each other.

### When should I stop using the crate?
Many dogs voluntarily choose their crate throughout their life — it becomes their preferred sleeping spot. Others can graduate to free roaming once reliably house-trained and past the destructive chewing phase (usually 12–18 months). Try leaving the crate open with access to a wider area and see how they handle it.

### Is crate training cruel?
No — when done correctly. Every major UK animal welfare charity (RSPCA, Dogs Trust, PDSA, Blue Cross, Battersea) supports crate training with proper guidelines. What IS cruel is excessive crating (8+ hours), using the crate as punishment, or forcing a distressed dog into one. Done right, most dogs love their crate.

## Summary: Your Crate Training Checklist

- [ ] Choose the right crate size (with divider for growing puppies)
- [ ] Set up with bedding, cover, and treats
- [ ] Days 1–3: Door open, positive associations only
- [ ] Days 3–5: Brief door closures while eating
- [ ] Days 5–10: Build to 30 minutes, door closed, you in room
- [ ] Days 10–14: Short absences, leaving the room
- [ ] Day 14+: Real-world crating, building duration
- [ ] Night-time: Start in bedroom, use warmth and sound
- [ ] Gradually move crate to permanent position
- [ ] Celebrate — your puppy has a safe, happy den! 🎉
