## Introduction

Whether you're a first-time breeder or your dog has had an unexpected mating, dog pregnancy can feel overwhelming. There's a lot to prepare for — and a narrow window to get it right.

This guide covers everything UK dog owners need to know: how to confirm pregnancy, what to expect week by week, how to set up a whelping space, what normal labour looks like, when to call the vet, and how to care for newborn puppies in those critical first days.

*Personal note from the editor: we wrote this guide while our own family was expecting — and it reminded us just how much a little preparation changes everything, for humans and dogs alike.*

## Can My Dog Be Pregnant? Signs to Look For

Dogs can become pregnant from a single mating during their fertile window (the oestrus phase of their heat cycle). Unlike humans, dogs don't show obvious early signs — which is why many owners are caught off guard.

### Early signs (weeks 1–3)
- **Slight nipple enlargement** — one of the earliest physical signs, appearing from around day 21
- **Mild lethargy** — she may seem slightly less energetic than usual
- **Reduced appetite** — some dogs experience a brief period of morning sickness around weeks 3–4
- **Increased affection** — some dogs become clingier; others more withdrawn
- **Clear vaginal discharge** — a small amount of clear mucus can be normal; anything coloured or foul-smelling needs vet attention

### Later signs (weeks 4–9)
- Visible weight gain, especially around the abdomen
- Enlarged, pink nipples and breast tissue development
- Visible puppy movement in the abdomen from around week 7
- Nesting behaviour — rearranging bedding, seeking quiet spaces
- Swollen vulva (may remain enlarged from heat)

**Important:** these signs alone aren't a reliable indicator. Always confirm with a vet.

## How to Confirm Dog Pregnancy

| Method | When | Accuracy |
|--------|------|----------|
| Blood test (relaxin hormone) | From day 22–27 | High — detects the pregnancy hormone produced by embryos |
| Ultrasound | From day 25–28 | Confirms viable foetuses + heartbeats, but can't reliably count puppies |
| X-ray | From day 45+ | Most accurate for counting puppies — important for whelping planning |
| Abdominal palpation (vet only) | Days 28–35 | Moderately accurate; not recommended at home (risk of injury) |

**Our recommendation:** book a vet appointment at day 25–28 for an ultrasound to confirm pregnancy and viability. Follow up with an X-ray around day 50–55 to count puppies — this helps you know when labour is complete.

## How Long Are Dogs Pregnant?

Canine pregnancy lasts approximately **63 days from ovulation** — though this can appear to vary between 57–72 days depending on how the mating date was recorded. Sperm can survive up to 7 days inside the female, so the actual date of conception may be earlier than the mating date.

For more accurate due date prediction, your vet can use **vaginal cytology** or **progesterone testing** to identify ovulation timing precisely — useful if you're planning a litter rather than dealing with an unexpected pregnancy.

## Week-by-Week Dog Pregnancy Guide

### Weeks 1–2: Fertilisation and Implantation
Eggs are fertilised in the fallopian tubes and begin travelling to the uterus. Embryos don't implant until around day 17–21. Physically, your dog looks and acts completely normal. No dietary changes needed yet.

### Week 3: Implantation and Early Development
Embryos implant in the uterine wall. A blood test can now confirm pregnancy. Nipples may begin to enlarge slightly. Some dogs show brief morning sickness — reduced appetite, occasional vomiting. Keep meals small and consistent. Avoid all medications unless prescribed by your vet.

### Week 4: Organs Begin Forming
Foetal organ development is underway. This is the most critical period for avoiding toxins, stress, and certain medications. Your vet can now confirm pregnancy via ultrasound and detect heartbeats. Begin gradually increasing her food intake — premium high-quality puppy food or a food specifically formulated for pregnancy/lactation is ideal.

> **🐾 Affiliate pick:** [Forthglade Complete Puppy Grain-Free](https://www.forthglade.com) — excellent for pregnant and nursing dogs. High protein, no fillers.

### Week 5: Puppies Take Shape
Foetuses are now recognisably dog-shaped. Claws, whiskers, and sex organs form this week. The abdomen begins to visibly enlarge. Increase food intake by around 25% and switch to 3–4 smaller meals per day — the growing puppies compress her stomach.

### Week 6: Rapid Growth
Puppies are growing quickly. Your dog's belly is now clearly visible. Nipples darken and may start producing colostrum (pre-milk). She may begin showing nesting behaviour — looking for quiet, enclosed spaces. Start introducing the whelping box so she becomes comfortable with it.

### Week 7: Preparation Time
You can usually see or feel movement in the abdomen. Fur grows on the puppies. Your dog needs increased calories — she may now require 50–100% more food than her normal intake. Book your X-ray appointment for days 50–55 to count the litter.

> **🐾 Affiliate pick:** [Royal Canin Maxi Starter Mother & Babydog](https://www.royalcanin.com) — specifically formulated for late pregnancy and nursing.

### Week 8: Final Preparations
Puppies are fully formed and beginning to move into birthing position. Your dog may lose her appetite again — this is normal as the puppies take up most of the abdominal space. Prepare your whelping supplies (see below). Start taking her rectal temperature twice daily — a drop from the normal 38.3–39.2°C to below 37.8°C indicates labour within 24 hours.

### Week 9: Labour Approaching
Your dog should now stay in the whelping box as much as possible. Discharge may increase. She'll be restless, off her food, and panting. Labour is imminent.

## Setting Up Your Whelping Box

A whelping box gives your dog a safe, warm, enclosed space to give birth and care for her puppies. Set it up at least two weeks before the due date so she can get used to it.

### Size guide
The box should be large enough for your dog to lie fully stretched out, plus room for the puppies. A rough guide:

| Dog size | Box dimensions |
|----------|---------------|
| Small breed (under 10kg) | 60cm × 60cm |
| Medium breed (10–25kg) | 90cm × 90cm |
| Large breed (25–45kg) | 120cm × 120cm |
| Giant breed (45kg+) | 150cm × 150cm |

### Essential features
- **Pig rails (anti-crush bars):** A low bar around the inside edge prevents the mother accidentally rolling onto a puppy. Critical for survival in the first days.
- **Low entry ramp or step:** High enough to keep puppies in, low enough for mum to get in and out easily after whelping.
- **Removable liner:** Waterproof base with washable, non-slip liner on top.
- **Warmth source:** A heat lamp or heating pad on one side only — puppies need to be able to move away from heat. Target 30–32°C for the first week.

## Signs of Labour: What's Normal, What's Not

### Stage 1 Labour (6–24 hours, occasionally up to 36 hours)
This is the preparatory phase — cervix dilates, uterine contractions begin (but aren't yet visible externally). Your dog will:
- Pace and seem restless
- Pant heavily
- Shiver or tremble
- Refuse food
- Repeatedly go in and out of the whelping box
- Dig and nest obsessively

**This is normal.** Keep the environment calm and quiet. Don't invite spectators. Limit who's in the room to one calm, familiar person.

**Temperature drop:** As noted above, a rectal temperature drop below 37.8°C reliably predicts labour within 12–24 hours. If you've been monitoring temperature, this is your clearest indicator.

### Stage 2 Labour (Active Delivery)
Visible straining begins. The first puppy typically arrives within 1–2 hours of active straining starting. Subsequent puppies arrive every 15–60 minutes, though this varies. A greenish-black discharge before the first puppy is normal — this is the placental fluid.

**What to expect with each puppy:**
1. Visible straining contractions
2. A water sac may appear first (can look like a green/grey balloon)
3. The puppy arrives — usually headfirst, though breech (tail-first) is also normal in dogs
4. Mum breaks the sac and severs the cord by chewing — let her do this if she will
5. She licks the puppy vigorously to stimulate breathing
6. The placenta is delivered — mum may eat it (normal, though limit her to 1–2 to avoid upset stomach)

### Stage 3 Labour (Placental Delivery)
A placenta should be delivered after each puppy. Keep count — a retained placenta can cause serious infection. If the number of placentas doesn't match the number of puppies, call your vet.

## When to Call Your Vet (Emergency Signs)

**Call immediately if:**
- Active straining for more than 30–45 minutes with no puppy delivered
- More than 4 hours between puppies when you know more are expected (from the X-ray count)
- Greenish-black discharge without a puppy following within 2–4 hours
- A puppy has been partially delivered and is stuck for more than 10 minutes
- Your dog becomes distressed, exhausted, or loses consciousness
- Bright red haemorrhage (a small amount of blood is normal; gushing is not)
- Any retained placenta not passed within 4–6 hours of the last birth

**UK emergency vet contacts:**
- Your own vet's out-of-hours number (always check this BEFORE the due date)
- [Vets Now](https://www.vets-now.com) — 24/7 emergency clinics across the UK
- [BlueCross Animal Hospital](https://www.bluecross.org.uk) — emergency services in London, Oxford, and Victoria

## Caring for Newborn Puppies

### The first 24 hours
The first 12–24 hours are critical. Every puppy must receive **colostrum** — the first milk — within 12 hours of birth. Colostrum provides maternal antibodies (passive immunity) that can't be delivered any other way. A puppy that misses colostrum is significantly more vulnerable to infection.

Ensure every puppy has access to a teat and is nursing effectively. Signs of successful nursing: a rounded belly, calm demeanour, and steady weight.

### Puppy weight: the most important indicator
Weigh every puppy at birth and at the same time every day for the first two weeks. A healthy puppy should:
- **Maintain or gain weight every 24 hours after day 2**
- **Double their birth weight by 10–12 days**
- Any puppy losing weight for more than 24 hours needs veterinary attention

Birth weight varies significantly by breed — a healthy Chihuahua puppy may weigh 100g; a healthy Great Dane puppy 500–700g. The important thing is the trend.

### Temperature: the invisible killer
Newborn puppies cannot regulate their own body temperature. Hypothermia is the leading cause of puppy death in the first days.

| Age | Target environmental temperature |
|-----|----------------------------------|
| Week 1 | 30–32°C in the whelping box |
| Week 2 | 26–28°C |
| Week 3 | 22–24°C |
| Week 4+ | Room temperature (if normal healthy development) |

Always provide a cooler area in the box — puppies crawl toward comfort, so they need to be able to move away from the heat source.

### Feeding — when mum needs help
Most healthy mothers nurse without issues. However, you may need to supplement or hand-rear if:
- A puppy is too weak to nurse
- Mum has more puppies than functional teats (count carefully)
- Mum has mastitis or is unwell
- A puppy is rejected or pushed out repeatedly
- Birth weight is very low for the breed

**Never give cow's milk** — it causes diarrhoea and does not meet puppy nutritional needs.

### The "fading puppy" warning signs
Fading Puppy Syndrome affects healthy-looking puppies that suddenly deteriorate in the first 2 weeks. Signs include:
- Continuous crying when littermates are quiet
- Failure to gain weight or weight loss
- Straying away from mum and littermates
- Pale gums
- Cold to the touch despite the warm box

Act quickly — a fading puppy needs a vet within hours. Gentle warming (not too fast) and assisted feeding while you travel.

## The Whelping Mum: Nutrition and Recovery

### During lactation
A nursing mother is nutritionally more demanding than during pregnancy. Calorie needs can be 2–4× her normal intake, depending on litter size. She should have **unlimited access to food and water** — don't restrict her.

Continue feeding the high-quality puppy/growth food from pregnancy throughout lactation. If she's struggling to maintain condition (her ribs become visible, she loses muscle mass), increase food further or supplement with a high-calorie paste.

> **🐾 Affiliate pick:** [Denes Nursing and Whelping Support](https://www.denes.com) — herbal supplement blend including raspberry leaf and nettle, traditionally used to support milk production.

### Post-whelping discharge
A reddish-brown or dark discharge (lochia) is normal for 4–6 weeks after birth as the uterus involutes. It should reduce in quantity over time and have no foul odour. **Call the vet if:** it becomes bright red and heavy, develops a foul smell, or your dog develops a fever.

### Mastitis
Mastitis (infection of the mammary glands) can occur when milk isn't being drawn off regularly. Signs: hard, hot, swollen, or painful teats; red or discoloured milk; fever; lethargy. Requires prompt veterinary treatment — untreated mastitis is extremely painful and can become life-threatening.

## Kennelling Regulations for Breeders (UK)

If you breed more than **3 litters per year**, you must be licensed under the [Animal Welfare (Breeding of Dogs) Regulations 2018](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/dog-breeding) and [Breeding and Sale of Dogs Act 1999](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1999/11). Your local council issues licences and conducts inspections.

For occasional or first-time litters, you don't need a licence — but you're still subject to the Animal Welfare Act 2006 and must ensure all puppies are properly socialised, vet-checked, microchipped, and sold only when healthy.

**Lucy's Law:** Since 2020, all puppies in England must be sold via the breeder or a licensed rescue. Third-party sales (pet shops, dealers) are illegal. Scotland and Wales have equivalent legislation.

## The First 8 Weeks: Puppy Development Milestones

| Age | Development milestones |
|-----|----------------------|
| Days 1–2 | Born blind and deaf; heat-seeking, nursing |
| Days 3–14 | Neonatal period — totally dependent on mum |
| Days 10–14 | Eyes begin to open (appear cloudy at first) |
| Days 14–21 | Ears open; first wobbly attempts at walking |
| Days 21–28 | Start of sensitive socialisation period; begin to play |
| Weeks 4–5 | Weaning begins; puppies eat moistened solid food |
| Weeks 5–7 | Primary socialisation — critical window for positive human exposure |
| Weeks 7–8 | Ready for individual human interaction; vaccinations can start at 8 weeks |
| **8 weeks** | **Earliest legal age for rehoming in the UK** |

## Preparing for Rehoming

Before puppies go to new homes, ensure:
- [ ] All puppies microchipped (legal requirement in England, Scotland, Wales, NI)
- [ ] First vaccination given (if over 8 weeks) or vet health check completed
- [ ] Puppy pack prepared (food, feeding schedule, vet records, breeder contact)
- [ ] Kennel Club registration if applicable
- [ ] All buyers have been vetted — ask about their setup, experience, whether they have children or other pets
- [ ] Signed purchase contract (recommended — sets out health guarantees and return policy)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I spay my dog while she's pregnant?**
Yes — emergency spaying (ovariohysterectomy) can be performed during pregnancy if there are welfare or medical grounds. This should only be done under veterinary advice.

**Q: My dog ate all the placentas — is that safe?**
Up to 2 placentas is generally fine. Eating more can cause severe diarrhoea. Quietly dispose of any extras she hasn't reached.

**Q: One puppy is much smaller than the others — what should I do?**
Runt puppies need special attention. Ensure they get access to the best teats (front teats produce more milk), monitor weight daily, and be prepared to supplement with puppy milk. They can thrive with extra care.

**Q: How do I tell if a puppy isn't getting enough milk?**
The clearest sign is weight loss or failure to gain. Crying continuously while littermates are settled, rooting frantically but not latching, and a loose rather than rounded belly are also indicators.

**Q: When can I handle the puppies?**
Gentle handling from birth is actually beneficial for socialisation — brief, calm contact is fine. Avoid disturbing the nest excessively in the first week. Mum may become anxious if she senses too much interference.

**Q: Should I breed my dog again?**
The RSPCA and most veterinary bodies recommend **no more than 4 litters per bitch**, with a rest period of at least one full heat cycle between litters. Bitches should not be bred before their second season or after age 8. Always prioritise your dog's welfare over litter frequency.
