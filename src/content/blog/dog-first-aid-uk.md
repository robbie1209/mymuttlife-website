## Introduction

Accidents happen in seconds. One moment Poppy is bounding through the park, the next she's limping on a thorn-pierced paw — or worse. Knowing what to do in those first few minutes can be the difference between a quick vet visit and a genuine emergency.

This guide covers the most common dog emergencies UK owners face, what to do (and critically, what not to do), and how to build a first aid kit that's actually useful. We've sourced guidance from the RSPCA, PDSA, Blue Cross and the British Veterinary Association — not internet forums.

**First, the golden rule:** First aid is exactly that — *first* aid. It stabilises your dog while you get them to a vet. It is never a substitute for professional veterinary care.

## The DRSABC Framework

Before you do anything in an emergency, run through DRSABC — used by both human and animal first responders:

- **D — Danger:** Is it safe for you to approach? Don't put yourself at risk.
- **R — Response:** Is your dog conscious? Call their name, tap gently.
- **S — Shout:** Call for help.
- **A — Airway:** Is the airway clear? Can you see/hear breathing?
- **B — Breathing:** Watch the chest rise. Count breaths (normal: 15–30/min at rest).
- **C — Circulation:** Check for a heartbeat. Place your hand on the chest just behind the left elbow.

If your dog is unconscious and not breathing: start CPR (see below) while someone calls an emergency vet.

## Common Dog Emergencies: What To Do

### 1. Cuts and Lacerations

**Minor cuts (surface level, stops bleeding within 5 minutes):**
1. Apply gentle pressure with a clean gauze pad for 5–10 minutes. Don't keep lifting to check — this disrupts clotting.
2. Once bleeding stops, clean with sterile saline solution (not hydrogen peroxide or antiseptic — these damage tissue).
3. Cover loosely with a dressing. Check daily for redness, swelling, or discharge.
4. Book a vet check if the wound is more than 1cm long or deep — it may need stitches.

**Serious cuts (won't stop bleeding, very deep, or near joints):**
1. Apply firm pressure with a clean cloth. Don't remove the cloth — add more on top if it soaks through.
2. Keep the wound elevated if possible.
3. Go to a vet immediately. Don't attempt to clean a deep wound yourself.

**What NOT to do:** Do not use cotton wool (fibres stick to wounds), do not use antiseptic creams on open wounds, do not apply a tourniquet unless you have specific training.

### 2. Paw Injuries

Paws are one of the most common injury sites — thorns, glass, cuts from ice salt, and burned pads from hot tarmac (a serious summer hazard).

**Thorn or glass in paw:**
1. If visible and easily graspable, use tweezers to remove in one smooth motion.
2. Clean with saline. Cover with a sock or bootie to prevent licking.
3. If you can't remove it cleanly, or if it's deep — vet visit.

**Hot pavement burns (July/August risk):**
The 7-second rule: if you can't hold your hand on the pavement for 7 seconds, it's too hot for paws. Tarmac can reach 60°C on a 25°C day.
1. Move your dog off the hot surface immediately.
2. Cool the pads with room-temperature water (not ice cold).
3. Cover loosely and see a vet — burns are more serious than they look.

### 3. Heatstroke

Heatstroke kills dogs — and it can develop in minutes. It's not just about hot cars. Dogs can overheat on warm walks, in conservatories, or even lying in a sunny garden.

**Signs of heatstroke:**
- Excessive panting and drooling
- Bright red or pale gums
- Thick, sticky saliva
- Weakness, wobbly gait
- Vomiting or diarrhoea
- Collapse, seizures

**What to do (this is time-critical):**
1. Move your dog to a cool, shaded area immediately.
2. Pour room-temperature (not ice cold) water over their body — especially the neck, armpits, and groin. Cold water causes blood vessels to constrict and can make things worse.
3. Fan the wet areas to promote evaporative cooling.
4. Offer small sips of cool water if they're conscious.
5. Call your vet while you're cooling — heatstroke causes organ damage that isn't always visible.
6. Get to a vet as fast as possible, even if they seem to recover.

**What NOT to do:** Don't use ice or ice-cold water. Don't cover your dog with wet towels (traps heat). Don't delay calling a vet.

### 4. Choking

A choking dog will be pawing at their mouth, making exaggerated swallowing motions, retching without producing anything, or struggling to breathe.

**If conscious:**
1. Open their mouth gently and look inside. If you can clearly see the object and reach it safely with two fingers, remove it — but be careful not to push it further in.
2. If you can't dislodge it manually, perform back blows: hold your dog with their head slightly lower than their body. Give 5 firm blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand.
3. Check the mouth again. Repeat up to 3 times.
4. If still choking: abdominal thrusts for small dogs (like the Heimlich manoeuvre), or chest compressions for large dogs. See detailed technique at [PDSA's pet first aid guide](https://www.pdsa.org.uk/pet-help-and-advice/pet-health/general/pet-first-aid).
5. Rush to a vet regardless of outcome — even if you dislodge the object, there may be internal damage.

**If unconscious:** Start CPR and call the vet immediately.

### 5. Dog CPR

If your dog is unresponsive and not breathing, CPR buys time until you reach a vet.

**For medium to large dogs:**
1. Lay your dog on their right side on a firm surface.
2. Tilt the head back slightly to open the airway.
3. **Rescue breaths:** Close the mouth, cover the nose with your mouth, blow gently until the chest rises. Give 5 rescue breaths.
4. **Chest compressions:** Place both hands over the widest part of the chest (about one-third of the way up from the sternum). Compress firmly 30 times at a rate of 100–120 per minute (to the beat of "Stayin' Alive").
5. Ratio: 30 compressions to 2 rescue breaths. Continue until breathing resumes or you reach the vet.

**For small dogs (under 10kg):**
- Use one hand to encircle the chest. Compress with thumb and fingers.
- Lighter pressure — their ribs are fragile.

**For barrel-chested breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs):**
- Lie dog on their back. Compress on the sternum.

This is a skill worth practising. The Blue Cross offer dog first aid courses across the UK — well worth an afternoon. [Find a course here](https://www.bluecross.org.uk).

### 6. Poisoning

Dogs are remarkably good at eating things they shouldn't. The UK's most common canine poisons include:

| Substance | Found In | Risk Level |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Xylitol | Sugar-free gum, peanut butter, some baked goods | ⚠️ Very High |
| Chocolate | Obvious, but also Christmas/Easter baking | ⚠️ Very High |
| Grapes & raisins | Fruit, mince pies, Simnel cake | ⚠️ Very High |
| Onions & garlic | Cooked or raw, in any dish | 🔶 High |
| Paracetamol | Human medicine cabinet | ⚠️ Very High |
| Slug pellets | Garden | ⚠️ Very High (metaldehyde) |
| Spring bulbs | Daffodils, bluebells, tulips | 🔶 Moderate–High |
| Blue-green algae | Lakes, canals (summer months) | ⚠️ Very High |
| Macadamia nuts | Snacks, baked goods | 🔶 Moderate |

**Signs of poisoning:** Vomiting, drooling excessively, trembling, seizures, pale gums, collapse, dilated pupils.

**What to do:**
1. Don't panic — but act fast.
2. Don't try to make your dog vomit at home. In some cases (caustic substances, sharp objects) this makes things much worse.
3. Call the **Animal Poison Line: 01202 509000** (24/7, charge applies — about £30 — but it could save your dog's life).
4. Also call your vet immediately.
5. If you know what they ate — take the packaging or a sample. It helps the vet enormously.

### 7. Broken Bones and Suspected Fractures

Signs: leg held off the ground, visible deformity, bone protruding, your dog won't bear weight, crying when touched.

1. Keep your dog as still as possible.
2. Don't attempt to splint the leg yourself — you're likely to cause more pain and worsen the injury.
3. Muzzle your dog — they will bite if in enough pain, even dogs who never normally would.
4. Improvise a stretcher if needed (a flat board, a rigid bag) — keep the spine as straight as possible.
5. Get to a vet immediately. Fractures are painful and can involve internal bleeding.

---

### 8. Seizures (Fits)

Witnessing your dog seize is terrifying. Here's what to do:

**During the seizure:**
1. Stay calm. Time the seizure.
2. Clear the area of anything they could hit.
3. Do NOT put your hand in their mouth — dogs don't swallow their tongues, and you will be bitten.
4. Dim the lights and reduce noise if possible.
5. Speak softly. Your voice may help.

**After the seizure:**
- Your dog will be disoriented, possibly temporarily blind, possibly wobbly. This is normal (postictal phase).
- Keep them warm and quiet.

**When to call the vet immediately:**
- Seizure lasts more than 5 minutes (status epilepticus — life-threatening)
- Multiple seizures within 24 hours
- First-ever seizure
- Dog doesn't recover within 30 minutes

---

### 9. Eye Injuries

Eyes are delicate and worsen quickly without treatment.

- Flush the eye gently with sterile saline solution. Don't rub.
- If there's an object in the eye, do NOT attempt to remove it.
- Cover loosely with a damp gauze pad to stop your dog rubbing.
- See a vet the same day — eye injuries can progress to blindness within hours.

---

### 10. Near-Drowning

Dogs can drown — especially brachycephalic breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs, Frenchies) and elderly dogs.

1. Remove from water. If they're unconscious, tip head down to drain water from lungs.
2. Lay on their side. Check for breathing and pulse.
3. Start CPR if not breathing.
4. Wrap in a towel to prevent hypothermia.
5. Rush to a vet even if they appear to recover — secondary drowning (fluid in lungs causing issues hours later) is a real risk.

## When To Go Straight To The Vet (No Waiting)

Don't wait for the morning if any of these apply:
- Difficulty breathing
- Suspected poisoning
- Seizure lasting more than 2 minutes
- Cannot bear any weight on a limb
- Eye injury
- Bleeding that won't stop after 10 minutes of pressure
- Pale, white, or blue gums
- Collapse or loss of consciousness
- Known ingestion of a toxic substance
- Penetrating chest wound
- Suspected heatstroke

## FAQs

**Can I give my dog paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain?**
Never. Both are toxic to dogs. Paracetamol causes liver failure; ibuprofen causes kidney failure and gastric ulcers. The only safe pain relief is prescribed by a vet.

**What's the difference between a seizure and a muscle tremor?**
Seizures typically involve the whole body, loss of consciousness, paddling movements, and are followed by a confused recovery phase. Tremors/twitching (e.g., during sleep) are usually minor and brief. When in doubt, video the episode and call your vet.

**Should I muzzle my dog before administering first aid?**
If your dog is in pain and you need to handle the injured area, yes. Even the most placid dog may bite defensively when in pain. A sock or strip of bandage works as an emergency muzzle: loop around the muzzle, tie under the chin, then tie behind the ears.

**Is hydrogen peroxide safe for cleaning dog wounds?**
No. Despite being a common suggestion online, hydrogen peroxide damages healthy tissue and slows healing. Sterile saline is the correct choice.

**What if I can't afford an emergency vet?**
PDSA PetAid practices offer free or reduced-cost treatment for pet owners receiving means-tested benefits. The RSPCA also operates clinics. Never let cost be the reason you delay getting help — call the clinic first, explain the situation, and ask about payment plans.

**Can I get pet first aid insurance?**
Yes — many pet insurers cover emergency vet costs. Check your policy for the 24-hour helpline number too; most insurers provide nurse advice over the phone at no cost.
