## Why Use a Harness Instead of a Collar?

Before we get into the picks, it's worth understanding why so many vets, trainers, and behaviourists now recommend harnesses over traditional collars for everyday walking:

- **Reduced neck strain** â€” Collars put all the pressure on your dog's throat and trachea. For breeds prone to breathing issues (Bulldogs, Pugs, French Bulldogs) or those with delicate necks (Whippets, Italian Greyhounds), a harness distributes force across the chest and shoulders instead.
- **Better control with pullers** â€” A front-clip harness redirects your dog's momentum when they pull, making walks more manageable without yanking on their neck.
- **Prevents escape artists** â€” Dogs who can slip a collar (looking at you, Greyhounds and Staffies) are much harder to wriggle free from a well-fitted harness.
- **Ideal for puppies and seniors** â€” Puppies are still developing, and senior dogs may have arthritis or spinal issues. A harness is gentler on both.

## How to Measure Your Dog for a Harness

Getting the right fit is everything. A loose harness is a safety risk (escape artists will find a way), and a tight one causes chafing and discomfort. Here's how to measure properly:

### What You'll Need
A soft tape measure (or a piece of string you can measure against a ruler).

### Step 1: Chest Girth (Most Important)
Wrap the tape around the **widest part of your dog's ribcage**, just behind the front legs. This is the measurement most harness brands use for sizing. Keep the tape snug but not tight â€” you should be able to fit two fingers between the tape and your dog's body.

### Step 2: Neck Circumference
Measure around the **base of the neck**, where a collar would sit. Some harnesses (like the PerfectFit) need this measurement too.

### Step 3: Back Length (Optional)
For modular harnesses, measure from the **base of the neck to the base of the tail** along the spine.

### Tips for Accurate Measuring
- Measure while your dog is **standing**, not sitting or lying down
- Take measurements **twice** and use the larger number
- If your dog is between sizes, **go up** â€” most harnesses have enough adjustment to tighten
- Check the specific brand's size chart â€” sizes aren't universal between brands
- If your dog has a thick coat, measure with the coat at its typical thickness

## Harness vs Collar: Which Is Better?

This isn't an either/or question â€” most dog owners use both. Here's when each makes sense:

**Use a harness when:**
- Walking dogs who pull (especially medium-to-large breeds)
- Walking brachycephalic (flat-faced) breeds â€” Pugs, Bulldogs, French Bulldogs
- Walking puppies or senior dogs
- Your dog can slip out of a collar
- You need extra control (reactive dogs, high-traffic areas)
- Your dog has neck, throat, or spinal issues

**Use a collar when:**
- Carrying ID tags (legally required in the UK under the Control of Dogs Order 1992)
- Your dog walks calmly without pulling
- Quick garden trips or toilet breaks
- Breeds with strong necks who prefer the freedom (some owners of larger breeds find collars work fine)

**Our recommendation:** Use a collar for ID tags (it's the law!) and a harness for walks. That way, your dog always has identification on and you get the control and comfort benefits of a harness when it matters most.

## How to Get Your Dog Used to a Harness

Some dogs take to a harness immediately. Others act like you're putting them in a straitjacket. If your dog is in the second camp:

1. **Let them sniff it** â€” Leave the harness on the floor near their bed for a day or two. Let them investigate it on their own terms.
2. **Reward generously** â€” Every time the harness appears, treats appear. Create a positive association.
3. **Practice at home** â€” Put the harness on for short periods indoors before attempting a walk. Reward calm behaviour.
4. **Don't force it** â€” If your dog is stressed, take a step back. Slow and steady wins this race.
5. **Try a different style** â€” Some dogs hate things going over their head. Switch to a step-in design (like the EzyDog or Ancol) if that's the issue.

## Looking After Your Dog's Harness

A good harness should last years with proper care:

- **Hand wash** with mild soap every few weeks (or after muddy walks)
- **Air dry only** â€” never tumble dry, as heat can damage buckles and weaken straps
- **Check for wear** â€” inspect stitching, buckles, and D-rings monthly
- **Replace if damaged** â€” a frayed strap or bent buckle is a safety hazard

Most nylon harnesses can handle a gentle machine wash on a cool cycle in a laundry bag, but always check the manufacturer's care instructions first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best dog harness in the UK?

For most dogs, the **Ruffwear Front Range** offers the best combination of comfort, durability, and versatility with both front and back clips. For budget buyers, the **Rabbitgoo No-Pull Harness** is the UK's bestseller on Amazon for good reason. If your dog has unusual proportions, the **PerfectFit Modular Harness** gives the most customisable fit available.

### Are harnesses better than collars for dogs?

For walking, yes â€” harnesses distribute pressure across the chest and shoulders rather than concentrating it on the throat. This is especially important for breeds prone to breathing issues, dogs who pull, puppies, and senior dogs. However, all dogs in the UK should still wear a collar with an ID tag as required by law.

### What harness is best for a dog that pulls?

A front-clip harness is best for pullers. When your dog pulls, the front attachment point redirects them back towards you rather than allowing forward momentum. The **Halti No-Pull Harness** (Â£15â€“Â£20) is specifically designed for this, and the **Rabbitgoo** and **TrueLove** also offer effective front-clip options at affordable prices.

### How tight should a dog harness be?

You should be able to fit **two fingers** between the harness and your dog's body at any point. Tighter than that causes chafing and restricted movement; looser and your dog may wriggle free. Check the fit regularly, especially with puppies who are still growing.

### Can a dog wear a harness all day?

It's generally not recommended. Harnesses can cause chafing, matting of fur, and skin irritation if worn for extended periods. Put the harness on for walks and activities, then take it off when you're home. If your dog needs to wear something all day for identification, a lightweight collar is better.

### What size harness does my dog need?

Measure your dog's **chest girth** â€” the widest part of the ribcage, just behind the front legs. Compare this measurement to the brand's size chart. If your dog is between sizes, go up. Every brand sizes slightly differently, so never assume a "Medium" in one brand equals a "Medium" in another.

### Are no-pull harnesses cruel?

No. A well-designed no-pull harness with a front clip simply redirects your dog's forward momentum without causing pain or discomfort. They're far gentler than choke chains, prong collars, or even regular collars when a dog pulls hard. Most UK dog trainers recommend front-clip harnesses as a humane tool for managing pulling.
