## What Is Separation Anxiety in Dogs? {#what-is-separation-anxiety}

Separation anxiety is a stress response triggered when a dog is left alone or separated from the person (or people) they're most attached to. It's not naughtiness, spite, or a lack of training — it's genuine distress.

The RSPCA estimates that **around 8 in 10 dogs find it hard to cope when left alone**, though not all of these cases are full-blown separation anxiety. The condition ranges from mild unease to severe panic that can result in self-injury.

**Important distinction:** There's a difference between a bored dog who chews your shoes because they've got nothing better to do, and an anxious dog who destroys the doorframe trying to follow you. Treatment is different for each.

### Separation Anxiety vs. Boredom

| Separation Anxiety | Boredom/Under-stimulation |
|---|---|
| Starts within minutes of you leaving | May take an hour or more to begin |
| Focused on exits (doors, windows) | Spread around the house |
| Vocalisation (howling, barking, whining) | May or may not vocalise |
| Follows you room to room when you're home | Generally relaxed when you're home |
| Won't eat treats or play with toys when alone | Happy to engage with enrichment |
| Happens every time you leave | May be intermittent |

## Signs Your Dog Has Separation Anxiety {#signs}

These behaviours typically start within the first 15-30 minutes of being left alone:

### Obvious Signs
- **Destructive behaviour** — chewing door frames, window sills, furniture, or their own bed
- **Excessive vocalisation** — barking, howling, or whining that neighbours may report
- **Toileting indoors** — even in house-trained dogs
- **Escape attempts** — scratching at doors, trying to get through windows

### Subtle Signs (Easy to Miss)
- **Pacing or circling** — repetitive movement patterns
- **Excessive drooling or panting** — stress responses
- **Not eating** — leaving food or treats untouched until you return
- **Hyper-attachment** — following you from room to room, watching you constantly
- **Over-the-top greetings** — acting as though you've been gone for weeks when it was 10 minutes
- **Pre-departure anxiety** — getting distressed when they see you put on shoes or pick up keys

**Top tip:** Set up a camera (even an old phone with an app like Alfred or Dog Monitor) to record what actually happens when you leave. Many owners are surprised by what they see. A [GPS tracker](/blog/best-dog-gps-trackers-uk/) can also help if your dog is an escape artist.

## Common Causes {#causes}

### 1. Change in Routine
The biggest single trigger. Moving house, a new job, kids starting school, or — the big one since 2020 — **returning to the office after working from home**. Dogs who spent 18 months with their owners 24/7 suddenly found themselves alone for 8 hours. The Dogs Trust reported a 67% increase in separation anxiety cases post-pandemic.

### 2. Lack of Early Socialisation
Puppies who weren't gradually taught to spend time alone during their critical socialisation window (3-14 weeks) are more likely to develop separation anxiety later. This is especially common in pandemic puppies who were rarely left.

### 3. Rehoming or Rescue
Dogs who've lost their previous home — whether through rehoming, rescue, or bereavement — are at higher risk. They've already learned that people can disappear.

### 4. Traumatic Experience
A burglary, a fire alarm, fireworks, or even a severe thunderstorm while home alone can create a negative association with being left.

### 5. New Baby or Family Change
A new baby dramatically changes household routines and the amount of attention a dog receives. Dogs who were previously the centre of attention can struggle with the shift. *(More on this below.)*

### 6. Age-Related Changes
Senior dogs with cognitive decline (canine cognitive dysfunction) can develop separation anxiety even if they were previously fine alone. Changes in hearing or vision can also increase anxiety.

## Breeds Most Prone to Separation Anxiety {#breeds}

While any dog can develop separation anxiety, some breeds are genetically predisposed:

**High risk:**
- Labrador Retrievers
- German Shepherds
- Border Collies
- Cocker Spaniels
- Cavalier King Charles Spaniels
- Vizslas
- Jack Russell Terriers

**Moderate risk:**
- Staffordshire Bull Terriers
- Bichon Frisé
- French Bulldogs
- Toy breeds generally

**Lower risk (but not immune):**
- Greyhounds
- Basset Hounds
- Shiba Inus
- Chow Chows

Breed predisposition doesn't mean destiny — environment, training, and individual temperament all play a role.

## How to Help: Step-by-Step Training Plan {#training}

This is the core of treatment. Medication and products can support the process, but **desensitisation training is the foundation**.

### Phase 1: Reduce Pre-Departure Triggers (Week 1-2)

Your dog has learned to associate certain actions with you leaving. Keys jangling, shoes going on, grabbing a coat — these are all "departure cues" that trigger anxiety *before you've even left*.

**What to do:**
1. Pick up your keys randomly throughout the day — then sit back down
2. Put your shoes on, walk around the house, take them off
3. Put on your coat, sit on the sofa for 10 minutes, take it off
4. Open and close the front door without leaving
5. Repeat until your dog stops reacting to these cues

**The goal:** Break the predictive chain. Keys ≠ leaving. Shoes ≠ abandonment.

### Phase 2: Practice Micro-Absences (Week 2-4)

Start with absences so short your dog doesn't have time to get anxious.

1. **Step 1:** Go to another room for 5 seconds, come back. No fuss.
2. **Step 2:** Gradually increase to 30 seconds, 1 minute, 2 minutes
3. **Step 3:** Add the front door. Step outside for 5 seconds, come back
4. **Step 4:** Build up to 5 minutes, 10 minutes, 15 minutes outside
5. **Step 5:** Once your dog can manage 30 minutes alone calmly, you can start increasing in larger increments

**Critical rules:**
- **Never return when your dog is actively distressed** — wait for a calm moment, even if brief, then come back. Returning to barking teaches them that barking brings you back.
- **Keep arrivals and departures boring.** No emotional goodbyes. No excited hellos. Just... normal.
- **Don't punish.** Ever. Your dog isn't being naughty. Punishment increases anxiety.
- **Use a camera** to monitor progress from your phone

### Phase 3: Build Duration (Week 4-8+)

Once your dog can handle 30 minutes:
- Increase by 10-15 minute increments
- Vary the duration (don't always make it longer — throw in short absences too)
- Practice at different times of day
- Leave via different exits if you have them

**Realistic timeline:** Most dogs need 4-12 weeks of consistent training to show significant improvement. Severe cases may take 6+ months.

### Phase 4: Maintenance

Even after your dog seems comfortable:
- Continue to vary absence length
- Don't suddenly jump from 2-hour absences to 8-hour workdays
- If you need to be away longer than your dog can handle during training, arrange a dog sitter, daycare, or ask a friend to pop in

## When to See a Behaviourist {#behaviourist}

**See your vet first** if your dog's separation anxiety is:
- Severe (self-injury, not eating, extreme destruction)
- Not improving after 4-6 weeks of consistent training
- Accompanied by other behavioural changes
- New in a senior dog (may indicate cognitive decline)

Your vet can:
- Rule out medical causes (pain, cognitive decline, UTIs causing indoor toileting)
- Prescribe medication if appropriate (fluoxetine or clomipramine are the most common — both are licensed for use in dogs in the UK)
- Refer you to a **clinical animal behaviourist**

### Finding a Behaviourist in the UK

Look for qualifications — this is an unregulated industry:
- **ABTC (Animal Behaviour and Training Council)** registered practitioners — the gold standard
- **APBC (Association of Pet Behaviour Counsellors)** members
- **CCAB (Certificated Clinical Animal Behaviourist)** — requires vet referral

**Expect to pay:** £150-300 for an initial consultation, £50-100 for follow-ups. Some pet insurance policies cover behavioural consultations — check your policy. *(See our [guide to pet insurance](/blog/best-dog-insurance-uk/) for policies that include behavioural cover.)*

**Avoid:** Anyone who suggests dominance theory, "alpha" corrections, or punishment-based methods for anxiety. These make separation anxiety significantly worse.

## Dogs and New Babies: A Special Note {#babies}

This deserves its own section because it's incredibly common and often overlooked until it's too late.

**The scenario:** You're expecting a baby. Your dog is the current "baby" of the house. Once the new arrival comes, everything changes — attention, routine, access to rooms, noise levels, sleep patterns. Dogs can develop separation anxiety even when you're *in the house* but suddenly unavailable.

### Before Baby Arrives (Start 2-3 Months Early)

1. **Gradually reduce one-on-one attention** — if your dog is used to being glued to you, start building independence now
2. **Practice alone time** — use baby gates to create spaces where the dog can't follow you
3. **Change routines gradually** — if walks will be at different times post-baby, start shifting now
4. **Introduce baby sounds** — play recordings of crying at low volume while doing positive things (treats, play)
5. **Set up the nursery early** — let your dog get used to new furniture, smells, and restricted access before the baby arrives
6. **Establish a safe space** — a crate, bed, or room that's "theirs" where they can retreat

### After Baby Arrives

1. **Bring home a blanket with baby's scent** before bringing baby home
2. **Keep greetings calm** — don't overcompensate by ignoring the dog, but don't let guilt drive over-the-top attention either
3. **Maintain at least one consistent routine** — even a short daily walk at the same time helps
4. **Include the dog** — walks with the pram, gentle supervision during tummy time (never leave dog and baby unsupervised)
5. **Watch for signs** — increased clinginess, pacing, toileting issues, or destructive behaviour when you're with the baby in another room

**The good news:** Most dogs adjust within 2-4 weeks if the transition is managed well. Labs in particular (including black Labs!) tend to be natural family dogs who take to babies beautifully.

## FAQ {#faq}

### How long can I leave my dog alone?

The RSPCA recommends **no more than 4 hours** for an adult dog, though this varies by age and individual:
- **Puppies (under 6 months):** 1-2 hours max
- **Adult dogs:** Up to 4 hours regularly; 6 hours occasionally
- **Senior dogs:** 2-4 hours (may need more frequent toilet breaks)

If you work full-time, consider a dog walker, daycare, or asking a neighbour to pop in.

### Will getting a second dog help?

Sometimes, but not always. If your dog's anxiety is specifically about *you* leaving, a second dog won't fix that — they'll just have company while they panic. If your dog is genuinely lonely and under-stimulated, a companion *might* help. This is a conversation for a behaviourist, not an impulse decision.

### Does crate training help with separation anxiety?

For dogs who see their crate as a safe space: yes, it can reduce destructive behaviour. For dogs who associate the crate with confinement: absolutely not. It can make anxiety worse and lead to injury. **Never force a distressed dog into a crate.** If your dog doesn't already love their crate, desensitise them to it separately before using it for alone-time.

### Is separation anxiety curable?

Most dogs improve significantly with consistent training and, where needed, medication. Some dogs will always need management (shorter absences, enrichment, calming products) but can live happy, comfortable lives. The key is starting treatment early — the longer it goes untreated, the harder it is to resolve.

### Can I use CBD oil for my dog's anxiety?

CBD products for pets are in a legal grey area in the UK. The VMD (Veterinary Medicines Directorate) considers them unauthorised veterinary medicines, meaning they can't be legally marketed for treating conditions in animals. Some owners report positive results, but there's limited clinical evidence for dogs specifically. If you want to try this route, speak to your vet.

## Final Thoughts

Separation anxiety is stressful — for your dog and for you. But it's manageable. The dogs who do best are the ones whose owners commit to a consistent, gradual training plan and resist the urge to rush it.

Start with a camera. Desensitise to departure cues. Build up absences slowly. Use calming products to take the edge off. And if you're stuck after a month, get professional help — a good behaviourist is worth every penny.

Your dog isn't trying to punish you for leaving. They're scared you won't come back. With patience and the right approach, you can show them that you always will.
