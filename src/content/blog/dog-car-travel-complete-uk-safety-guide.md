## Introduction

Every year, the RSPCA responds to thousands of calls about dogs left in hot cars. Dogs are injured in car accidents because they weren't properly restrained. And thousands more arrive at their destinations stressed, sick, and exhausted — not because the journey was too long, but because no one prepared them for it.

Travelling by car with your dog should be easy. It can be, with the right setup.

This guide covers everything: UK legal requirements for restraining your dog, which harnesses and crates actually hold up in a crash test, how to prevent and manage travel sickness, what to do if your dog overheats, and how to make even a five-hour motorway run comfortable for both of you.

Whether you're heading to the beach this summer, driving to the vets, or moving house — this is the guide to bookmark.

## UK Law: Are You Actually Required to Restrain Your Dog in the Car?

This surprises many dog owners: **there is no specific law requiring you to use a harness or crate for your dog in a car.** However, there is Rule 57 of the Highway Code, which states:

> *"When in a vehicle make sure dogs or other animals are suitably restrained so they cannot distract you while you are driving or injure you, or themselves, if you stop quickly."*

Failing to comply with Rule 57 could mean:
- **Careless driving charge** — up to nine penalty points and an unlimited fine
- **Invalid car insurance** — most policies require animals to be safely restrained; a dog loose in the car during an accident could void your claim
- **Being charged with animal cruelty** under the Animal Welfare Act 2006 if injury results from inadequate restraint

**Bottom line:** You're legally expected to restrain your dog. The law doesn't specify how — harness, crate, or boot guard — but "on the back seat loose" is a risk you shouldn't take.

### What Counts as Suitable Restraint?

The Highway Code doesn't define this in detail, but the consensus from the RSPCA, Dogs Trust, and road safety experts is:
- A **properly fitted crash-tested harness** attached to the seatbelt
- A **secured travel crate** that won't slide or tip in an emergency stop
- A **boot divider or dog guard** for dogs in the boot of estate/SUV vehicles (note: guards alone don't prevent dogs being thrown forward)

A dog loose on the back seat — or sitting on a passenger's lap — is not suitable restraint.

## Choosing the Right Restraint: Harness vs Crate vs Boot Guard

### Dog Car Harnesses

A car harness is a walking harness's sturdier cousin — designed specifically to spread the forces of a sudden stop across the dog's chest and shoulders, rather than the neck.

**What to look for:**
- Crash test certification (look for Centre for Pet Safety tested products)
- Padded chest plate — distributes impact
- Short tether to seatbelt buckle (prevents excessive forward movement)
- Separate clip positions for car use vs. walking
- Correct sizing — measure your dog's girth, not just neck

### Travel Crates

For many dogs — particularly larger breeds, anxious dogs, and puppies — a secured crate is the safest and most comfortable option. Dogs in crates are contained even if the crate moves slightly; they can't be thrown across the vehicle, and the crate provides a familiar den environment that reduces stress.

**Types:**
- **Soft-sided crates:** Lightweight, good for calm dogs on short journeys. Not crash-protective.
- **Hard plastic crates (e.g., Ruff Land, IATA-approved):** Durable, good for most situations. Some crash testing available.
- **Aluminium travel crates (e.g., Variocage, Fido):** Engineered for crash safety, used by working dog handlers and police. Expensive but genuinely protective.
- **Crash-tested dog boxes (van/SUV):** Fixed to the vehicle floor or boot. Best-in-class protection. Brands: Variocage, EzyRider, MSA.

**Sizing guide:**
Your dog should be able to stand, turn, and lie down comfortably. Too large and they'll be thrown around inside; too small and they can't adopt a comfortable resting position.

| Dog Size | Crate Size |
|----------|-----------|
| Toy/small (<10kg) | S/M — approx 60×45cm floor area |
| Medium (10–25kg) | M/L — approx 75×55cm floor area |
| Large (25–45kg) | L/XL — approx 100×70cm floor area |
| Giant (45kg+) | XXL — measure individually |

**Securing the crate:** A crate that slides in the boot is almost as dangerous as no crate. Use non-slip matting and a seatbelt loop or boot tie-down. The Variocage and similar designs bolt directly to vehicle anchor points — significantly safer.

### Boot Guards and Dividers

A boot guard (or dog guard) is a metal or fabric barrier that prevents your dog climbing from boot to passenger cabin. It does NOT stop your dog being thrown into the guard in a crash — it just keeps them out of the front seats.

**Use a boot guard when:**
- Your dog is well-behaved and not a crash risk to themselves in the boot
- Combined with a fitted boot liner or hammock
- As a secondary measure alongside a harness or crate

**Don't rely on a boot guard alone** for safety in accident scenarios.

## Preventing Dog Travel Sickness

Travel sickness (motion sickness) is extremely common in dogs — particularly puppies whose inner ear structures are still developing. Even adult dogs who weren't acclimatised to car travel as pups can develop it.

### Signs Your Dog Has Travel Sickness:
- Excessive drooling (often the first sign)
- Yawning and restlessness
- Whining or whimpering
- Vomiting
- Lethargy or freezing in place
- Lip licking

### Why It Happens

The inner ear sends movement signals that conflict with what the eyes see — especially if the dog is facing backwards, can't see out of the window, or is in an enclosed boot. Anxiety also plays a major role: a dog who vomits once in the car may become anxious about car journeys, which triggers more nausea.

### Practical Solutions

**Before the journey:**
- Fast your dog for 2–3 hours before travel (not overnight — just skip the meal before a long trip). An empty stomach significantly reduces nausea.
- Position your dog to face forward, at window level if possible. Dogs in boots with no window visibility are more susceptible.
- Keep the car cool and well-ventilated — heat dramatically worsens nausea.

**During the journey:**
- Open windows slightly (2–3 inches) — equalising air pressure reduces sickness in humans and dogs alike.
- Drive smoothly — hard braking and cornering worsen symptoms.
- Take regular breaks (every 1–2 hours on long journeys).

**Calming and desensitisation:**
For dogs who've become anxious about car travel, a slow desensitisation programme works well:

1. **Week 1:** Sit in the stationary car with your dog. Treats, praise. No engine.
2. **Week 2:** Engine on, stationary. Treats throughout.
3. **Week 3:** Short 5-minute drives to pleasant destinations (the park, not the vet).
4. **Week 4 onwards:** Gradually increase journey length.

This process takes 3–6 weeks but is highly effective for anxious or car-sick dogs.

| **Adaptil Travel Spray** | Pheromone | Spritz crate or car 15 mins before journey; calming signal |

## Heatstroke in Cars: The Real Risk

This cannot be overstated: **dogs die in parked cars every year in the UK, and it happens faster than most people expect.**

### How Hot Does a Car Get?

| Outside Temp | Car Temp After 10 mins | Car Temp After 30 mins |
|-------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| 22°C (warm UK day) | 36°C | 47°C |
| 25°C (hot UK day) | 40°C | 52°C |
| 29°C (heatwave) | 45°C | 57°C |

Opening windows a crack reduces temperature by only 2–4°C — not enough to make a parked car safe.

**Dogs can die of heatstroke in as little as 15 minutes at these temperatures.**

### Never Leave Your Dog in a Parked Car When:
- It's above 15°C outside
- You'll be away for more than 5 minutes
- You don't have full shade AND constant ventilation (fans, air con running)

### Signs of Heatstroke in the Car

If you return to your car and your dog shows these signs, treat it as an emergency:

- Heavy, laboured panting
- Excessive drooling (thick, ropey saliva)
- Bright red gums or tongue
- Glazed, unfocused eyes
- Staggering or collapse
- Vomiting

**Emergency response — STOP WAIT COOL:**
1. **Stop** — remove from hot environment immediately
2. **Water** — move to shade, offer cool (not ice cold) water to drink
3. **Cool** — apply cool water to neck, armpits, groin, paw pads. Wet towels. Never ice.
4. **Vet** — call immediately even if dog appears to recover. Internal damage can be delayed.

**Call your vet or Animal Poison Line:** 01202 509000 (charges apply)

### Safe Summer Car Travel

- **Travel in early morning or evening** — avoid 11am–3pm in summer
- **Never leave your dog unattended** in a parked car in warm weather
- **Use a cooling mat** in the crate or on the seat
- **Keep fresh water accessible** — mount a travel water bottle to the crate or use a Kurgo Muck Mug
- **Run air con before loading your dog** — pre-cool the car before they get in

## Long-Distance Car Journeys: Keeping Your Dog Comfortable

For journeys over 2–3 hours, planning makes a real difference.

### Before You Leave

- **Last exercise** 30–60 mins before departure — tired dogs travel better
- **Pack a travel bag:** water, collapsible bowl, treats, favourite toy, poop bags, dog first aid kit
- **Familiar bedding** in the crate or on the seat — your scent + their scent reduces anxiety
- **Check your route** for dog-friendly rest stops (Motorway Services Pet Relief Areas, and apps like Bring Fido and Dog Friendly Britain)

### During the Journey

**Rest stops:**
- Every 1.5–2 hours minimum — dogs need to toilet, stretch, and hydrate
- Never let your dog off-lead at a motorway service area — traffic noise and unfamiliar scents cause flight risks even in well-trained dogs
- Use a long line (5m) for a stretch and sniff at rest areas

**In the car:**
- Don't feed a full meal during the journey — small treats only
- Fresh water available at every stop
- Keep temperature cool (dogs overheat easily even in a moving car)
- Talk to your dog — your voice is calming

### On Arrival

- Allow 15–20 minutes for your dog to decompress before you expect them to be "normal"
- First stop should be a toilet break away from the car
- Don't rush introductions to new environments — let them sniff and explore at their pace

## Breed-Specific Considerations

Some dogs have structural or temperamental traits that make car travel harder:

| Breed Type | Challenge | What Helps |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Brachycephalic (French Bulldogs, Pugs, Bulldogs)** | Breathing difficulties in heat; prone to heatstroke | AC running at all times, never in summer peak hours, frequent breaks |
| **Greyhounds/Sighthounds** | Thin skin, little body fat — no padding against hard surfaces | Deep cushioned bedding, keep temperature moderate |
| **Giant breeds (Great Dane, Mastiff)** | Doesn't fit in standard crates | Custom-fitted van or SUV with Variocage or similar |
| **Herding breeds (Border Collie, GSD)** | Highly reactive to movement, may fixate on passing traffic | Window shade, forward-facing crate, desensitisation |
| **Anxious/rescue dogs** | Travel triggers flight response | Adaptil, slow desensitisation, consult ABTC behaviourist |
| **Puppies under 12 weeks** | Inner ear not fully developed, motion sick easily | Short trips only, no long journeys until 12–16 weeks |

## Dog Car Travel: Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can my dog travel in the front seat?**
Yes, legally — but it's not recommended. Airbag deployment can injure or kill a dog in the front passenger seat. If you must use the front seat, deactivate the passenger airbag and use a properly fitted harness.

**Q: My dog barks constantly in the car. How do I stop it?**
Barking during car travel is usually anxiety, over-stimulation, or previous negative associations (the car only goes to the vet). Use the desensitisation programme above. A covered crate (calmer, less stimulation) and calming supplements (YuCALM, Adaptil) can help. If the behaviour is severe, consult an ABTC-registered behaviourist.

**Q: Is it legal to transport a dog in an open pick-up truck bed in the UK?**
No. Under the Highway Code and Road Traffic Act, this would likely be considered inadequate restraint and potentially constitute an animal welfare offence under the Animal Welfare Act 2006.

**Q: How long can a dog travel in a car?**
With regular rest stops (every 1.5–2 hours), most healthy adult dogs can manage 6–8 hours per day. Senior dogs, puppies, and brachycephalic breeds should have shorter stints of 3–4 hours maximum.

**Q: My dog refuses to get in the car. What can I do?**
This is almost always anxiety, often from a previous bad experience. Work through the desensitisation programme above — start with the car stationary, doors open, treats and play, no pressure. Build slowly. It takes weeks, not days, but the success rate is very high.

**Q: Do I need a special harness for my dog's breed?**
Most crash-tested harnesses are available in sizes that cover most breeds. The key is correct sizing — measure your dog's girth rather than going by breed label. For brachycephalic breeds, avoid back-clip harnesses that put pressure on the chest; look for designs with broader chest coverage.

## Quick Reference: Dog Car Travel Checklist

Use this before every long journey:

### Planning
- [ ] Dog walked and exercised before departure
- [ ] Dog fasted 2 hours before (long journeys)
- [ ] Route checked for dog-friendly rest stops
- [ ] Travel bag packed (water, bowl, treats, lead, poop bags, first aid kit)

### Safety
- [ ] Harness fitted and clipped in, OR crate secured and latched
- [ ] No loose items in the dog's zone
- [ ] Airbag deactivated if dog is in front seat
- [ ] Windows closed enough to prevent dog hanging head out (injury risk)

### Comfort
- [ ] Familiar bedding in crate/travel area
- [ ] Temperature cool — air con or open window
- [ ] Calming spray applied if anxious dog (15 mins before)
- [ ] Water accessible at next stop

### Ongoing
- [ ] Break every 1.5–2 hours
- [ ] Toilet stop on lead, not off-lead at services
- [ ] Check dog's temperature and breathing at each stop
- [ ] Never leave alone in parked car in warm weather
