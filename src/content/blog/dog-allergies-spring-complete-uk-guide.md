Spring is a glorious time for dog walks — longer days, bluebells in the woods, parks coming back to life. But for millions of UK dogs, it's also the start of allergy season. If your dog starts scratching obsessively, licking their paws raw, or developing red, inflamed skin every year between February and June, seasonal allergies are almost certainly the culprit.

Canine atopic dermatitis (the technical name for environmental allergies in dogs) affects an estimated **10-15% of all dogs in the UK**, according to veterinary research from the University of Nottingham. That's potentially 1.5 million itchy, uncomfortable dogs — and many owners don't realise what's causing it.

This guide covers everything UK dog owners need to know: what triggers spring allergies, how to spot the signs early, which treatments actually work (including the newest options like Cytopoint and Apoquel), and practical steps you can take at home to give your dog relief — without spending a fortune at the vet.

## What Causes Dog Allergies in Spring? {#what-causes-dog-allergies-in-spring}

Unlike humans, who tend to sneeze and get watery eyes from pollen, dogs absorb allergens primarily **through their skin**. This is why the most common symptom is itching rather than sneezing.

The main spring triggers in the UK include:

### Tree Pollen (February–May)
The earliest allergy trigger. **Birch, oak, ash, and plane trees** are the biggest offenders in the UK. Tree pollen season typically begins in late February in southern England and peaks in April. If your dog's itching starts before the grass is even growing, tree pollen is likely the cause.

### Grass Pollen (May–August)
The most common pollen allergy in both humans and dogs. **Timothy grass, ryegrass, and meadow fescue** are widespread across the UK. Grass pollen peaks in June and July but can start affecting sensitive dogs from late April.

### Weed Pollen (June–September)
**Dock, nettle, and plantain** pollen can trigger reactions later in the season. These tend to overlap with grass pollen, making summer the hardest time for allergy-prone dogs.

### Mould Spores
Often overlooked. Mould thrives in damp UK conditions, particularly in **leaf litter, compost heaps, and damp woodland floors**. Mould spore counts rise significantly in spring as the weather warms and moisture levels remain high.

### Dust Mites & Indoor Allergens
While not strictly "spring" triggers, dust mites multiply as homes warm up. If your dog seems worse indoors, dust mites could be contributing — especially if they sleep on upholstered furniture or in rooms with carpet.

> **Important note:** Flea allergy dermatitis (FAD) is actually the **most common allergy in UK dogs**, according to the PDSA. Before assuming your dog has pollen allergies, make sure their flea treatment is fully up to date. A single flea bite can cause intense itching in a sensitive dog.

## Symptoms: How to Tell If Your Dog Has Seasonal Allergies {#symptoms-how-to-tell-if-your-dog-has-seasonal-allergies}

Dogs show allergies very differently to humans. Here's what to watch for:

### Primary Symptoms

| Symptom | What It Looks Like | Where to Check |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Excessive scratching** | Persistent scratching, especially after walks | Ears, armpits, groin, belly |
| **Paw licking/chewing** | Compulsive licking, pink/brown staining on paws | Between toes, paw pads |
| **Red, inflamed skin** | Visible redness, warmth to the touch | Belly, inner thighs, ears |
| **Ear infections** | Head shaking, brown/yellow discharge, smell | Inner ear flap, ear canal |
| **Hot spots** | Moist, oozing patches of infected skin | Any area, common on hips/flanks |
| **Hair loss** | Patchy thinning, especially from scratching | Around eyes, paws, flanks |

### Secondary Symptoms

- **Watery or red eyes** — less common than in humans but does occur
- **Sneezing** — occasional, usually mild
- **Excessive shedding** — beyond normal spring moult
- **Scooting** — can indicate anal gland issues triggered by allergies
- **Behavioural changes** — restlessness, poor sleep, irritability (they're uncomfortable!)

### The "Seasonal Pattern" Test

The biggest clue that your dog has environmental allergies rather than food allergies is **timing**. Ask yourself:

- Does the itching start in spring and ease in winter? → **Environmental allergy**
- Is the itching year-round, regardless of season? → **Could be food allergy or dust mites**
- Did the itching start suddenly in a dog over 1 year old? → **Likely environmental**
- Has the itching been present since puppyhood? → **Could be food-related**

Most dogs develop seasonal allergies between **1 and 3 years of age**. If your puppy is itchy, food allergies or parasites are more likely culprits.

## When to See the Vet (and What to Expect) {#when-to-see-the-vet}

### See your vet if:

- Itching is severe enough to disrupt sleep or daily life
- Skin is broken, bleeding, or oozing
- You notice ear discharge or a bad smell from the ears
- The dog is losing patches of fur
- Over-the-counter treatments haven't helped after 2 weeks
- You suspect a secondary infection (bacterial or yeast)

### What to expect at the appointment

**Initial consultation (£40–£70):**
Your vet will examine the skin, check ears, and take a history. They'll want to rule out fleas, mites, and fungal infections first.

**Possible tests:**
- **Skin scrape** — rules out mites (Sarcoptic mange looks similar to allergies)
- **Cytology** — checks for yeast or bacterial infection on the skin
- **Blood test (serology)** — identifies specific allergens (£200–£400)
- **Intradermal skin testing** — the "gold standard" for allergy testing, usually done by a veterinary dermatologist (£300–£500)
- **Exclusion diet** — to rule out food allergies (8–12 weeks of strict diet trial)

> **Cost-saving tip:** Many vets offer **allergy packages** that bundle testing and follow-ups at a reduced rate. PDSA provides free and low-cost vet care for qualifying pet owners. Check eligibility at [pdsa.org.uk](https://www.pdsa.org.uk).

## Treatments That Actually Work {#treatments-that-actually-work}

There's no cure for canine atopic dermatitis, but modern treatments can manage symptoms incredibly effectively. Here's what's available in the UK in 2026:

### 1. Apoquel (Oclacitinib) — Prescription

**What it is:** An oral tablet that blocks the itch signal in your dog's body by targeting a specific enzyme (JAK1). Developed by Zoetis, it's been a game-changer since its UK launch.

**Pros:**
- Works within 4 hours, full effect in 24 hours
- Very effective for most dogs
- Can be used long-term
- Daily tablet — easy to administer

**Cons:**
- Prescription-only (requires vet visit)
- Can suppress immune function slightly — not ideal for dogs with existing infections
- Not recommended for dogs under 12 months
- Costs approximately **£1.50–£2.50 per tablet** depending on dog size

**Best for:** Dogs with moderate to severe seasonal itching who need fast, reliable relief.

### 2. Cytopoint (Lokivetmab) — Prescription Injection

**What it is:** A monthly injection given by your vet that uses monoclonal antibodies to neutralise the itch-signalling protein (IL-31). Think of it as a precisely targeted missile that takes out the itch without affecting the rest of the immune system.

**Pros:**
- One injection lasts 4–8 weeks
- No daily tablets to remember
- Very targeted — minimal side effects
- Safe for dogs of all ages (including puppies)

**Cons:**
- Requires a vet visit for each injection
- Costs **£60–£120 per injection** depending on dog size
- Doesn't work for every dog (some don't respond)
- Only targets itch — doesn't treat underlying inflammation

**Best for:** Owners who prefer a "set and forget" approach, dogs who won't take tablets, or dogs with other health conditions where daily medication is risky.

### 3. Antihistamines — Over-the-Counter

**What to know:** Antihistamines work well in humans but are **much less effective in dogs**. Studies show they help only about 10-30% of allergic dogs. However, they're cheap and safe, so worth trying first.

**Options (always check with your vet first):**
- **Chlorphenamine (Piriton)** — The most commonly recommended. Dose: 4mg per 10-20kg of body weight, 2-3 times daily. *Only use the plain chlorphenamine version — NOT Piriton Allergy-S or any version with added decongestants, which can be toxic to dogs.*
- **Cetirizine (Piriteze)** — Less sedating. Your vet can advise on dosing.
- **Loratadine** — Another non-sedating option, but less evidence in dogs.

> **⚠️ NEVER give your dog antihistamines without consulting your vet first.** Some formulations contain ingredients like pseudoephedrine or xylitol that are **toxic to dogs**. The PDSA specifically warns against giving human medications without veterinary guidance.

**Cost:** £3–£8 from any pharmacy.

### 4. Corticosteroids — Prescription

**What to know:** Steroids like prednisolone are very effective at stopping itch and inflammation. They're cheap and fast-acting — but long-term use causes significant side effects (increased thirst/urination, weight gain, diabetes risk, thinning skin).

**Best for:** Short bursts during severe flare-ups, or while waiting for Apoquel/Cytopoint to kick in.

**Cost:** Very cheap — £10–£20 per course.

### 5. Immunotherapy (Desensitisation) — Specialist

**What it is:** After allergy testing identifies your dog's specific triggers, a custom vaccine is created. Your dog receives gradually increasing doses to build tolerance over 6–12 months.

**Pros:**
- The only treatment that addresses the **root cause**
- 60-70% of dogs show significant improvement
- Once established, may reduce or eliminate need for other medications
- Can be given at home (subcutaneous injections or sublingual drops)

**Cons:**
- Requires specialist allergy testing first (£300–£500)
- Takes 6–12 months to see full benefit
- Ongoing cost of ~£50–£80 per month for the vaccine
- Doesn't work for all dogs

**Best for:** Dogs with severe, multi-season allergies where long-term medication costs would be higher than immunotherapy.

### Treatment Comparison at a Glance

| Treatment | Speed | Effectiveness | Monthly Cost | Side Effects |
|-----------|-------|--------------|-------------|--------------|
| Apoquel | Hours | High (80%+) | £45–£75 | Low-moderate |
| Cytopoint | Days | High (70-80%) | £60–£120 | Very low |
| Antihistamines | Days | Low (10-30%) | £3–£8 | Drowsiness |
| Steroids | Hours | Very high | £10–£20 | High (long-term) |

## Home Remedies & Prevention Tips {#home-remedies-and-prevention-tips}

You can significantly reduce your dog's allergy symptoms with these practical steps — many of which cost nothing:

### After Every Walk

1. **Wipe down your dog's paws and belly** with a damp cloth or unscented baby wipes. This removes pollen before it's absorbed through the skin. Pay attention to between the toes.
2. **Brush their coat** to remove pollen trapped in fur — do this outside to avoid bringing it into the house.
3. **Time your walks.** Pollen counts are typically highest in the **early morning (5-10am) and early evening**. Midday walks often have lower pollen levels. Check the Met Office pollen forecast at [metoffice.gov.uk/weather/warnings-and-advice/seasonal-advice/pollen-forecast](https://www.metoffice.gov.uk/weather/warnings-and-advice/seasonal-advice/pollen-forecast).

### At Home

4. **Wash bedding weekly** at 60°C to kill dust mites and remove pollen.
5. **Use non-biological detergent** for your dog's bedding — biological detergents contain enzymes that can irritate sensitive skin (recommended by the PDSA).
6. **Hoover regularly** — especially if you have carpet. Consider a HEPA filter vacuum.
7. **Avoid scented products** near your dog — air fresheners, plug-ins, and scented candles can all irritate already-sensitised skin.
8. **Keep windows closed** on high pollen days, especially in bedrooms where your dog sleeps.

### Bathing & Skin Care

9. **Bathe your dog every 1-2 weeks** during allergy season with a **medicated oatmeal shampoo** or one containing chlorhexidine (for bacterial prevention). Oatmeal soothes inflamed skin and helps restore the skin barrier.
10. **Don't over-bathe** — more than twice a week strips natural oils and makes skin worse.
11. **Consider a leave-on conditioner** or skin spray for dogs with very dry, irritated skin.

### Diet & Supplements

12. **Omega-3 fatty acids** (fish oil) are the most evidence-backed supplement for skin health. They help strengthen the skin barrier and reduce inflammation. Look for products with high EPA/DHA content.
13. **Feed a high-quality diet** — a well-nourished dog has a healthier skin barrier, which means fewer allergens get through.
14. **Probiotics** — emerging research suggests gut health influences skin allergies. It's not a proven treatment yet, but there's growing veterinary interest.

### Garden Management

15. **Keep grass short** — reduces pollen production in your own garden.
16. **Avoid composting near the house** — mould spores from compost heaps are a common trigger.
17. **Rinse your dog after garden play** — a quick hose-down on warm days can make a big difference.

## Seasonal Allergy Calendar: Month-by-Month UK Guide {#seasonal-allergy-calendar}

Knowing what's in the air each month helps you prepare and manage symptoms proactively.

| Month | Main Triggers | Severity | Action |
|-------|--------------|----------|--------|
| **February** | Early tree pollen (alder, hazel) | Low-Moderate | Start omega-3 supplements; watch for early signs |
| **March** | Tree pollen builds (birch, willow) | Moderate | Begin paw-wiping routine; consider antihistamines |
| **April** | Peak tree pollen (birch, oak, ash) | High | Full allergy management; vet visit if symptoms worsen |
| **May** | Tree pollen + grass pollen starts | Very High | Dual trigger period — most dogs peak here |
| **June** | Peak grass pollen | Very High | Highest pollen counts; limit walks on hot, windy days |
| **July** | Grass pollen + weed pollen starts | High | Maintain all precautions; check for secondary infections |
| **August** | Grass + weed pollen | Moderate-High | Symptoms may start easing for some dogs |
| **September** | Late weed pollen + mould spores | Moderate | Mould from damp conditions; watch leaf litter |
| **October** | Mould spores | Low-Moderate | Wind-down period; review treatment plan for next year |
| **November–January** | Low allergen period | Low | Recovery time; maintain skin barrier with omega-3 |

> **Tip:** Sign up for pollen alerts from the Met Office and check the forecast before morning walks. On "very high" pollen days, shorter walks on hard surfaces (pavements, tracks) expose your dog to less pollen than romps through long grass.

## FAQs {#faqs}

### Can dogs take Piriton?
Plain chlorphenamine (original Piriton) can be given to dogs **under veterinary guidance only**. Never use Piriton variants that contain decongestants or other active ingredients — these can be toxic. Your vet will advise the correct dosage based on your dog's weight and health history.

### Are dog allergies getting worse in the UK?
There's emerging evidence that climate change is extending the UK pollen season and increasing pollen counts. Warmer springs mean trees and grasses produce pollen earlier and for longer. This likely means more dogs will develop allergies, and those already affected may have longer symptom periods.

### Can I use coconut oil on my dog's itchy skin?
Coconut oil is safe for dogs and can provide temporary moisture relief for dry, flaky skin. However, it won't treat the underlying allergy and can sometimes worsen symptoms by trapping heat in inflamed skin. It's a supplementary measure, not a treatment.

### Should I get my dog allergy tested?
If your dog's allergies are mild and respond to basic management (paw wiping, omega-3, occasional antihistamines), testing may not be necessary. But if symptoms are severe, recurrent, or require long-term medication, allergy testing can identify specific triggers and make immunotherapy (desensitisation) possible — which is the only treatment that addresses the root cause.

### How much does treating dog allergies cost in the UK?
Costs vary enormously depending on severity:
- **Mild:** £5–£20/month (supplements, shampoo, antihistamines)
- **Moderate:** £50–£100/month (Apoquel or Cytopoint)
- **Severe:** £150–£300/month (specialist care, immunotherapy, multiple medications)

Pet insurance can cover allergy treatment if the policy was in place before symptoms developed. Check your policy's exclusions carefully.

### My dog only itches in spring — is it definitely allergies?
Most likely, yes. The seasonal pattern is the strongest indicator of environmental allergies. However, fleas are also more active in spring, and some dogs develop seasonal anxiety that manifests as excessive licking. A vet can help distinguish between these causes.

## The Bottom Line

Spring allergies in dogs are common, manageable, and — with the right approach — shouldn't stop your dog from enjoying the best season for walks. The key is early action: start omega-3 supplements in February, establish a paw-wiping routine before pollen peaks, and don't wait for severe symptoms before seeing your vet.

Modern treatments like Apoquel and Cytopoint have transformed allergy management for UK dogs. Combined with practical home measures, most allergic dogs can live comfortably through spring and summer with minimal disruption.

If your dog is itching right now, start with the basics: check flea treatment is up to date, wipe paws after every walk, and book a vet appointment if symptoms are affecting their quality of life. Spring should be enjoyed — by both ends of the lead.
