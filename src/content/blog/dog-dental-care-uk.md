# Dog Dental Care: The Complete UK Guide (2026)

## Why Dog Dental Care Matters More Than You Think

Here's a stat that surprises most dog owners: **dental disease is the second most common health problem in dogs**, beaten only by ear infections. By the age of three, the vast majority of dogs will already show some signs of gum disease.

Yet despite this, dental care is one of the most overlooked parts of dog ownership in the UK. We wouldn't dream of skipping our own dentist appointments, but many of us have never so much as glanced at our dog's teeth.

The consequences go beyond bad breath. Untreated dental disease can lead to chronic pain, tooth loss, and in severe cases, bacteria entering the bloodstream and affecting the heart, kidneys, and liver. It's serious stuff — and it's almost entirely preventable.

This guide covers everything you need to know about dog dental care in the UK: how to brush your dog's teeth (even if they hate it), what products actually work, how much professional treatment costs, and how to spot problems early.

## Understanding Your Dog's Teeth

Dogs have 42 adult teeth — 20 on top and 22 on the bottom. Puppies start with 28 baby teeth, which begin falling out around 3–4 months of age and are usually fully replaced by 6–7 months.

### How Dental Disease Develops

The progression follows a predictable pattern:

1. **Plaque forms** — A sticky, colourless film of bacteria builds up on teeth after eating. This happens within hours.
2. **Plaque hardens into tartar** — If not removed within 24–48 hours, plaque mineralises into tartar (also called calculus) — a hard, yellowish-brown deposit you can't brush off.
3. **Gingivitis develops** — Tartar irritates the gums, causing redness, swelling, and bleeding. At this stage, it's still reversible.
4. **Periodontal disease sets in** — If left untreated, the infection spreads below the gum line, damaging the bone and tissue supporting the teeth. This is irreversible and painful.

### Breeds at Higher Risk

Some dogs are more prone to dental problems than others:

- **Small breeds** — Chihuahuas, Yorkshire Terriers, Dachshunds, and Toy Poodles often have crowded teeth, making plaque harder to remove
- **Flat-faced (brachycephalic) breeds** — Pugs, French Bulldogs, and Shih Tzus have misaligned jaws and crowded teeth
- **Greyhounds** — Known for poor dental health, often needing extractions
- **Cavalier King Charles Spaniels** — Prone to early-onset dental disease

If your dog falls into any of these categories, dental care needs to be a top priority from day one.

## How to Brush Your Dog's Teeth: A Step-by-Step Guide

Daily brushing is the single most effective thing you can do for your dog's dental health. The RSPCA, Dogs Trust, and Blue Cross all recommend it. Here's how to do it properly — even if your dog has never had a toothbrush near their mouth.

### What You'll Need

- **Dog-specific toothpaste** — Never use human toothpaste (fluoride and xylitol are toxic to dogs). Dog toothpastes come in flavours like chicken, beef, and liver — much more appealing to your pup.
- **A dog toothbrush** — You can use a finger brush (rubber thimble), a long-handled dog toothbrush, or a child-sized soft-bristle toothbrush in a pinch.

### The Gradual Introduction Method (Dogs Trust Recommended)

**Don't rush this.** If your dog hasn't had their teeth brushed before, forcing it will make things worse. This process takes 1–2 weeks.

**Week 1: Getting comfortable**

1. **Day 1–2:** Let your dog sniff and lick the toothpaste from your finger. Reward them. That's it.
2. **Day 3–4:** Gently lift your dog's lip and touch the outside of their teeth with your finger (with toothpaste). Mark the behaviour with "yes" or "good" and reward.
3. **Day 5–7:** Rub your finger along the outer surfaces of the front teeth for a few seconds. Build up gradually.

**Week 2: Introducing the brush**

4. **Day 8–9:** Let your dog lick toothpaste off the brush. Reward.
5. **Day 10–11:** Gently brush the front teeth in small circular motions. Just a few teeth — don't force the issue.
6. **Day 12–14:** Gradually increase to brushing all teeth, focusing on the outer surfaces where plaque builds up most.

### Brushing Tips That Actually Help

- **Aim for daily** — Even 2–3 times per week makes a significant difference
- **Focus on the outside surfaces** — The tongue does a reasonable job of cleaning the inside surfaces
- **Use the chin rest technique** — Teach your dog to rest their chin on your open palm. It keeps their head still and makes the process calmer
- **Keep sessions short** — 30–60 seconds is enough. It doesn't need to be a marathon
- **Always end on a positive note** — Stop before your dog gets stressed, and reward afterwards
- **Best time:** After their evening meal, as part of the wind-down routine

### If Your Dog Really Won't Tolerate Brushing

Some dogs — especially older rescues or dogs with existing dental pain — genuinely struggle with tooth brushing. In these cases:

- Try a **finger brush** instead (less intimidating than a standard brush)
- Use a **dental gel** you can apply to the gums without brushing
- Rely more heavily on **dental chews and water additives** (see below)
- Speak to your vet or a qualified behaviourist for help with desensitisation

## Spotting Dental Problems Early

Dogs are remarkably good at hiding pain. Many will continue eating normally even with severe dental disease. Watch for these warning signs:

### The Red Flags

- **Bad breath** — Some "dog breath" is normal, but persistent foul odour (especially fishy) indicates infection
- **Red, swollen, or bleeding gums** — Gingivitis often appears as a thin red line along the gum
- **Yellow/brown deposits on teeth** — Visible tartar buildup
- **Drooling more than usual** — Especially blood-tinged saliva
- **Pawing at the mouth or rubbing their face**
- **Reluctance to eat hard food** — Preferring wet food or eating on one side
- **Loose, broken, or missing teeth**
- **Swelling on the face** — Could indicate a tooth root abscess
- **Dropping food while eating**

### When to See the Vet

If you spot any of the above signs, book a vet appointment promptly. Many veterinary practices offer **free dental clinics** or nurse checks as part of their pet care plans — always worth asking.

Your vet should check your dog's teeth at their annual health check, but don't wait for the yearly appointment if something looks wrong.

## Professional Dental Treatment: What to Expect & How Much It Costs

When home care isn't enough, your vet may recommend a professional dental procedure.

### What Happens During a Professional Dental Clean

1. **Pre-anaesthetic blood test** — To check your dog is healthy enough for a general anaesthetic
2. **General anaesthetic** — Essential for thorough examination and treatment. Your dog needs to be completely still.
3. **Full oral examination** — Checking every tooth, probing for pockets, assessing gum health
4. **Dental X-rays** — To see what's happening below the gum line (many problems are invisible from the surface)
5. **Scaling** — Ultrasonic removal of plaque and tartar above and below the gum line
6. **Polishing** — Smoothing the tooth surfaces to slow future plaque buildup
7. **Extractions** — If teeth are too damaged to save, they'll be removed

### How Much Does It Cost in the UK? (2026 Prices)

Professional dental treatment is one of the most common — and expensive — vet bills UK dog owners face.

## Dental Care by Life Stage

### Puppies (Up to 12 Months)

- Start handling their mouth from day one — lift lips, touch teeth, get them comfortable
- Begin brushing as soon as you bring them home (even with baby teeth)
- Teething occurs around 3–7 months — provide appropriate chew toys to relieve discomfort
- Frozen KONGs and cold carrots can soothe sore gums during teething
- Check that all baby teeth have fallen out by 7 months — retained baby teeth need vet attention

### Adult Dogs (1–7 Years)

- Daily brushing is the target; 3 times weekly minimum
- Annual dental check at the vet
- Supplement with dental chews and appropriate toys
- Watch for early signs of gingivitis (reversible if caught early)

### Senior Dogs (7+ Years)

- Dental disease accelerates with age — be extra vigilant
- More frequent vet dental checks (every 6 months)
- Older dogs may need softer dental chews
- Anaesthetic risk is slightly higher for seniors — your vet will assess this
- Don't assume "old dog breath" is normal — it usually indicates a problem

## Dog Dental Care FAQ

### How often should I brush my dog's teeth?
Daily is ideal. The RSPCA recommends brushing every day, but even 2–3 times per week makes a meaningful difference. Plaque hardens into tartar within 24–48 hours, so daily brushing prevents the cycle from starting.

### Can I use human toothpaste on my dog?
**No.** Human toothpaste contains fluoride and often xylitol, both of which are toxic to dogs. Always use a toothpaste formulated specifically for dogs.

### My dog won't let me brush their teeth. What should I do?
Go back to basics with the gradual introduction method. If your dog is anxious or aggressive around mouth handling, consult your vet or a qualified behaviourist. In the meantime, use dental gels, water additives, and VOHC-approved dental chews.

### Are bones good for cleaning dogs' teeth?
Raw meaty bones can help, but they carry risks including tooth fractures, constipation, and internal damage from splinters. Cooked bones should **never** be given as they're more likely to splinter. The RSPCA recommends consulting your vet before giving bones, and always supervising.

### How do I know if my dog has a toothache?
Dogs hide pain well. Look for subtle signs: reluctance to eat hard food, chewing on one side, pawing at the mouth, drooling, bad breath, or behavioural changes like being more withdrawn or irritable.

### At what age do dogs need dental treatment?
There's no set age — it depends on the individual dog, breed, diet, and home care routine. Some dogs need their first dental clean as young as 2–3 years old, while others may not need one until much later. Regular vet checks are the best way to stay on top of it.

Dog dental care doesn't have to be complicated. Start brushing early (or start now, even if your dog is older), use dental chews as a supplement, and keep an eye out for warning signs. A few minutes of daily prevention is worth far more than a £500 vet bill and a dog in pain.
