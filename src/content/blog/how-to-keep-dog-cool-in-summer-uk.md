## Introduction

Every summer, the UK gets warmer — and every summer, UK vets treat hundreds of dogs for heatstroke that could have been prevented.

The brutal truth: dogs can't sweat through their skin. They cool themselves almost entirely by panting, which is far less efficient than sweating. On a warm British day (even 20°C feels hot to a double-coated dog), the risk of overheating is very real — and it escalates fast.

This guide covers everything: danger temperatures, the products that actually work, what to do if you think your dog has heatstroke, and how different breeds need different approaches. Bookmark it before summer arrives.

## 🌡️ What Temperature Is Too Hot for Dogs?

Most vets agree that **20°C and above** warrants extra caution for exercise, and **25°C+** is genuinely dangerous without proper management.

| Air Temperature | Risk Level | Advice |
|----------------|-----------|--------|
| Under 15°C | ✅ Safe | Normal activity |
| 15–19°C | ✅ Fine | Normal activity for most breeds |
| 20–24°C | ⚠️ Caution | Reduce intensity, carry water |
| 25–28°C | 🔴 High risk | Early morning/late evening walks only |
| 29°C+ | 🚨 Dangerous | No outdoor exercise except toilet breaks |

**Pavement test:** Place your hand flat on the pavement for 7 seconds. If it's uncomfortable for you, it's burning your dog's paws. Tarmac can reach 70°C when the air temperature is just 25°C.

**Car temperatures:** A car parked in 22°C sun can reach 47°C within an hour. Dogs left in cars die — even with windows cracked. In England and Wales it is not illegal to break a car window to rescue a dog, but you must call 999 first and stay at the scene.

## Signs of Heatstroke in Dogs

Heatstroke is a veterinary emergency. If you see these signs, act immediately — you have minutes, not hours.

**Early warning signs:**
- Excessive, frantic panting
- Drooling more than usual
- Bright red gums or tongue
- Restlessness, can't settle
- Seeking shade or lying flat on cool surfaces

**Escalating signs (emergency):**
- Vomiting or diarrhoea
- Glazed, distant eyes
- Weakness or stumbling
- Loss of coordination
- Collapse

**What to do right now:**
1. Move them to shade or indoors immediately
2. Pour cool (NOT cold or iced) water over the neck, armpits, and groin — these are the body's main heat exchange points
3. Let them drink small amounts if they can
4. Fan them to help evaporation
5. Call your vet and go — don't wait to see if they improve
6. Never cover them in wet towels (traps heat)

Sources: PDSA, Blue Cross, RSPCA Heatstroke Guidelines 2024

## 10 Ways to Keep Your Dog Cool in Summer

### 1. Time Walks Correctly

The single most impactful change. Walk before 8am and after 7pm on warm days. This isn't just about air temperature — pavement temperature peaks 2–3 hours after peak sun.

A quick morning garden toilet break mid-day is fine. A 45-minute walk at noon in July isn't.

### 7. Create Shade in Your Garden

If your dog spends time in the garden, make sure there's adequate shade that moves with the sun. A spot shaded at 10am may be in full sun by noon.

Options:
- Patio umbrella or sun sail
- Pop-up canopy tent (useful for picnics too)
- Dense planting along south-facing walls

### 8. Wet Their Paws and Belly

If you don't have a pool, a wet towel or gentle garden hose works surprisingly well when applied to the right areas:

- **Paw pads** — large surface area for heat exchange
- **Belly/groin area** — minimal fur, direct blood vessel access
- **Neck and armpits** — where the body naturally dissipates heat

Avoid cold shock — room-temperature water is ideal. Ice-cold water can cause blood vessels to constrict, which actually traps heat inside.

### 9. Keep Indoors Cool

Curtains closed on the sunny side of the house make a significant difference. Fans help when paired with a damp towel hung in front of them (DIY evaporative cooler).

Dogs naturally seek the coolest spot — bathroom tiles and kitchen floors are often their choice in summer. Let them. Don't force them onto a dog bed if they've decided the hallway tiles are the place to be.

If you have an electric fan, create airflow rather than pointing it directly at your dog — most dogs don't love the direct blast.

### 10. Adjust Grooming (But Don't Shave Double-Coated Breeds)

Regular brushing during summer helps remove dead undercoat that traps heat. For many breeds, a thorough groom before summer makes a real difference.

**The double-coat myth:** Never shave a double-coated breed (Husky, Golden Retriever, German Shepherd, Border Collie). Their double coat works as insulation in both directions — it keeps heat out in summer as well as warmth in during winter. Shaving destroys this system and can damage the coat permanently.

Single-coated breeds (Greyhounds, Boxers, French Bulldogs) have less natural insulation and may overheat more quickly — they need extra vigilance in hot weather.

## Breed-Specific Risk: Who Needs Extra Care?

### 🔴 Highest Risk

**Brachycephalic breeds (flat-faced):** French Bulldogs, English Bulldogs, Pugs, Boxers, Shih Tzus, Cavalier King Charles Spaniels

These breeds have restricted airways that make panting — their only cooling mechanism — significantly less effective. Many vets recommend avoiding ALL outdoor exercise above 20°C for severe brachycephalic dogs. If your French Bulldog has had BOAS surgery, follow your vet's specific guidance.

Signs of overheating in brachycephalic breeds can appear faster and with less warning. Know their baseline breathing, and if anything changes — act fast.

**Obese dogs:** Extra body fat acts as insulation, preventing heat escape. Overweight dogs are at dramatically higher risk in hot weather. If your dog is carrying extra weight, summer is an excellent motivator to address it.

**Dogs on certain medications:** Some antihistamines, diuretics, and cardiac medications affect heat regulation. Check with your vet.

### 🟡 Elevated Risk

**Double-coated/heavy-coated breeds:** Huskies, Samoyeds, Chow Chows, Bernese Mountain Dogs

Ironically, their insulating coats do help in summer — but they're still at greater risk than light-coated breeds if exposed to prolonged heat. They were bred for cold climates and don't thermoregulate as efficiently.

**Puppies and senior dogs:** Less efficient temperature regulation than prime-age adults. Puppies especially can overheat quickly during play without realising it.

**Dogs with heart or respiratory conditions:** Anything that compromises circulation or breathing increases heatstroke risk.

### 🟢 Lower Risk (But Not Zero Risk)

Short-coated, medium-energy breeds in normal health. But remember — no dog is immune to heatstroke. Every summer, fit, healthy working-age Labradors end up in emergency vet practices.

## Safe Summer Exercise: What to Do Instead

When outdoor walks aren't safe, dogs still need mental and physical stimulation.

**Indoor exercise:**
- Scent games — hide treats around the house
- Learning new tricks (highly tiring mentally)
- Snuffle mats and puzzle feeders
- Tug games in air-conditioned rooms
- Recall practice up and down stairs

**Water-based exercise:**
- Paddling pools in shaded garden
- Dog-friendly lakes and rivers (check local Blue Green Algae warnings — see UKHSA map)
- Dog-friendly lido or outdoor swim venue (increasingly popular in UK)
- Early morning wild swimming — coolest at dawn

**Blue-Green Algae Warning (IMPORTANT):** Blue-green algae blooms appear in warm weather in still water — ponds, lakes, reservoirs. It can be fatal to dogs within hours of contact. Before any water swimming, check the UK Environment Agency bloom map or look for visual warning signs (green/blue scum on the surface). If in any doubt, don't let them in.

## Car Travel in Summer

If you must travel with your dog in summer:

- Use a sunshade on rear windows — children's sunshades work perfectly
- Keep the car moving with air con running — parked cars heat up fast even with windows cracked
- Bring frozen water bottles to place near (not directly on) your dog
- Stop every 90 minutes minimum for water and shade
- Never leave them in the car unattended — not even for 2 minutes

## Paw Pad Care in Summer

Paw pads can burn on hot tarmac and sand. Signs include: excessive licking of paws, limping, pads appearing red or blistered.

**Prevention:** Walk on grass wherever possible. Use the 7-second hand test. Avoid midday walks.

**Treatment:** Rinse in cool water, keep clean, apply paw balm, contact your vet if blistering occurs.

## Summer Hydration: How Much Should Dogs Drink?

A general guide: dogs need approximately 50–60ml of water per kg of body weight per day at rest — more in heat or during exercise.

| Dog Weight | Daily Water at Rest | Hot Day Requirement |
|------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 5kg (small) | 250–300ml | Up to 500ml |
| 15kg (medium) | 750–900ml | Up to 1.5L |
| 30kg (large) | 1.5–1.8L | Up to 3L |
| 45kg (giant) | 2.25–2.7L | Up to 4.5L |

Signs of dehydration: dry, sticky gums; skin that stays "tented" when pinched rather than springing back; lethargy; dark urine.

## 6 Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I take my dog swimming in the sea?**
A: Yes, with precautions. Rinse off immediately after (saltwater irritates skin and is harmful if swallowed in quantity). Avoid strong currents. Check for jellyfish. Don't let them drink seawater. Swimming dogs can tire quickly — watch for signs of exhaustion.

**Q: Is it OK to shave my dog for summer?**
A: Only single-coated breeds. Never shave double-coated breeds — their coat insulates against heat as well as cold. Regular brushing to remove dead undercoat is the right approach.

**Q: My dog loves running — how do I manage exercise in summer?**
A: Shift completely to early morning (before 8am) and late evening (after 7pm). Bring water on every outing regardless of distance. If your dog is panting heavily within 10 minutes of moderate exercise, conditions are too warm — turn back.

**Q: What temperature is safe for dogs in a car?**
A: With air conditioning running and the car moving, most normal-health dogs can handle typical UK summer temperatures. Parked cars are the danger — never leave dogs unattended in a stationary car in warm weather.

**Q: Are ice cubes safe for dogs?**
A: Yes. Ice cubes in water bowls are safe and help keep water cool. Ice lollies made from dog-safe ingredients are also fine. The old concern about ice causing bloat (GDV) has been largely debunked — it's exercising after drinking large amounts of water rapidly that's the real risk factor.

**Q: My dog is obsessed with sunbathing — should I stop them?**
A: Some dogs love sunbathing and will self-regulate by moving to shade when they get too warm. But not all dogs do — particularly brachycephalic breeds, which can overheat without obvious distress signals. Create shade options and watch their behaviour. If they're panting heavily while sunbathing, redirect them indoors.

## Summary

Summer in the UK is getting hotter, and dogs are suffering for it. The simple steps — walk early/late, provide shade and water, invest in a cooling mat — can genuinely save your dog's life.

The breeds most at risk (flat-faced, obese, senior) need the most vigilance. But every dog owner should know the signs of heatstroke and have a plan for acting on them.

The best summer for your dog is a cool, comfortable one spent mostly in the shade with a frozen Kong and a paddling pool. They don't need a heatwave adventure — they need you to look out for them.
