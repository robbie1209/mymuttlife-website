## Introduction

Take a look at your dog right now. Can you see their waist? Can you feel their ribs without pressing hard?

If the answer is no — you're not alone. According to the **PDSA Animal Wellbeing (PAW) Report 2025**, over **51% of UK dogs are overweight or obese**. That's more than half of all pet dogs in Britain carrying excess weight that's silently shortening their life.

The hard truth: obesity in dogs isn't just a cosmetic issue. It's linked to **diabetes, arthritis, heart disease, breathing problems, and cancer**. Overweight dogs live an average of **18 months less** than dogs at a healthy weight (PDSA, 2024).

The good news? Unlike many health conditions, this one is entirely reversible — and you're already taking the first step by reading this.

This guide covers everything you need to help your dog reach and maintain a healthy weight safely, without crash diets or miserable walks.

## Is My Dog Overweight? How to Tell

Vets use a system called the **Body Condition Score (BCS)** to assess weight objectively — it's more useful than the scale alone, because healthy weight varies enormously between breeds.

### The 5-Point BCS Scale (WSAVA)

| Score | Description | What you see/feel |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Severely underweight | Ribs, spine, hips visible from a distance. No fat cover. |
| 2 | Underweight | Ribs easily visible. Waist very prominent. |
| **3** | **Ideal** | **Ribs felt easily but not seen. Waist visible from above. Tuck visible from side.** |
| 4 | Overweight | Ribs hard to feel under fat layer. Waist barely visible. |
| 5 | Obese | Ribs cannot be felt. No waist. Heavy fat deposits on chest, back, tail. |

### The Home Check (30 seconds)

1. **Rib test:** Run both thumbs along your dog's spine and feel outward. The ribs should feel like knuckles on the back of your hand — firm bumps with light cover. If they feel like knuckles on your palm (covered in flesh) — overweight.

2. **Waist check:** Look down at your dog from above. There should be a clear inward curve behind the ribcage. No curve = overweight.

3. **Tuck check:** Look from the side. The belly should tuck up from the ribcage toward the hind legs. A level or downward-hanging belly = overweight.

If two or three of these are off, it's worth talking to your vet. Most practices offer **free weight clinics** — no appointment needed.

## Why Is My Dog Overweight? The Real Causes

Before you feel guilty, it's worth understanding *why* dogs gain weight — because the answer isn't always "too many treats."

### 1. Overfeeding (the most common cause)
Most owners significantly underestimate how much food they're giving. **A study from the University of Liverpool (2023)** found that the majority of owners either don't weigh food at all or consistently over-pour by 20–30%.

Bag feeding guidelines are also notoriously generous — manufacturers have a commercial incentive to suggest higher volumes.

### 2. Too many treats
A single Bonio biscuit contains around 65 calories. A dentastix is 70 calories. For a 15kg Spaniel with a daily calorie allowance of ~620 calories, two treats = 20% of their daily intake — before any food. These add up faster than most owners realise.

### 3. Neutering
Neutered dogs have a **lower metabolic rate** than intact dogs — often 20–30% lower. Many owners don't adjust food intake after neutering, leading to gradual weight gain over months.

### 4. Age
Senior dogs (7+ years) naturally become less active and their metabolism slows. A portion that was perfect at 4 years old is too much at 8.

### 5. Breed predisposition
Some breeds are genuinely more prone to weight gain:
- **Labrador Retrievers** — a gene variant (POMC deletion) affecting hunger signals is present in ~25% of Labs
- **Cocker Spaniels** — high appetite, lower activity needs than appearance suggests
- **Cavalier King Charles Spaniels** — often have lower energy requirements
- **Dachshunds** — back problems reduce activity, weight gain follows
- **Basset Hounds** — naturally slow metabolism
- **Beagles** — food-motivated, will overeat if allowed

### 6. Medical conditions
If your dog has gained weight despite no change in diet or exercise, it's worth ruling out:
- **Hypothyroidism** — underactive thyroid, common in older dogs
- **Cushing's disease** — excess cortisol, causes pot-belly appearance
- **Steroid medication** — prednisolone and similar drugs cause weight gain

Always get a vet check before starting a weight loss programme. Don't assume it's simple overfeeding.

## Safe Dog Weight Loss: The Vet-Approved Approach

### Rule 1: Lose weight slowly
The safe rate of weight loss for dogs is **1–2% of body weight per week**. For a 30kg Labrador, that's 300–600g per week. Faster than this causes muscle loss and can stress internal organs.

Expect the full programme to take **3–6 months** for moderate weight loss (10–15% of body weight). There are no shortcuts worth taking.

### Rule 2: Get a vet baseline first
Before changing anything:
- Weigh your dog at the vet (home scales aren't accurate enough for small losses)
- Get blood tests to rule out thyroid/hormonal issues
- Establish the **target weight** with your vet's help
- Calculate daily calorie allowance

Most vets will give you a **calorie target** rather than a food volume — this is far more useful.

### Rule 3: Measure food by weight, not cups
This is the single biggest change most owners can make. A measuring cup of dry food can vary by 30–50% depending on how it's filled and the kibble size. Invest in a **cheap digital kitchen scale** (£8–15 on Amazon) and weigh every meal.

### Rule 4: Count ALL calories — including treats
During a weight loss programme, treats must come out of the daily calorie allowance — not on top of it. Options:
- Use a portion of your dog's daily kibble as training treats
- Switch to low-calorie treats (carrot sticks, cucumber, blueberries)
- If using commercial treats, count the calories and reduce the meal accordingly

**Low-calorie treat options (per piece):**
- Baby carrot: ~4 calories
- Cucumber slice: ~1 calorie
- Blueberry: ~1 calorie
- Rice cake (plain, broken up): ~7 calories per piece
- Freeze-dried meat (small piece): ~5–8 calories

### Rule 5: Don't starve — adjust
A common mistake is halving food overnight. This leads to a miserable, obsessed dog and is potentially dangerous. Work with your vet to reduce intake by **10–20% initially**, then adjust based on monthly weigh-ins.

## Exercise for Weight Loss: What Actually Works

Exercise alone won't shift significant weight — diet is 80% of the equation. But exercise is critical for:
- Preserving muscle mass during weight loss
- Improving cardiovascular health
- Mental wellbeing (an exercised dog is less food-obsessed)

### The key: increase gradually

If your dog has been sedentary, don't suddenly double their walks. This risks joint injury in heavier dogs.

**Week 1–2:** Add 10 minutes to existing walks
**Week 3–4:** Add another 10 minutes, or add a second short walk
**Month 2+:** Build toward the breed's recommended daily exercise level

### Low-impact exercise for overweight dogs

Overweight dogs — especially those with joint issues — benefit from **low-impact exercise** that protects the joints while burning calories:

🏊 **Hydrotherapy / swimming** — ideal. Burns calories without joint stress. Many UK vet practices refer to hydrotherapy pools. Costs ~£30–50/session.

🚶 **Steady lead walks** — controlled pace, no off-lead sprinting until a healthier weight is achieved.

🐕 **Sniffing walks** — 20 minutes of sniffing is mentally equivalent to an hour of walking. Adds enrichment without physical strain.

**Activities to avoid until a healthy weight is reached:**
- Off-lead ball chasing (high-impact on joints)
- Jumping (on/off furniture, into cars)
- Stairs (for breeds with back problems or severe obesity)

## The Weight Loss Timeline: Month by Month

### Month 1
- Vet weigh-in and baseline blood tests
- New food/portion plan in place
- Measuring every meal by weight
- Treats counted and reduced
- Extra 10–15 min walk daily
- **Expected loss:** 1–3% of body weight

### Month 2
- Monthly vet weigh-in (or home weigh — weigh yourself holding the dog, subtract your weight)
- Adjust portions if progress too slow/fast
- Build exercise programme
- Owner: stay consistent, watch treat creep
- **Expected loss:** 1–2% per week (cumulative ~8–12% of starting weight)

### Month 3–4
- Midpoint assessment with vet
- Dog should be noticeably more active, moving better
- Waist beginning to show
- **Expected loss:** 15–20% of starting weight (if programme followed)

### Month 5–6
- Approaching target weight
- Transition to maintenance food/portion
- Establish maintenance weighing schedule (every 4–6 weeks)
- Celebrate! 🎉

## Breed-Specific Challenges

### Labrador Retrievers
Labs with the POMC gene variant don't feel full — they're genuinely hungrier than other dogs. Strategies:
- High-fibre food (keeps them fuller longer)
- Slow feeder bowl or puzzle feeder at every meal
- Two small meals rather than one large one
- Never free-feed

### French Bulldogs & Brachycephalic Breeds
Excess weight worsens breathing problems dramatically. Even 10–15% weight loss can dramatically improve comfort and reduce panting. Prioritise weight loss but avoid intense exercise — focus on cool, slow walks.

### Dachshunds
Weight gain puts enormous pressure on their long spines. IVDD (intervertebral disc disease) risk increases significantly with obesity. Low-impact exercise only — swimming ideal if available.

### Cavalier King Charles Spaniels
Many have lower calorie requirements than their size suggests. Measure food carefully — they can maintain weight on surprisingly small portions.

### Spayed/Neutered dogs of any breed
Reduce maintenance calories by 20–30% post-surgery. Don't wait for weight gain to happen.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### "But she looks hungry!"
Dogs are masters at looking hungry. Most overweight dogs are not calorie-deprived — they're conditioned to expect more food than they need. Hunger face ≠ genuine hunger.

### Giving in to begging
This is the number one saboteur of dog weight loss plans. Every family member needs to be on board. One person sneaking treats can undo a week's progress.

### Weighing too often
Daily weighing creates anxiety and misleading data (dogs retain water, bowels vary). Weigh every 2 weeks maximum.

### Comparing to other dogs
"My neighbour's Lab is twice the size and seems fine" — doesn't mean it IS fine. BCS 4–5 dogs have reduced life expectancy regardless of how happy they appear.

### Stopping too soon
When the dog starts looking noticeably better, it's tempting to relax. Push through to the vet's target weight. The last few kilos are important.

## When to See a Vet Urgently

Contact your vet if your dog:
- Has gained weight rapidly with no change in diet
- Has a swollen or pot-bellied appearance
- Is lethargic, drinking excessively, or urinating more
- Is struggling to breathe at rest
- Has severely reduced mobility due to weight

These can indicate medical conditions (Cushing's, hypothyroidism, fluid accumulation) that need investigation before any diet changes.

## FAQs

**Q: How long does it take for a dog to lose weight?**
Safe weight loss is 1–2% of body weight per week. A 30kg Labrador losing 5kg would take approximately 8–12 weeks if the programme is followed consistently.

**Q: Can I just cut my dog's food in half?**
No — sudden large reductions can cause nutritional deficiencies and make your dog miserable. Work with your vet to calculate the correct reduced portion, usually 10–20% below maintenance initially.

**Q: Are there weight loss jabs for dogs like Ozempic?**
There is no GLP-1 equivalent licensed for dogs in the UK as of 2026. Some specialist referral practices have used veterinary-licensed medications off-label, but diet and exercise remain the first-line approach.

**Q: My vet isn't taking my dog's weight seriously — what should I do?**
You can ask for a referral to a vet nurse weight clinic, or seek a second opinion. Some vet practices offer Purina Body Condition assessments — ask specifically for a weight management consultation.

**Q: Can I give my dog human food during weight loss?**
Some human foods are low-calorie and safe: carrot sticks, cucumber, apple slices (no seeds), blueberries. Avoid: grapes, onions, garlic, corn on the cob, anything processed.

**Q: Will my dog be miserable on a diet?**
Short-term hunger is normal during adjustment. Most dogs adapt within 1–2 weeks and become noticeably more energetic and engaged as weight comes off. A lighter dog is a happier dog.

## Conclusion

Dog obesity is one of the most common and preventable health problems in the UK — and one of the most heartbreaking, because it shortens the life of an animal who trusts us completely to make the right choices.

The process isn't complicated: weigh the food, count the treats, move a bit more, check in with the vet monthly. What it requires is consistency — from every person in the household, every day.

Start today. Book that vet weigh-in. Get the digital scale. Pick up the slow feeder.

Your dog has no idea they're on a diet. They just know they feel better, move better, and have more energy for the walks they love.

That's the whole point.
