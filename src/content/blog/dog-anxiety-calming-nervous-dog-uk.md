## What Is Dog Anxiety? {#what-is-dog-anxiety}

Dog anxiety is an emotional response to a perceived threat — real or imagined. Just like in humans, anxiety in dogs triggers a stress response: increased heart rate, elevated cortisol levels, and behavioural changes designed to help the dog cope.

The key difference between fear and anxiety:
- **Fear** is a response to a specific, identifiable threat (a loud bang, a stranger)
- **Anxiety** is the *anticipation* of a threat, often without one being present

A dog who panics during fireworks is experiencing **fear**. A dog who starts pacing and drooling every time you pick up your car keys is experiencing **anxiety** — they're anticipating being left alone.

Understanding this distinction matters because the treatment approaches differ.

## Types of Dog Anxiety {#types-of-dog-anxiety}

### 1. Separation Anxiety

The most common form. Affects an estimated 14-20% of dogs in the UK (PDSA PAW Report, 2024). Dogs become distressed when left alone or separated from their primary attachment figure.

**Signs:** Destructive behaviour, excessive barking/howling, toileting indoors (only when alone), attempts to escape, pacing, drooling.

**Common triggers:**
- Change in routine (owner returning to office)
- Moving house
- Loss of a family member or another pet
- Being rehomed
- Puppies who were separated too early from their litter

> **Post-lockdown note:** Vets across the UK saw a significant spike in separation anxiety cases from 2021 onwards, as dogs raised during lockdown had to adjust to owners returning to work. Many of these dogs are now 4-5 years old and may still struggle.

### 2. Noise Anxiety

Fireworks, thunderstorms, construction work, traffic — noise phobias affect roughly 25-49% of dogs (RSPCA). Bonfire Night and New Year's Eve are the peak periods in the UK.

**Signs:** Trembling, hiding, panting, trying to escape, refusing to go outside, loss of appetite.

### 3. Generalised Anxiety

Some dogs seem anxious about *everything* — new environments, strangers, other dogs, car journeys, the vet. This is generalised anxiety, and it's often rooted in genetics, early life experiences, or inadequate socialisation during the critical 3-16 week window.

**Signs:** Hypervigilance, startling easily, reluctance to explore, persistent lip-licking and yawning (when not tired or hungry).

### 4. Social Anxiety

Fear or anxiety around other dogs, unfamiliar people, or crowded environments. Often seen in:
- Dogs with limited early socialisation
- Rescue dogs with unknown histories
- Dogs who've had negative experiences (attacks, rough handling)

### 5. Age-Related Anxiety

Senior dogs (typically 8+ years) may develop anxiety linked to cognitive decline — Canine Cognitive Dysfunction (CCD). Similar to dementia in humans, CCD can cause confusion, disorientation, and increased anxiety, particularly at night.

**Signs:** Restlessness at night, getting stuck in corners, forgetting familiar routes, staring into space, increased vocalisation.

## Signs Your Dog Is Anxious {#signs-your-dog-is-anxious}

Dogs can't tell us they're anxious, but their body language speaks volumes. Here's what to watch for:

### Obvious Signs
- Trembling or shaking
- Excessive barking, whining, or howling
- Destructive behaviour (chewing furniture, scratching doors)
- Toileting indoors (when previously house-trained)
- Pacing or inability to settle
- Attempting to escape or hide
- Drooling (excessive, beyond normal)

### Subtle Signs (Often Missed)
- **Lip-licking** (when there's no food around)
- **Yawning** (when they're not tired)
- **Whale eye** (showing the whites of their eyes)
- **Ears pinned back**
- **Tucked tail**
- **Turning their head away** (avoidance)
- **Paw lifting**
- **Refusing treats** (a normally food-motivated dog refusing treats is a red flag)
- **Excessive self-grooming** (licking paws raw)
- **Displacement behaviours** (suddenly sniffing the ground intensely in a stressful situation)

> **Important:** Many owners mistake anxiety signs for "naughty" behaviour. A dog who destroys the sofa when left alone isn't being spiteful — they're genuinely distressed. Punishment will make anxiety *worse*, not better.

## What Causes Dog Anxiety? {#what-causes-anxiety}

### Genetics
Some breeds are genetically predisposed to anxiety (see breed section below). A 2020 Finnish study of over 6,000 dogs found that fearfulness was the most common anxiety trait, and had strong breed-specific patterns.

### Early Life Experiences
The critical socialisation window (3-16 weeks) is everything. Puppies who miss out on positive exposure to diverse people, animals, sounds, and environments during this period are significantly more likely to develop anxiety.

**Puppy farms** are a major contributor to anxiety in UK dogs. Dogs from puppy farms typically experience:
- Early maternal separation
- Limited environmental exposure
- Stressful transport conditions
- Lack of human socialisation

### Trauma
A single traumatic event — a dog attack, a car accident, harsh punishment — can create lasting anxiety. Dogs can also develop anxiety from repeated low-level stress (a neighbour's dog who barks at them daily through the fence).

### Medical Causes
Always rule out medical issues first. Pain, thyroid problems, neurological conditions, and certain medications can all cause or worsen anxiety-like symptoms.

### Changes in Routine
Dogs are creatures of habit. Major life changes can trigger anxiety:
- Moving house
- New baby (see section below)
- Change in work schedule
- Loss of a companion (human or animal)
- Rehoming

## How to Calm an Anxious Dog: 12 Proven Techniques {#how-to-calm-an-anxious-dog}

### 1. Create a Safe Space

Every anxious dog needs a retreat — somewhere they can go when the world feels too much.

**How to set it up:**
- Choose a quiet area away from windows and external noise
- Use a covered crate (with the door always open) or a cosy corner with blankets
- Add an item of your worn clothing for scent comfort
- Never force them into the space or disturb them when they're there
- Consider a [Snuggle Puppy](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B000S753WK?tag=mymuttlife-21) with a heat pack and simulated heartbeat for puppies and newly adopted dogs

### 2. Desensitisation and Counter-Conditioning

The gold standard for treating anxiety. Works by gradually exposing your dog to the anxiety trigger at a very low intensity while pairing it with something positive.

**Example for noise anxiety:**
1. Play the triggering sound (e.g., fireworks recording) at the lowest possible volume
2. Immediately offer high-value treats (cheese, chicken, liver)
3. Gradually increase the volume over days/weeks
4. If the dog shows any stress signs, reduce the intensity

**Key rules:**
- Go at your dog's pace — this can take weeks or months
- Never flood (expose them to the full trigger at once)
- Sessions should be short (5-10 minutes)
- End each session on a positive note

> **Resource:** The Dogs Trust Sound Therapy programme is free and specifically designed for noise-phobic dogs: [www.dogstrust.org.uk/help-advice/dog-behaviour-health/sound-therapy-for-pets](https://www.dogstrust.org.uk/help-advice/dog-behaviour-health/sound-therapy-for-pets)

### 3. The Dogs Trust "Chin Rest" Technique

Developed by the Dogs Trust, the chin rest teaches your dog to voluntarily place their chin on your hand or a cushion. This gives them a **choice-based coping mechanism** — something they can actively do when feeling anxious, which reduces feelings of helplessness.

**Steps:**
1. Hold your flat hand out, palm up
2. When your dog's nose touches your hand, mark ("Yes!") and treat
3. Gradually wait for them to rest their chin on your hand before marking
4. Build duration slowly
5. Eventually, transfer this to a cushion or their bed

This technique is especially useful for vet visits and grooming — situations where the dog typically has no control.

### 4. Calm, Predictable Routine

Anxious dogs thrive on predictability. Keep feeding times, walk times, and bedtime as consistent as possible.

**Tips:**
- Use the same pre-walk routine (same lead, same door, same command)
- Establish a "wind-down" routine before bedtime
- If your schedule must change, introduce changes gradually
- Use a [LickiMat](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B07FTDPBFQ?tag=mymuttlife-21) at set times — the repetitive licking action is inherently calming (releases endorphins)

### 5. Exercise (But the Right Kind)

Physical exercise burns off stress hormones, but for anxious dogs, the *type* of exercise matters more than the amount.

**Good for anxious dogs:**
- Sniffari walks (slow, let them sniff everything — 20 minutes of sniffing is equivalent to an hour of physical exercise for mental tiredness)
- Swimming (naturally calming)
- Fetch in a quiet, familiar garden
- Trick training (mental engagement with positive reinforcement)

**Avoid for anxious dogs:**
- Dog parks (overstimulating)
- High-adrenaline activities (agility, flyball) if they're already in a heightened state
- Forced socialisation with unknown dogs
- Long walks near busy roads

### 6. Calming Music and Sound

Research from the Scottish SPCA and the University of Glasgow (2017) found that soft rock and reggae were the most calming genres for dogs. Classical music also showed benefits.

**Options:**

### 10. Training "Settle" and "Place"

Teaching your dog to voluntarily settle on a mat or bed is one of the most powerful tools for managing day-to-day anxiety.

**"Place" training steps:**
1. Lead your dog to a specific mat or bed
2. When they step on it, mark ("Yes!") and treat
3. Build to lying down on the mat
4. Build duration (start with 2 seconds, work up to minutes)
5. Proof with mild distractions
6. The mat becomes a portable "safe zone" you can take anywhere

**Advanced:** Take the mat to the vet, the café, friends' houses. The mat cue becomes associated with "relax here" regardless of location.

### 11. Nose Work and Enrichment

Sniffing is a natural calming behaviour. When a dog is actively using their nose, their heart rate drops and stress hormones decrease.

**Simple enrichment ideas:**
- Scatter feeding on the lawn (throw a handful of kibble into grass)
- [Snuffle mat](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B08L4LCYC9?tag=mymuttlife-21) (£12-18) — hides food in fabric strips
- Cardboard box with scrunched paper and hidden treats
- Frozen [KONG Classic](https://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B0002AR0I8?tag=mymuttlife-21) stuffed with peanut butter (xylitol-free) and banana
- "Find it" game (hide treats around the house)

### 12. Your Own Energy

Dogs are emotional sponges. Research from Linköping University (Sweden, 2019) found that dogs' long-term cortisol levels mirror their owners'. If you're stressed, your dog feels it.

**Practical tips:**
- Stay calm when your dog is anxious (easier said than done, but crucial)
- Don't over-comfort — constant fussing can reinforce the idea that something is wrong
- Use a calm, low tone of voice
- Breathe slowly (dogs can detect breathing patterns)
- Model the behaviour you want — if you want them to settle, you settle first

## When to See a Vet or Behaviourist {#when-to-see-a-vet}

Self-help techniques work for mild to moderate anxiety. But some dogs need professional support. **See your vet if:**

- Anxiety is getting worse despite consistent training
- Your dog is injuring themselves (breaking teeth on crate bars, ripping nails trying to escape)
- They've stopped eating for more than 24 hours
- Anxiety is affecting their quality of life significantly
- You suspect age-related cognitive decline
- The behaviour started suddenly (could indicate pain or medical issue)

### What Your Vet Might Suggest

**Medication options (prescription only):**
- **Fluoxetine (Reconcile/Prozac)** — SSRI, takes 4-6 weeks to reach full effect, used for long-term anxiety management
- **Clomipramine (Clomicalm)** — Tricyclic antidepressant, particularly effective for separation anxiety
- **Trazodone** — Short-acting, useful for situational anxiety (vet visits, fireworks)
- **Sileo (dexmedetomidine)** — Oromucosal gel specifically licensed for noise aversion in dogs

> **Cost guide (UK):** Initial behavioural consultation: £50-150. Prescription medication: £15-40/month depending on drug and dog size. Some pet insurance policies now cover behavioural treatment — check your policy.

### Finding a Qualified Behaviourist

Not all "dog trainers" are qualified to treat anxiety. Look for:
- **ABTC (Animal Behaviour and Training Council)** registered practitioners — the UK's regulatory body
- **CCAB (Certified Clinical Animal Behaviourist)** — the gold standard
- **Members of APBC (Association of Pet Behaviour Counsellors)**
- **Avoid** anyone who uses punishment-based methods — these make anxiety significantly worse

**Free resources:**
- [Dogs Trust Dog School](https://www.dogstrust.org.uk/dog-advice/training/dog-school) — classes across the UK
- [Blue Cross behaviour advice](https://www.bluecross.org.uk/pet-advice/dog-behaviour)
- Your vet can refer to an ABTC-registered behaviourist

## Anxiety at Different Life Stages {#anxiety-life-stages}

### Puppies (8 weeks – 6 months)

- **Normal anxiety:** Mild distress when separated, nervousness around new things
- **Key action:** Socialise widely during the 3-16 week window. Positive exposure to: different people, other vaccinated dogs, vehicles, household appliances, different surfaces, vet visits
- **Warning signs:** Extreme fear responses, inability to recover from startling events

### Adolescents (6 – 18 months)

- **Fear periods:** Dogs go through a second fear period around 6-14 months. Sudden fearfulness of previously accepted things is normal
- **Key action:** Don't force exposure. If they're scared, create distance and reward calm behaviour
- **Warning signs:** Increasing reactivity, avoidance of previously enjoyed activities

### Adults (2 – 7 years)

- **Most stable period**, but anxiety can emerge after traumatic events or life changes
- **Key action:** Maintain socialisation. Dogs who stop meeting new people/dogs can become anxious around them
- **Warning signs:** New onset of anxiety symptoms without obvious cause (see a vet)

### Seniors (8+ years)

- **Cognitive decline** can cause night-time restlessness, confusion, and increased anxiety
- **Key action:** Keep routines very consistent. Add nightlights. Consider supplements (Aktivait, YuMOVE Calming Care)
- **Warning signs:** Pacing at night, loss of house training, getting stuck in corners, staring at walls

## Preparing an Anxious Dog for a New Baby {#preparing-for-baby}

If your dog is already anxious, the arrival of a baby needs extra planning. Start 2-3 months before the due date:

### Before Baby Arrives

1. **Gradually adjust routines** — If walk times will change, start shifting them now
2. **Sound desensitisation** — Play recordings of baby crying at low volume, paired with treats. The Dogs Trust has free sound therapy resources
3. **Create baby-free zones** — Install baby gates now so the dog adjusts before the baby arrives
4. **Scent introduction** — After birth, bring home a blanket with the baby's scent before the baby comes home
5. **Practice with a doll** — Carry a doll, sit with it, use the pushchair. Reward calm behaviour around it
6. **Brush up "settle" and "leave it"** commands

### When Baby Arrives

- Let the dog investigate at their own pace (supervised)
- Reward calm behaviour near the baby with high-value treats
- Never punish interest or sniffing — that's how dogs learn
- Maintain some dedicated dog-only time each day
- Never leave dog and baby unsupervised, regardless of breed or temperament

### Red Flags (Get Professional Help Immediately)

- Stiffening, lip-curling, or growling near the baby
- Resource guarding that wasn't present before
- Complete withdrawal or refusal to be in the same room
- Any snapping or lunging

> **Resource:** [Dogs Trust — Dogs and Babies](https://www.dogstrust.org.uk/help-advice/dog-behaviour-health/dogs-and-babies) — excellent free guide with step-by-step preparation advice.

## FAQs {#faqs}

### Can anxiety in dogs be cured?
It depends on the type and severity. Mild anxiety can often be managed effectively with training and environmental changes. More severe anxiety may require lifelong management with a combination of behavioural modification and medication. The goal is usually *management* rather than *cure* — reducing anxiety to a level where the dog can live a happy, comfortable life.

### Is my dog anxious or just badly behaved?
If the behaviour only occurs in specific contexts (when left alone, during storms, around strangers), it's almost certainly anxiety, not disobedience. A truly "naughty" dog would misbehave regardless of context. Anxiety behaviours are also accompanied by physiological signs (panting, drooling, trembling) that voluntary misbehaviour is not.

### How long does it take to help an anxious dog?
Mild cases may see improvement in 2-4 weeks with consistent training. Moderate cases typically take 2-3 months. Severe anxiety can take 6+ months and may require medication alongside behavioural work. There are no overnight fixes — patience and consistency are everything.

### Should I comfort my anxious dog or ignore them?
**Comfort them** — but calmly. The old advice to "ignore them so you don't reinforce the fear" has been debunked. You cannot reinforce an emotion. However, frantic over-comforting ("Oh no, poor baby, what's wrong?!") can signal to your dog that there IS something to worry about. Instead: be a calm, reassuring presence. Gentle strokes, quiet voice, relaxed body language.

### Can puppies grow out of anxiety?
Some puppies do grow out of mild anxiety with proper socialisation. However, anxiety that's left unaddressed during the critical developmental windows often gets *worse*, not better. Early intervention is always better than waiting to see.

### Is it cruel to crate an anxious dog?
If the dog hasn't been properly crate-trained, forcing them into a crate can make anxiety significantly worse. However, a dog who's been gradually introduced to a crate and views it as their safe den can find it enormously comforting during anxious episodes. The key: the crate door should always be open (during anxiety episodes), and the dog should *choose* to go in.

## Summary: Your Anxiety Action Plan

1. **Identify the type** — What specific situations trigger your dog's anxiety?
2. **Rule out medical causes** — Book a vet check if the anxiety is new or worsening
3. **Create a safe space** — Every anxious dog needs a retreat
4. **Start desensitisation** — Gradually expose to triggers at low intensity + positive associations
5. **Try calming products** — Adaptil, ThunderShirt, supplements (see our recommendations above)
6. **Increase enrichment** — Sniff walks, puzzle feeders, nose work
7. **Be consistent** — Routines, calm energy, patience
8. **Get help if needed** — ABTC behaviourist or veterinary referral for severe cases

**Your dog didn't choose to be anxious.** With understanding, consistency, and the right approach, most anxious dogs can live much happier, calmer lives.
