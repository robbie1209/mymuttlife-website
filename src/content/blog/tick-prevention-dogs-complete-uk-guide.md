## Introduction

Spring is here, and so are the ticks.

From March to October, tick populations across the UK explode — and your dog is their preferred host. Whether you're walking Poppy across the South Downs, taking a morning run through woodland, or simply letting your dog sniff the hedgerow at the bottom of the garden, the risk is real, present, and — crucially — preventable.

But here's what catches most owners out: ticks aren't just unpleasant. They're vectors for some genuinely serious diseases, including Lyme disease (Borrelia burgdorferi), babesiosis, and anaplasmosis. Lyme disease in dogs can cause chronic joint pain, kidney disease, and neurological symptoms — and it's on the rise in the UK.

The good news? With the right prevention plan, tick-borne disease is almost entirely avoidable.

This guide covers everything you need to know for 2026: the best prescription and over-the-counter tick treatments, how to do a proper tick check, how to safely remove a tick if you find one, which habitats to watch out for, and the warning signs of tick-borne illness every dog owner should recognise.

## 🦟 UK Tick Season: When Is the Risk Highest?

Many owners assume ticks are only active in summer. They're not.

| Month | Risk Level | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| January | 🟢 Low | Cold slows tick activity but doesn't eliminate it |
| February | 🟢 Low | Some activity in mild spells |
| March | 🟡 Moderate | Season begins — first eggs hatching |
| **April** | 🔴 **High** | Peak nymph activity — hardest to spot |
| **May** | 🔴 **High** | Full season, all life stages active |
| **June–August** | 🔴 **High** | Adult and nymph peak |
| **September–October** | 🔴 **High** | Adult activity continues, especially in warm years |
| November | 🟡 Moderate | Slowing but still active |
| December | 🟢 Low | Risk drops significantly |

**The danger months:** April, May, and September–October tend to catch owners off guard — April because people don't think "tick season" has started, and autumn because they think it's over.

**Climate note:** Milder UK winters mean tick seasons are starting earlier and ending later than they did a decade ago. The UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) confirmed in 2024 that Lyme disease cases have increased year-on-year since 2018.

## 🗺️ Where Are Ticks Found in the UK?

Ticks live in vegetation — they don't jump or fly. They "quest": climbing to the tips of grass, bracken, and heather, and waiting with their front legs outstretched to attach to a passing host.

**High-risk habitats:**
- **Woodland and forest edges** (especially mixed and deciduous woodland)
- **Long grass, bracken, and heather** — moorland and upland areas
- **Areas with deer, sheep, or rabbits** (major tick hosts)
- **Urban parks** with wooded sections or rough grass
- **Coastal scrubland and dunes**

**High-risk UK areas (Public Health England / UKHSA data):**
- Scottish Highlands and Islands
- New Forest, Hampshire
- Exmoor and Dartmoor
- South Downs and Surrey Hills
- North York Moors and Yorkshire Dales
- Welsh uplands
- Lake District
- Forest of Dean

**Important:** Ticks are not restricted to rural areas. They've been found in London's Richmond Park, city-fringe woodland, and suburban gardens near green space. If your garden backs onto meadow, scrub, or trees — check your dog.

## 🛡️ Tick Prevention: Which Products Work Best?

Prevention is always better than removal. These are your options:

### Prescription Treatments (Most Effective)

**1. Bravecto (fluralaner) — Oral chew or spot-on**
- **Duration:** 12 weeks (oral); 4 months (spot-on)
- **Kills ticks within:** 12 hours (oral), 48 hours (spot-on)
- **Covers:** Ticks + fleas
- **Prescription required:** Yes (UK)
- **Best for:** Dogs who don't tolerate topical treatments, active working dogs
- **Average cost:** £18–£30 per dose (depending on dog weight)

> 🐾 **Vet note:** Bravecto has APVMA and VMD approval. Some owners on online forums worry about isoxazoline side effects (tremors, seizures) — these are rare but real, and worth discussing with your vet, particularly for dogs with a history of neurological issues.

**2. NexGard (afoxolaner) — Monthly oral chew**
- **Duration:** 1 month
- **Kills ticks within:** 48 hours
- **Covers:** Ticks + fleas
- **Prescription required:** Yes (UK)
- **Best for:** Owners who prefer monthly dosing, dogs that like chews
- **Average cost:** £10–£18 per monthly dose

**3. Simparica / Simparica Trio (sarolaner) — Monthly oral chew**
- **Duration:** 1 month
- **Kills ticks within:** 8 hours (fastest of the oral isoxazolines)
- **Covers:** Ticks, fleas, and (Trio version) roundworm/hookworm/heartworm
- **Prescription required:** Yes (UK)
- **Average cost:** £12–£20 per dose

---

### Topical Spot-On Treatments

**4. Advantix (imidacloprid + permethrin) — Monthly spot-on**
- **Repels AND kills** ticks — unique advantage vs products that kill-on-contact only
- **Duration:** 1 month
- **Covers:** Ticks, fleas, mosquitoes, sandflies
- **Prescription required:** No (available OTC)
- **⚠️ Critical warning:** Permethrin is **highly toxic to cats**. Do not use on dogs in households with cats. Keep treated dogs away from cats for 24–72 hours.
- **Best for:** Dogs in high-risk areas; households without cats
- **Cost:** £8–£15 per dose

**5. Frontline Plus (fipronil + S-methoprene) — Monthly spot-on**
- **Kills on contact** (doesn't repel)
- **Duration:** 1 month
- **Prescription required:** No
- **Honest note:** Some tick resistance to fipronil has been documented in certain UK tick populations (Ixodes ricinus). Frontline remains effective for fleas but may be less reliable for heavy-tick-burden areas.
- **Cost:** £6–£12 per dose

**6. Seresto Collar (imidacloprid + flumethrin)**
- **Duration:** 8 months — the longest of any single product
- **Repels AND kills** ticks
- **Prescription required:** No
- **Best for:** Owners who don't want to remember monthly dosing; active dogs
- **⚠️ Watch for:** Counterfeit Seresto collars are common online. Only buy from registered UK veterinary pharmacies or Pets at Home. Check the collar packaging has the Elanco batch number and veterinary medicine product authorisation number.
- **Cost:** £35–£50 per collar (8 months = good value)

## 🔍 How to Check Your Dog for Ticks

Even with preventative treatment, you should check your dog after every walk in tick habitat — especially April through October.

**When to check:** Within 1–2 hours of returning from a walk (ticks take time to settle and begin feeding — removing them before they attach or before 24 hours prevents disease transmission).

**How to check:**

1. **Run your fingers slowly through the coat** against the direction of growth — you're feeling for small lumps, not just looking
2. **Focus on hotspots first:**
   - Around the ears and inside the ear canal
   - Between the toes and around the paw pads
   - Groin and armpits (warm, protected areas ticks love)
   - Under the collar and around the neck
   - Around the tail base and hindquarters
   - Around the eyelids
3. **Use good lighting** — use your phone torch if needed
4. **Part the coat** in sections for long-coated breeds
5. **Don't forget yourself** — check your own ankles and legs too

**Tick identification:**
- Unengorged tick: tiny (1–3mm), brown/tan, eight-legged (arachnid, not insect)
- Engorged tick: can swell to 10–15mm, bluish-grey, looks like a small grape or seed
- Larval ticks: <1mm — very easy to miss

## ✂️ How to Remove a Tick Safely

**The most important rule:** Never squeeze the tick's body, never cover it in Vaseline or nail polish, and never burn it off. These methods cause the tick to regurgitate stomach contents into your dog — dramatically increasing disease transmission risk.

**The right way:**

### Step 1: Get a proper tick removal tool
Use a tick hook (O'Tom Tick Twister) or fine-pointed tweezers designed for tick removal. Standard tweezers are too wide and risk squeezing the body.

**Recommended tools:**
- **O'Tom Tick Twister** — £5–£8 for a pack of 2 (different sizes for different tick sizes). Excellent, vet-recommended.
- **Tick Key** — slides under the tick; good for large engorged ticks
- Avoid: blunt household tweezers

### Step 2: Position correctly
Get as close to the skin as possible — hook underneath the tick's body (mouthparts), not around the engorged sac.

### Step 3: Twist and lift
With the Tick Twister, rotate gently in one direction (the direction doesn't matter — it's a myth that you must turn counter-clockwise). Apply gentle, steady upward pressure simultaneously. The tick will release cleanly in 2–3 rotations.

With tweezers: grasp as close to the skin as possible, pull upward with slow, steady pressure — no twisting.

### Step 4: Dispose safely
Drop the tick into a sealed bag or jar with a small amount of rubbing alcohol. Do not crush it with your fingers.

### Step 5: Clean and monitor
Clean the bite site with antiseptic wipe or diluted iodine. Note the date and location on your dog's body.

**Monitor the bite site for 2–3 weeks** — if you see a bulls-eye rash (erythema migrans), swelling, or your dog becomes lethargic, contact your vet.

## ⚠️ Signs of Tick-Borne Disease in Dogs

Most tick bites don't cause illness — but some do. The main diseases to be aware of in the UK:

### Lyme Disease (Borrelia burgdorferi)
The most common tick-borne disease in the UK, spread by Ixodes ricinus (the sheep tick).

**Symptoms (often appear 2–5 months after bite):**
- Shifting lameness (limps on different legs at different times)
- Lethargy and reduced exercise tolerance
- Swollen lymph nodes
- Loss of appetite
- Fever
- In severe cases: kidney disease (Lyme nephritis) — the most dangerous complication

**Testing:** Your vet can run a blood test (C6 antibody test or Western Blot). The SNAP 4Dx Plus test detects Lyme alongside other diseases.

**Treatment:** Doxycycline (30-day course) is the standard UK treatment. Most dogs respond well if caught early. Lyme nephritis carries a poorer prognosis.

**Vaccine:** The Lyme disease vaccine for dogs (Nobivac Lyme) is available in the UK as a non-core vaccine — worth discussing with your vet if you live in or regularly visit high-risk areas.

---

### Babesiosis (Babesia canis / Babesia gibsoni)
Less common in the UK but present, particularly in the south of England and imported via travel from Europe.

**Symptoms:** Pale gums, fatigue, dark urine, jaundice, high fever, rapid breathing

**This is an emergency.** Babesiosis destroys red blood cells — it can be fatal within days without treatment. If your dog shows these signs after a tick exposure, call your vet immediately.

---

### Anaplasmosis (Anaplasma phagocytophilum)
Emerging tick-borne disease, particularly in Scotland and northern England.

**Symptoms:** Lethargy, fever, limping, vomiting, diarrhoea, appearing generally unwell 1–2 weeks after tick exposure

---

### Ehrlichiosis
Rare in native UK ticks but possible in dogs that have travelled to southern Europe or the Middle East.

---

**The key rule:** If your dog has had a tick bite and becomes unwell in the following 4–6 weeks — especially with lameness, fever, or fatigue — tell your vet. Mention the tick exposure. It changes the diagnostic picture significantly.

## 🐾 Breed-Specific Considerations

### Small Breeds (Chihuahuas, Yorkshire Terriers, Miniature Dachshunds)
Ticks are proportionally larger relative to body size. Check between all toes carefully — ticks love the webbing. Small breeds may also be more sensitive to isoxazoline products; discuss dosing carefully with your vet.

### Brachycephalic Breeds (French Bulldogs, Pugs, Bulldogs)
Thick skin folds around the face, neck, and tail are prime tick-hiding spots. Check all folds carefully. Oral treatments are generally preferred — topical application can be difficult around folds.

### Long-Coated Breeds (Golden Retrievers, Border Collies, Setters, Spaniels)
Ticks are much harder to find. A fine-tooth comb run through the coat is a useful supplement to finger-checking. Consider Seresto collar as a belt-and-braces alongside topical/oral treatment.

### Working Dogs (Spaniels, Labradors, Border Collies)
High-risk — daily exposure to rough vegetation. Monthly prescription treatments are strongly recommended. Build tick checks into your post-walk routine.

### Puppies (Under 8 Weeks)
Most tick treatments are not licensed for very young puppies. Speak to your vet — some topical treatments are safe from 7–8 weeks, but prescription oral isoxazolines have a minimum age of 8 weeks and minimum weight requirements.

### Senior Dogs
Some older dogs with kidney or liver disease may not be suitable for certain treatments. Your vet may recommend alternatives. Don't skip tick prevention just because your dog is elderly — they're often more vulnerable to tick-borne illness.

## 🌿 Natural Tick Prevention: Does Any of It Work?

Pet owners frequently ask about natural alternatives — essential oils, apple cider vinegar, garlic. Here's the honest breakdown:

| Remedy | Evidence | Safety | Verdict |
|--------|----------|--------|---------|
| Garlic | No scientific evidence; disrupts tick scent — low | **Toxic to dogs in quantity** | ❌ Avoid |
| Tea Tree Oil | Some repellent effect in small studies | **Toxic to dogs — do not apply** | ❌ Avoid |
| Apple Cider Vinegar | Anecdotal only, no controlled studies | Safe in small amounts | ❌ Not effective |
| Coconut Oil | Lauric acid has some repellent activity in lab conditions | Safe topically | ⚠️ Minimal real-world protection |
| Rose Geranium Oil | Some promising animal studies | Safe topically in dilution | ⚠️ Weak evidence; not a replacement |
| Neem Oil | Insecticidal properties in agriculture | Can irritate skin; not tested in dogs | ⚠️ Limited data |

**The honest answer:** No natural remedy has clinical evidence supporting reliable tick repellency comparable to licensed veterinary products. If you're in a low-risk area and doing occasional woodland walks, extra vigilance + daily checks may be enough. In high-risk areas, licensed treatment is strongly recommended.

## ❓ Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can ticks kill a dog?**
A: Not directly from the bite itself. However, the diseases ticks carry — particularly babesiosis — can be life-threatening without rapid treatment. Lyme nephritis (kidney disease caused by Lyme infection) is also serious. Prompt treatment and prevention dramatically reduce the risk.

**Q: My dog had a tick but seems fine. Do I still need to see a vet?**
A: Not immediately. Monitor your dog closely for 4–6 weeks. If they develop lethargy, lameness, or any symptoms from the list above, mention the tick bite when you call. If the tick was attached for more than 24 hours, it's worth calling your vet for advice even if your dog seems well.

**Q: How long does a tick need to be attached to transmit Lyme disease?**
A: Current scientific consensus is that Borrelia transmission typically requires 24–48 hours of attachment — which is why checking and removing ticks promptly is so effective. However, this varies by tick species and disease, and there's no guaranteed safe window. Remove all ticks as quickly as possible.

**Q: Can ticks transmit diseases between dogs?**
A: Not directly — ticks need to feed on an infected host first, then transfer to another host. But if multiple dogs use the same tick-infested habitat, they're all at risk from the environment.

**Q: My dog is scratching a lot after a walk — could it be ticks?**
A: Possibly — but more likely fleas, grass seeds, or contact dermatitis from plants. Check carefully for ticks (they don't always cause scratching at first). If you find no ticks but scratching persists, consult your vet.

**Q: Can I use cat tick treatments on my dog?**
A: No — some cat treatments contain permethrin at doses that are toxic to dogs. Always use products specifically licensed for dogs. And conversely, never use Advantix (a dog product) near cats.

**Q: Is there a Lyme disease vaccine available for UK dogs?**
A: Yes — Nobivac Lyme (MSD Animal Health) is licensed in the UK as a non-core vaccine. It's recommended for dogs in high-risk areas (Scottish Highlands, New Forest, Exmoor, etc.) or dogs that spend a lot of time in tick habitat. Ask your vet whether it's appropriate for your dog.
