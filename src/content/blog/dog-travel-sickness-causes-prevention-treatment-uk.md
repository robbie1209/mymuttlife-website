## Introduction

You're halfway up the M6, bags in the boot, Spotify on shuffle — and then it starts. The yawning. The drooling. The look. You already know what's coming next.

Dog travel sickness affects an estimated 1 in 5 dogs in the UK, yet most owners either suffer through it or give up on car travel altogether. Neither is necessary. Whether your dog has always been wobbly in the car or has recently developed a problem, there are proven solutions — from simple management techniques to prescription medication — that genuinely work.

This guide covers everything: why dogs get travel sick, which breeds are most at risk, what the warning signs actually look like before the vomit arrives, and a step-by-step treatment plan starting with the things you can try tonight.

## Why Dogs Get Travel Sick: The Science

Dog travel sickness has two distinct causes, and understanding which one affects your dog changes how you treat it.

### 1. The Inner Ear (True Motion Sickness)

The most common cause — especially in puppies — is a mismatch between what the vestibular system (inner ear) detects and what the eyes see. In a moving car, the inner ear picks up motion but a dog staring at the back seat sees no corresponding visual movement. The brain interprets this conflict as poisoning and triggers nausea.

**Good news:** The vestibular system matures as puppies grow. Many dogs that were travel sick as puppies naturally improve between 12–18 months of age. This is why some owners notice the problem "fixed itself."

**PDSA note:** Dogs whose ear canals haven't fully developed — including very young puppies — are particularly susceptible to vestibular disruption.

### 2. Anxiety and Conditioned Responses

For some dogs, travel sickness is primarily psychological. A puppy that was sick on their first vet journey can associate the car with nausea, creating a conditioned fear response that triggers physical symptoms even on short journeys.

Signs that anxiety is the driver (not just inner-ear motion):
- Symptoms start before the car moves (just getting in causes panting/drooling)
- Dog was previously fine in the car, then became sick after a stressful journey
- Dog calms down on longer motorway journeys (smooth, straight roads)

Many dogs have **both** causes active simultaneously, which is why multi-modal treatment works best.

## Warning Signs of Dog Travel Sickness

Vomiting is the obvious one, but these signs appear earlier — knowing them lets you intervene before things escalate:

**Early warning signs (act now):**
- Excessive yawning or lip licking
- Drooling more than usual
- Restlessness — standing up, sitting down, unable to settle
- Whining or vocalising
- Fixed, glazed stare
- Trembling or shaking

**Escalating signs:**
- Refusing to get in the car at all
- Retching without vomiting
- Vomiting
- Loss of balance after journey
- Lethargy post-travel

If your dog shows multiple early warning signs, that's your cue to stop (safely) and let them settle before continuing.

## Which Dogs Are Most at Risk?

### Age
Puppies are significantly more susceptible due to underdeveloped vestibular systems. Most improve naturally by 12–18 months. Senior dogs can also develop travel sickness if vestibular disease sets in with age.

### Breed Susceptibility

| Breed Category | Risk Level | Notes |
|----------------|-----------|-------|
| Brachycephalic (French Bulldog, Pug, Boxer) | High | Respiratory compromise + inner ear anatomy |
| Spaniels (Cocker, Springer) | Moderate-High | Anxiety-prone; ear anatomy |
| Herding breeds (Collie, Shepherd) | Moderate | Highly motion-sensitive |
| Sighthounds (Greyhound, Whippet) | Low | Tend to travel well once settled |
| Labradors & Golden Retrievers | Low-Moderate | Generally good travellers but can develop conditioned anxiety |
| Toy breeds (Chihuahua, Maltese) | Moderate | Small body = more pronounced motion effect |

### Individual History
- Dogs with a history of ear infections have increased risk (vestibular disruption)
- Dogs that were never socialised to car travel as puppies
- Rescue dogs with unknown car history

## Prevention: Start Here Before Medication

Most dogs can be significantly improved with management changes alone. Try these before reaching for medication.

### 1. Face Forward

Dogs that face the direction of travel experience significantly less vestibular conflict. A dog in a crate, secured on the back seat facing backward, experiences more motion sickness than one secured forward-facing.

**Fix:** Use a front-facing secured harness or forward-facing travel crate. Keep dogs away from side windows where the motion blur is most disorienting.

### 2. Lower the Window (Just Slightly)

Cracking a window 5–10cm equalises air pressure inside the car, which reduces nausea for many dogs. Don't open it fully — this causes its own stress and safety risks — just enough to allow air circulation.

### 3. Keep the Car Cool

Heat amplifies nausea. Use air conditioning on journeys over 30 minutes, and never leave a dog in a warm car even briefly. Core body temperature rises quickly and makes motion sickness significantly worse.

A cooling mat in the crate or footwell can help on hot days. (Ruffwear Jet Stream Cooling Vest is also useful for high-risk journeys in summer.)

### 4. Fast Before Travel

Feed your last meal at least 2–3 hours before a journey. An empty stomach reduces the likelihood and severity of vomiting. For puppies, 4 hours is better.

**Do not** fast for longer than 4 hours — hypoglycaemia is a risk in young or small dogs.

### 5. Strategic Breaks

Stop every 1–2 hours on longer journeys. Even 10 minutes off the car, on lead, sniffing grass resets the vestibular system and allows the dog to rehydrate. Don't let a dog run immediately after getting out — gentle movement first.

### 6. Gradual Car Desensitisation (The Long Game)

This is the most effective long-term fix for anxiety-related travel sickness. It takes 2–4 weeks but produces lasting results.

**The programme:**
- **Week 1, Days 1–3:** Let dog sit in stationary car with engine off. Reward calmness. 5 minutes, end positively.
- **Week 1, Days 4–7:** Engine on, stationary. Reward calmness. Build to 10 minutes.
- **Week 2:** Short drives — 2 minutes round the block. End at somewhere positive (park, friend's house — not the vet).
- **Week 3:** Extend to 10-minute journeys. Mix destinations — not always the vet or groomers.
- **Week 4:** Normal journeys, monitoring symptoms.

**Key rule:** Never push through obvious distress. If your dog is showing early warning signs, the session is over. End positively with a treat and try again tomorrow at the previous level.

Dogs Trust endorses this gradual exposure approach as the foundation of resolving travel anxiety.

## Natural Remedies: What the Evidence Says

### Ginger

Used in humans for centuries, ginger has genuine anti-nausea properties. For dogs, small amounts of fresh or dried ginger can be effective — particularly for mild motion sickness with a vestibular cause.

**Dose:** Approximately 1/4 teaspoon of dried ginger powder for small dogs (under 10kg), 1/2 teaspoon for medium dogs, 3/4 teaspoon for large dogs. Mix into food 30 minutes before travel.

**Note:** Don't use ginger biscuits (high sugar) or ginger ale (artificial sweeteners). Plain dried ginger or specific dog ginger treats only.

**Evidence:** Moderate — human clinical evidence is strong; canine-specific studies are limited but supported by veterinary anecdote (Blue Cross).

### Adaptil Travel Spray

Adaptil is a synthetic pheromone replicating the "dog-appeasing pheromone" produced by nursing mothers. It has good evidence for reducing travel-related anxiety and is widely recommended by UK vets.

**How to use:** Spray in the car 15 minutes before your dog gets in. Don't spray directly on the dog. Reapply before each journey.

**Available at:** Pets at Home, Amazon, VetUK — approximately £12–16 for travel spray.

**Evidence:** Clinical trials published in *Veterinary Record* show significant reduction in anxiety-related symptoms including vomiting.

### YuCALM

YuCALM is a veterinary-strength calming supplement containing L-theanine, Lemon Balm, and B vitamins. It's particularly effective for anxiety-driven travel sickness.

**Best use:** Load up for 4–6 weeks to see full effect. Can also be used as a one-off higher dose 2 hours before travel for occasional journeys.

**Available at:** Amazon, Pets at Home, Viovet — approximately £20–30 for 60 tablets.

### Lavender

Some owners report reduced stress with lavender — a small cotton pad with a drop of diluted lavender oil placed near (not on) the dog's crate. Evidence is anecdotal but it's safe and low-cost to try.

**Warning:** Never use essential oils directly on dogs. Concentrated lavender is toxic if ingested.

## Veterinary Treatment: Prescription Options

If natural methods and management changes haven't resolved the problem after 4–6 weeks, speak to your vet. There are effective prescription medications available.

### Cerenia (Maropitant)

The gold standard prescription medication for dog travel sickness in the UK. Licensed specifically for travel sickness in dogs.

**How it works:** Acts on the vomiting centre in the brain, blocking the NK1 receptor pathway. Unlike older antihistamine-type medications, Cerenia targets the root cause rather than just sedating the dog.

**Dosing:** 8mg/kg given 2 hours before travel. Can be used on consecutive days but vet supervision recommended.

**Effectiveness:** Clinical trials showed complete prevention of vomiting in 93% of dogs (Zoetis/NOAH data). Significant reduction in other symptoms including salivation and restlessness.

**Side effects:** Mild sedation, temporary pain at injection site (if injectable form). Oral tablets well tolerated.

**Cost:** £15–30 per treatment from your vet. Worth it for essential journeys or when all else has failed.

### Antihistamines

Some vets recommend over-the-counter antihistamines such as Benadryl (diphenhydramine) for mild travel sickness. These work via light sedation and have some anti-nausea effect.

**Important:** Always check with your vet before giving any human medication. Dosing is weight-dependent and some formulations contain xylitol (toxic to dogs). Never give cetirizine (Piriton contains chlorphenamine — ask your vet specifically about this).

**Effectiveness:** Less targeted than Cerenia. Better for anxiety-driven symptoms than true vestibular motion sickness.

### Anxiolytics for Anxiety-Driven Sickness

For dogs where anxiety is the primary driver, your vet may prescribe a short-acting anxiolytic such as:
- **Trazodone** — short-acting, given 1–2 hours before travel
- **Alprazolam** — benzodiazepine, licensed for use in dogs in the UK under certain conditions

These are situational medications, not long-term solutions. Best combined with the desensitisation programme above.

## Travel Gear: Setting Up the Car for a Sick-Prone Dog

The right equipment significantly reduces symptoms. Here's what works.

### Travel Crates vs Harnesses

**Travel crate (recommended for motion-sick dogs):**
- Provides a den-like space that reduces visual motion
- Forward-facing positioning reduces vestibular conflict
- Prevents the dog from watching out of side windows
- Best: Variocage (crash-tested, UK legal compliance), Ruffwear Haul Bag (soft, portable)

**Safety harnesses:**
- Good for dogs that are calm but need restraint
- Ensure it's crash-tested — look for FCA (Finite Capacity Assessment) certified harnesses
- Sleepypod Clickit Sport remains the gold standard for safety testing

### Seat Covers & Clean-Up Kit

Accept that accidents happen and prepare:
- Waterproof boot liner and rear seat cover
- Enzymatic cleaner (removes odour fully — regular cleaners don't, which can create a negative association)
- Roll of paper towels and biodegradable bags
- Spare collar and lead if the dog gets distressed and needs out quickly

### Travel Water Bowl

A collapsible travel bowl is essential for rest stops. Dogs that arrive at a rest stop dehydrated are more likely to be sick on the next leg. Ruffwear Quencher and Dexas Snack Duo are good UK-available options.

## The Complete Pre-Journey Checklist

Run through this before every journey with a travel-sick dog:

- [ ] Last meal: 2–3 hours minimum before departure
- [ ] Adaptil: sprayed in car 15 minutes ago
- [ ] YuCALM / ginger: given 30–60 minutes ago (if using)
- [ ] Cerenia: given 2 hours ago (if prescribed)
- [ ] Car temperature: cool, air conditioning on
- [ ] Window: cracked 5–10cm on dog's side
- [ ] Crate/harness: forward-facing and secured
- [ ] Clean-up kit in easy reach
- [ ] Collapsible water bowl and water in the car
- [ ] Journey broken into segments if >2 hours (rest stops planned)
- [ ] Destination: positive whenever possible (not vet/groomer for desensitisation journeys)

## Breed-Specific Guide

### French Bulldog / Brachycephalic Breeds

The double challenge: respiratory compromise makes nausea worse, and brachycephalic breeds already run hotter. Keep the car very cool. Short journeys only until desensitised. Cerenia is particularly valuable here — discuss with your vet. Avoid rear-facing positions entirely.

### Labradors

Generally good travellers but prone to anxiety-driven sickness if early car experiences were negative. The desensitisation programme works very well. Natural remedies (Adaptil + ginger) often sufficient without medication.

### Spaniels

Cocker and Springer Spaniels are ear-infection prone, which disrupts the vestibular system. If travel sickness develops suddenly in a previously settled Spaniel, get ears checked first — it may not be the car at all.

### Greyhounds & Sighthounds

Often excellent travellers once they've learned to lie down. A comfortable padded crate base helps. Main challenge is temperature regulation — prone to overheating in cars. Keep cool.

### Puppies (All Breeds)

Maximum patience. Most puppy travel sickness resolves naturally. Focus on positive associations (end journeys at parks, friends' houses, not just vet) and gradual exposure. Avoid medications in puppies under 16 weeks unless vet-directed.

### Senior Dogs

Sudden onset of travel sickness in a previously settled older dog warrants a vet visit — idiopathic vestibular disease (often misdiagnosed as stroke) can present this way. Very treatable but needs proper diagnosis.

## When to See a Vet

Seek veterinary advice if:
- Vomiting is severe (more than 3 times on one journey)
- Dog is vomiting blood
- Dog is unable to walk or balance normally after a journey
- Travel sickness developed suddenly in an adult dog with no previous history
- Natural/management approaches haven't improved things after 4–6 weeks
- You need a prescription (Cerenia) for an essential upcoming journey

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My dog drools but never actually vomits — does that count as travel sickness?**
A: Yes. Drooling is one of the primary signs of nausea in dogs. The same causes and treatments apply. Many dogs experience significant nausea without vomiting.

**Q: Can I give my dog Kwells (for humans) to stop travel sickness?**
A: Don't. Kwells contains hyoscine hydrobromide, which is not licensed for dogs and can cause excessive sedation and other side effects. Speak to your vet about appropriate medications.

**Q: My puppy is fine on long motorway journeys but sick on short local trips — why?**
A: Smooth, straight motorway driving creates much less vestibular conflict than the stops, starts, and corners of local driving. This is actually a good sign — it suggests a true motion sickness cause rather than anxiety, and suggests the vestibular system will mature with age.

**Q: Should I put my dog in the front seat where they can see more?**
A: Not recommended. Airbags are a serious injury risk for dogs in the front. A rear-facing crate in the boot or secured on the back seat facing forward is safer and generally just as effective.

**Q: Will travel sickness get better on its own?**
A: For puppies under 18 months, yes — often significantly. For adult dogs with anxiety-driven sickness, it can get worse without intervention (the negative association reinforces itself). Treatment works best the earlier you start.

**Q: How long does desensitisation take?**
A: Most dogs show meaningful improvement in 3–4 weeks with a consistent programme. Some highly anxious dogs take 6–8 weeks. A few require medication alongside desensitisation to break the anxiety cycle first.
