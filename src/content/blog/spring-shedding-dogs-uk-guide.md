## Spring Shedding in Dogs: Why April Turns Your Home Into a Fur Factory

April arrives, the clocks go forward, and suddenly your black leggings are covered in enough fur to build a second dog.

If you're wondering whether your dog is moulting excessively — or why they seem to shed twice as much in spring as any other time of year — you're not alone. Spring shedding is one of the most common concerns dog owners bring to their vets every April.

The good news: in the vast majority of cases, it's entirely normal. The better news: with the right grooming routine, you can manage it without losing your mind (or your vacuum cleaner).

This guide covers everything you need to know about spring shedding in dogs — why it happens, which breeds are most affected, what a healthy vs abnormal shed looks like, and a practical week-by-week grooming routine to get you through the fur season.

---

## Why Do Dogs Shed More in Spring?

Dog shedding is driven by **photoperiod** — the number of daylight hours in a day. As days lengthen through March and April, your dog's body registers the change via the pineal gland and triggers a hormonal cascade that signals the coat to shift.

The spring shed is your dog dropping their **winter undercoat** — the thick, insulating layer grown during autumn and winter — in preparation for a lighter summer coat. This process is sometimes called "blowing coat," and in double-coated breeds, it's dramatic.

**What triggers the spring shed:**
- Increasing daylight hours (the dominant trigger)
- Rising temperatures (secondary factor)
- Hormonal changes linked to the photoperiod shift

Dogs kept exclusively indoors under artificial lighting can shed more continuously throughout the year, because the artificial light disrupts the seasonal photoperiod signal. This is why some owners notice their indoor dogs "always seem to be shedding" — the seasonal peaks are blurred.

**The two annual shedding peaks:**
- **Spring (March–May):** Winter coat drops, lighter summer coat grows in
- **Autumn (September–November):** Summer coat drops, winter undercoat grows in

Spring shedding is typically heavier than autumn shedding, because the winter coat is denser.

## Double-Coated vs Single-Coated Breeds: Why It Matters

Not all shedding is equal. The degree of spring shedding depends primarily on whether your dog is double-coated or single-coated.

### Double-Coated Breeds (Heavy Seasonal Shedders)

Double-coated breeds have two distinct layers: a dense, woolly undercoat close to the skin, and a longer, coarser outer coat (guard hairs) that repels water and dirt. It's the undercoat that "blows" in spring.

**High-shed double-coated breeds:**

| Breed | Shed Level | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| German Shepherd | 🔴 Very heavy | Year-round + 2 heavy seasonal blows |
| Siberian Husky | 🔴 Very heavy | Dramatic spring blow — can fill bin bags |
| Golden Retriever | 🔴 Very heavy | Dense undercoat; feathering adds complexity |
| Labrador Retriever | 🟠 Heavy | Dense otter tail + body undercoat |
| Border Collie | 🟠 Heavy | Rough-coated more than smooth |
| Corgi (Pembroke & Cardigan) | 🟠 Heavy | Surprisingly heavy for small dog |
| Chow Chow | 🔴 Very heavy | Double coat + mane; major spring blow |
| Akita | 🔴 Very heavy | One of the heaviest seasonal shedders |
| Bernese Mountain Dog | 🟠 Heavy | Long double coat |
| Alaskan Malamute | 🔴 Very heavy | Arctic working coat; dramatic blowing |

### Single-Coated Breeds (Moderate or Low Shedders)

Single-coated breeds have one layer without a dense undercoat. They shed, but without the dramatic seasonal blows of double-coated dogs.

| Breed | Shed Level | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Poodle | 🟢 Low | Hair (not fur) — grows continuously, minimal shed |
| Maltese | 🟢 Low | Single silky coat; minimal shedding |
| Bichon Frisé | 🟢 Low | Curly single coat; hair sheds into coat |
| Shih Tzu | 🟡 Low-moderate | Some seasonal increase |
| Dachshund (smooth) | 🟡 Moderate | Noticeable spring shed, manageable |
| Boxer | 🟡 Moderate | Short single coat |
| Greyhound/Whippet | 🟡 Low-moderate | Fine short coat, minimal volume |
| French Bulldog | 🟡 Moderate | Short dense coat with spring increase |

**Note on "hypoallergenic" breeds:** No dog is truly hypoallergenic — all dogs produce dander (the actual allergen). Low-shedding breeds produce less airborne dander, which can reduce allergic reactions, but they're not allergen-free.

## What Normal Spring Shedding Looks Like

Normal spring shedding looks like a lot of fur — and for double-coated breeds, it can seem alarming. Here's what to expect:

**Normal signs:**
- Visible clumps of undercoat coming away during brushing
- Loose fur on furniture, flooring, and clothing
- "Tufts" of undercoat visible on the dog's back and flanks
- The shed fur is soft and fluffy (undercoat texture), not the coarser outer guard hairs
- The skin beneath looks healthy — pink and clear
- Your dog is otherwise well: eating normally, active, good energy

**Timeline:** The main spring blow typically lasts **3–6 weeks** in most breeds, peaking mid-April to mid-May in the UK. After the blow, shedding settles back to a lower baseline until the autumn.

## When to Be Concerned: Abnormal Shedding

Most spring shedding is completely normal. But some shedding patterns warrant a vet visit.

**See your vet if you notice:**

| Sign | Possible cause |
|------|---------------|
| Bald patches or thinning in specific areas | Alopecia, ringworm, mange, hormonal issue |
| Red, inflamed, or flaky skin beneath the shed | Dermatitis, atopy, secondary infection |
| Excessive scratching alongside shedding | Fleas, mites, allergies |
| Dull, brittle coat quality | Nutritional deficiency, hypothyroidism |
| Shedding with weight gain, lethargy, cold intolerance | Hypothyroidism (common in middle-aged dogs) |
| Shedding after giving birth (bitch) | Normal post-whelping; monitor |
| Shedding continuing heavily past 8 weeks | Worth reviewing with vet |
| Black/brown dots at skin level (walking) | Flea dirt — treat immediately |

**Hypothyroidism note:** This is one of the most commonly missed causes of excessive shedding in middle-aged dogs, particularly in breeds like Golden Retrievers, Labradors, and Cocker Spaniels. Classic signs include shedding + weight gain + low energy + cold sensitivity. A blood test confirms it. Treatment (daily medication) is effective and affordable.

## Spring Grooming Routine: Week-by-Week

For double-coated breeds going through a full spring blow, this is the routine that works.

### Week 1–2: The De-shed

**Goal:** Remove as much loose undercoat as possible before it ends up everywhere else.

**Tools needed:**
- Slicker brush (daily)
- Undercoat rake or deshedding tool (every other day)
- Grooming table or non-slip mat (optional but helps)

**Steps:**
1. Start with the slicker brush — work in sections from rear to front, with the coat growth
2. Follow with the undercoat rake through the densest areas (haunches, neck, belly)
3. For breeds with feathering (Goldens, Collies), use a pin brush on the longer leg and chest fur
4. Work in 15-minute sessions — shedding season can make dogs twitchy if you go too long

**The bath + blow-dry method:** Many groomers recommend a warm bath with a deshedding shampoo during the blow period, followed by a high-velocity blow dry. This loosens the undercoat dramatically and can significantly shorten the blow period. If you have access to a dog dryer (or a groomer who uses one), this is worth doing once at the start of the blow.

### Week 3–4: Maintaining the Flow

**Goal:** Keep the undercoat moving out so it doesn't mat.

**Matting risk:** During a heavy blow, if loose undercoat isn't removed regularly, it can mat against the skin — particularly in the armpits, groin, and behind the ears. Mats can cause skin irritation and, in severe cases, trap moisture and bacteria.

**Routine:**
- Daily slicker brush (10 minutes)
- Undercoat rake every 2–3 days
- Check for mats in high-risk areas daily
- If you find a mat: work from the outside in with a mat splitter or your fingers before any brush or comb

### Week 5–6: The Tail End

**Goal:** Clear the last of the loose undercoat and assess coat condition.

By now, shedding should be visibly reducing. The coat should start to look more settled and even.

**Post-blow care:**
- Continue weekly brushing (now easier)
- Assess coat and skin condition — spring is a good time for a flea/tick prevention review
- Book a professional groom if coat is looking uneven or there are any matting areas you couldn't clear

## Diet and Supplements: Supporting a Healthy Spring Coat

A healthy coat sheds more cleanly and regrows more quickly. Nutrition plays a significant role.

**Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA + DHA):** The most evidence-backed nutritional support for coat health. Reduces inflammation, supports skin barrier function, and improves coat shine and texture. Sources:
- Oily fish (sardines, salmon) in fresh or raw diets
- Fish oil supplements — look for EPA + DHA content on the label, not just "fish oil"
- Omega-3 specific dog supplements (Yumega Plus, VetriScience Omega-3 supplements)

**Recommended dose:** As a guide, BSAVA suggests approximately 50–70mg EPA+DHA per kg bodyweight per day for skin support, but check with your vet for your specific dog's needs.

**What to avoid during shedding season:**
- Sudden diet changes (increase stress shedding)
- Low-quality foods with poor protein content (coat quality reflects protein intake)
- Over-supplementing with Vitamin A (can paradoxically worsen shedding in excess)

**Signs of nutritional shedding:** Dull, brittle fur. Excessive dandruff alongside the shed. These suggest the coat isn't being supported nutritionally, not just that the season is turning.

## Breed-Specific Spring Shedding Advice

### Labrador Retriever

Labs have a dense, short double coat and are year-round moderate shedders with two heavy seasonal blows. The spring blow often surprises new Lab owners.

**Key advice:** The rubber grooming glove works brilliantly on Labs — better than a standard brush for many owners. A deshedding shampoo bath in early April followed by a blow-dry can dramatically reduce the duration of the blow.

### German Shepherd

GSDs are among the heaviest shedders of any breed. Their spring blow can last 6–8 weeks and produce truly remarkable quantities of fur.

**Key advice:** Daily undercoat rake is non-negotiable during the blow. Professional grooming with a high-velocity dryer at the start of the blow can reduce fur volume by up to 70%. Also: GSDs can develop coat-related skin issues if their undercoat isn't moved — check for "hot spots" (moist, reddened skin) during the blow period.

### Golden Retriever

Goldens have feathering on the legs, chest, tail, and belly that requires separate attention from the undercoat. The feathering can mat if left ungroomed during the blow.

**Key advice:** Use a pin brush or comb (not just a slicker) through the feathering every day during the blow. A mat splitter is worth keeping in your grooming kit.

### French Bulldog

Frenchies are single-coated but do have a noticeable spring shed. The short coat means hairs embed in fabrics more readily than the fluffy shedding of double-coated breeds.

**Key advice:** Daily rubber mitt grooming during the blow. Use a lint roller on upholstery regularly. A deshedding shampoo bath is effective. Watch for skin irritation — Frenchies are prone to atopy and the spring pollen increase can coincide with shedding to create composite skin issues.

### Poodle / Doodle Crosses

Poodles themselves have very low shedding. Doodle crosses (Labradoodle, Goldendoodle, Cockapoo) have highly variable coat types depending on which parent's genetics dominate. A "Poodle coat" Doodle will have minimal shedding and grow continuously. A "Retriever coat" Doodle will shed normally.

**Key advice for Doodles:** If your Doodle sheds, groom as per the relevant retriever breed. If it doesn't, maintain regular professional clips (every 6–8 weeks) — the non-shedding coat grows continuously and will matt without cutting.

## Spring Shedding and Allergies (in People)

If someone in your household is allergic to dogs, spring shedding season can be particularly challenging. The increased dander in the air during a heavy shed can spike symptoms.

**Management strategies:**
- Groom outside where possible during the blow period
- HEPA air purifier in main living spaces
- Wash dog bedding weekly during the blow
- Brush the dog daily (removes loose dander before it disperses)
- Vacuum with a high-filtration vacuum (HEPA-rated)
- Antihistamines may be appropriate for human family members during peak weeks (consult GP)

**Note:** The allergen in dog dander is Can f 1, a protein found in skin cells, saliva, and urine — not the fur itself. The fur carries the dander, but it's the protein that triggers allergic reactions. This is why "hypoallergenic" breeds don't eliminate allergy risk — they just produce and disperse less dander.

## Protecting Your Home During the Spring Blow

A few practical measures that make a real difference during peak shedding:

**Furniture:**
- Washable slipcovers or throws on sofas and chairs during the blow period
- Lint rollers in every room — buy in bulk (Amazon 3-packs are the most economical)
- Rubber squeegee on carpet and stairs — more effective than a vacuum for embedded short hairs (Labradors, Frenchies)

**Floors:**
- Sweep or mop hard floors daily — vacuuming alone circulates fur into the air
- Door mats at garden door catches a lot of loose fur shed during outdoor grooming

**Car:**
- Boot liner or seat covers during the blow
- A dedicated pet-hair-specific rubber brush for upholstery

**Washing:**
- Add a laundry mesh bag to washes — catches fur before it clogs the filter
- Pet-specific laundry additives (like Furzone) help loosen embedded fur in the wash cycle

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it normal for my dog to shed in huge clumps in April?**

Yes, for double-coated breeds this is completely normal. The loose undercoat comes out in clumps during the spring blow — you may be able to pull large tufts out with your fingers. This is expected and not a sign of a health problem, as long as the skin beneath looks healthy and your dog is behaving normally.

**My dog never used to shed this much — is something wrong?**

If shedding has increased significantly compared to previous years, it's worth considering: Has their diet changed? Have they been unwell recently? Are they showing any other symptoms (lethargy, weight change, skin irritation)? If any of these are present, a vet check is sensible. Sometimes a heavier shed is simply because last year's wasn't as thorough — it varies year to year.

**Should I shave my double-coated dog to reduce shedding?**

No. This is one of the most common grooming mistakes for double-coated breeds. Shaving a double-coated dog removes both the insulating undercoat and the protective outer guard hairs. The coat often regrows incorrectly ("coat blow" or "post-clipping alopecia"), and the dog loses its natural temperature regulation. It also doesn't prevent shedding — the hairs just come back shorter. Regular deshedding is the right approach.

**How long does the spring shed last?**

For most breeds, 3–6 weeks. For heavy double-coated breeds (GSD, Husky, Akita), it can run 6–8 weeks. With regular grooming and one or two deshedding baths, you can often shorten the blow period noticeably.

**Can stress cause more shedding?**

Yes. Cortisol (the stress hormone) can increase shedding independently of the seasonal trigger. Changes at home, fireworks, illness, or a new pet can all cause a stress shed. This typically settles once the stressor passes. If it persists, consult your vet.

**My dog's been desexed — does this affect shedding?**

Desexed dogs can sometimes have slightly different coat texture and shed patterns, particularly bitches after spaying. Some owners report heavier shedding post-spay. This is hormonally driven and usually stabilises. Omega-3 supplementation can help with coat quality if it changes noticeably.

## When to See a Groomer

Some shedding situations are best handled by a professional:

- **First spring blow with a new double-coated dog** — a professional groomer can assess coat type, set you up with the right tools, and demonstrate technique
- **Matting** — if the coat has matted during the blow, do not attempt to cut out mats at home without proper tools (you risk cutting skin)
- **"Coat blow" not resolving after 8 weeks** — a groomer can often dislodge persistent undercoat with specialist tools
- **Large or difficult breeds** — a GSD or Husky undergoing a full spring blow is easier for two people than one

The Kennel Club maintains a list of accredited groomers at thekennelclub.org.uk. Look for groomers affiliated with the British Dog Groomers' Association (BDGA) or City & Guilds qualified.

## The Bottom Line

Spring shedding is one of the most predictable events in the dog-owning calendar — and one of the most manageable with the right approach.

Double-coated breeds will blow their coat for 3–8 weeks starting in March or April. Regular grooming (daily during the peak), a deshedding bath early in the blow, and a lint roller budget will get you through it.

Watch the skin rather than the volume of fur — healthy skin beneath a heavy shed is reassuring. Any irritation, bald patches, or behavioural changes alongside the shedding are reasons to see your vet.

The fur season is real. But it does end.
