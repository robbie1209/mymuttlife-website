## Introduction

Deciding to add a second dog to your household is one of the most exciting things a dog owner can do. Two dogs can be great companions, keep each other active, and — if things go well — form a bond that's genuinely heartwarming to watch.

The problem is, a lot of introductions go wrong. Not because the dogs aren't compatible, but because the humans rush it.

Dogs don't naturally share space, resources, or attention the way we assume they will. A badly handled first meeting can set the tone for months of tension. But get it right, and two dogs who might have struggled can become inseparable.

This guide covers everything you need to know: when and where to make the introduction, how to read body language, what to do in the first hours and days at home, and how to manage common problems like resource guarding and jealousy.

Whether you're bringing home a puppy, a rescue, or an adult dog from a breeder — this is how you do it.

## Before You Bring the New Dog Home

### Is Your Resident Dog Ready?

Before you even think about a second dog, honest question: is your current dog ready?

A few things to consider:

**Health:** Older dogs with painful joints, sight loss, or illness may find a bouncy new companion overwhelming. Talk to your vet before adding to the household.

**Behaviour:** If your resident dog has existing behaviour issues — reactivity, resource guarding, separation anxiety — these will almost certainly get worse with an unmanaged introduction. Work with a qualified behaviourist (look for ABTC-accredited professionals at abtcouncil.org.uk) before bringing home dog number two.

**Lifestyle:** A high-energy working breed paired with a gentle senior is a mismatch that can cause real stress. Think about energy level, play style, and social preferences, not just "they're both dogs."

### Choosing the Right Match

Research suggests opposite-sex pairings tend to get along more easily, though there's plenty of variation. More important than sex is:

- **Energy level match** — a Collie and a Basset Hound will lead very different lives
- **Play style compatibility** — rough-and-tumble terriers vs cautious spaniels can clash
- **Size differential** — a tiny dog can be injured by an enthusiastic large breed during play
- **Age gap** — a puppy and a senior need careful management; middle-aged adults often integrate most smoothly

Many rescue centres do temperament assessments and can advise on compatibility. Use this — it's there for a reason.

## Step 1: The Neutral Ground Meeting

**Never introduce two dogs at home first.** Your resident dog has already claimed the house, garden, and you. Bringing a stranger into their territory puts them on the defensive before the relationship has even started.

Instead, choose a neutral location:

- A quiet section of a local park (avoid peak dog-walking times)
- A friends' garden neither dog has visited
- A car park or quiet open space with no strong scent history

### What You'll Need
- Two calm, confident handlers — one per dog
- Two well-fitted harnesses (not collars — easier to control, less pressure on neck)
- Two leads of the same length (around 2m)
- High-value treats for both dogs (something genuinely exciting — small pieces of chicken or cheese)
- Poo bags, water

### The Parallel Walk

This is the technique recommended by Dogs Trust, and it works.

**How to do it:**

1. Both dogs arrive at the meeting spot separately (different cars, or at different times by a few minutes)
2. Walk them in the same direction but about 10–15 metres apart — far enough that they can see each other but aren't focused on each other
3. Keep moving. Movement is calming for dogs; standing still allows tension to build
4. Reward both dogs generously for calm, relaxed behaviour — looking away from the other dog, sniffing the ground, checking in with you
5. Gradually close the gap over the course of the walk — only if both dogs remain relaxed
6. After 10–15 minutes of parallel walking, allow a brief sniff — a few seconds, side-on, not face-to-face
7. Walk on again, then allow another brief greeting

**What you're looking for:**
- Loose, wiggly body language
- Sniffing at a normal pace
- Mutual interest and disengagement
- Play bows (a great sign)

**Warning signs to watch for:**
- Stiff, tall posture
- Hard stare (fixed gaze without blinking)
- Tail raised high and stiff (not wagging broadly)
- Growling
- One dog constantly trying to get away while the other pursues

If you see any of these, increase the distance and slow down.

### What If It Doesn't Go Well?

Don't panic. A tense first meeting doesn't mean the dogs won't get along. It means the introduction was too fast.

Separate them calmly, give both dogs space, and try again another day at a greater distance with even higher-value rewards. Some dogs need three or four parallel walks before they're comfortable enough for direct greeting.

## Step 2: Arriving Home

Assuming the neutral ground meeting went reasonably well, it's time to bring the new dog home. This part is just as important.

### Before They Arrive

**Remove resources.** Pick up:
- All food and water bowls (from both dogs' usual spots)
- Toys, especially favourites
- Chews and treats
- Your resident dog's bed (temporarily)

This isn't permanent — it's just to remove anything either dog might guard during the early days.

**Create a separate space for the new dog.** A crate with a comfortable bed, or a room they can access via a baby gate, gives the new dog a retreat and prevents your resident dog from feeling their entire home has been invaded. The [Ellie-Bo Dog Crate](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=dog+crate+ellie-bo) (affiliate) is a solid UK favourite — well-made and easy to fold flat when not needed.

**Put away your resident dog's bed.** They'll get it back in a few days. For now, you don't want the new dog sleeping on it or being told off for approaching it.

### The First Hour at Home

1. Bring both dogs into the house/garden at roughly the same time, still on leads
2. Let them explore without being forced together
3. Praise and reward calm, relaxed behaviour
4. If they show interest in each other — sniffing, gentle investigation — let it happen briefly, then redirect
5. After 20–30 minutes, remove the new dog to their safe space and give your resident dog some solo attention

The goal is controlled, short interactions with plenty of decompression time in between.

## Step 3: The First Week

### Supervised, Then Separated

For the first week, follow a simple rule: **when you can't supervise, separate.**

Use baby gates, a crate for the new dog, or different rooms. This prevents incidents that could permanently damage the relationship before it's had a chance to form.

The [Lindam Stair Gate](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=dog+baby+gate+pressure+fit) (affiliate) is a reliable pressure-fit option that doesn't require drilling and works well between rooms. For dogs who jump, go for a taller gate (at least 80cm).

### Feeding Separately — Always

Feed the dogs in separate rooms for the first few weeks, minimum. Remove bowls immediately after eating.

Food is one of the most common triggers for conflict between dogs. Even dogs who seem relaxed around each other can resource guard around mealtimes. It's not worth the risk.

Slow feeders can help reduce excitement and meal-related stress for both dogs: the [LickiMat Wobble](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=lickimat+wobble) (affiliate) is great for slowing down gulpers and giving the new dog something calming while settling in.

### Managing Attention and Jealousy

Your resident dog may show signs of jealousy — pushing in between you and the new dog, attention-seeking behaviour, or sulking.

**What to do:**
- Greet and attend to your resident dog first in all interactions (this reinforces their existing status)
- Give your resident dog one-on-one time each day — a walk without the new dog, a training session, or simply some quiet time together
- Avoid fussing over the new dog while ignoring the resident dog in the same space
- Don't punish jealousy behaviour — manage it through structure instead

### Sleep Arrangements

Where the dogs sleep matters. For the first few weeks:

- New dog sleeps in their own space (crate or gated room)
- Resident dog keeps their usual sleeping spot
- Don't allow them to share sleeping spaces until the relationship is well established

## Step 4: Building a Positive Relationship

Once the initial settling-in is done (usually 2–4 weeks for most dogs), you can begin actively building positive associations between the two.

### Joint Activities

- **Parallel walks**: Keep doing them, even at home. Walking side by side builds cooperation
- **Shared training sessions**: Teach them that both dogs sitting calmly at the same time earns rewards — this teaches them to exist in the same space without competing
- **Sniff walks**: Let both dogs explore a new area together, off-lead if safe — sniffing is naturally calming and companionable

### Play

When play begins naturally, let it happen — but keep sessions short and calm. Break them up with a "that's enough" before either dog gets over-aroused, then reward both for settling.

Warning signs that play is tipping into conflict:
- One dog constantly pursuing while the other tries to escape
- Play that's not reciprocal (one dog doing all the chasing, one always fleeing)
- Hard, tense body after play
- Growling that sounds sharp and tense rather than playful

Truly reciprocal play should involve role reversal — each dog takes turns being "it." If you don't see this, interrupt and redirect.

### Puzzle Toys and Enrichment

Giving both dogs appropriate mental stimulation reduces competition and boredom-related tension. The [Nina Ottosson Dog Tornado](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=nina+ottosson+dog+tornado) (affiliate) is excellent for individual enrichment — give each dog their own puzzle in separate spaces.

## Introducing a Puppy to an Older Dog

This is a specific scenario that deserves its own section, because puppy introductions have unique challenges.

### The Problem With Puppies

Puppies are socially unaware. They don't read body language well, they play too roughly, they invade personal space — and they don't understand a dog telling them to back off.

An older dog who gives a growl or snap as a warning is doing exactly what dogs are supposed to do. **Don't punish this.** A growl is communication. If you punish the older dog for communicating, you remove their ability to warn — which is when dogs bite without warning.

### Protecting the Older Dog

- Give your older dog escape routes and high spaces the puppy can't reach
- Prevent the puppy from pestering the older dog during rest
- Ensure your older dog gets exercise without the puppy — they shouldn't have to slow down or play when they don't want to
- Watch for signs of stress in the older dog: hiding, eating less, loss of interest in things they normally enjoy

### Managing the Puppy

- Puppies tire out but then restart — prevent overwhelming the older dog by using crate rest for the puppy
- Redirect puppy energy onto appropriate toys and play with you
- Teach the puppy "leave it" early so you have a reliable interrupt

Most older dogs adjust within a few weeks once the puppy learns to read their signals. The relationship that develops is often wonderful — adult dogs teach puppies social rules that we as humans simply can't.

## Introducing a Rescue Dog

Rescue dogs bring an unknown history. Some will have lived with other dogs. Others won't. Some will have trauma around dogs from past experiences.

### What to Expect From a Rescue

The "3-3-3 rule" is widely used in rescue circles:
- **3 days**: Completely overwhelmed, may not show their real personality
- **3 weeks**: Starting to understand the routine, beginning to relax
- **3 months**: Truly settled, real personality emerging

This means judgements made in the first week — "they don't get on" — are often premature. Give the relationship time.

### Temporary Separate Routines

Some rescue dogs need longer separation periods before integration. If the rescue shows signs of significant anxiety or reactivity, work with an ABTC-accredited behaviourist rather than forcing interactions.

## Common Problems and Solutions

### Resource Guarding Between Dogs

**Signs:** Stiffening when the other dog approaches their food, toy, bed, or person. Growling, snapping when the other dog comes close to something they value.

**What to do:**
- Feed separately, always
- Pick up all toys when not supervised
- Trade games: teach both dogs that approach + specific toy = even better treat, then both toys go away
- Never punish growling — it's communication; work on the underlying anxiety instead

### One Dog Bullying the Other

**Signs:** One dog constantly chasing, humping, or body-checking the other. The other dog looks tense, tries to hide, or can't relax.

**What to do:**
- Interrupt and redirect the pursuing dog — don't let sessions continue to the point of stress
- Give the lower-status dog plenty of alone time and relief from the other dog
- Ensure equal but separate attention
- If it continues beyond 4–6 weeks, consult a qualified behaviourist

### Fights

Dogs can and do fight, even in established households. Most fights are brief and cause minimal injury — the important thing is not to escalate.

**In the moment:**
- Do NOT put your hands near their mouths (serious bite injury risk)
- Use noise (clap, bang a surface) to interrupt
- Throw a large object (folded blanket, pillow) between them
- If it continues: wheelbarrow technique — grab each dog by the back legs and pull backward and to the side simultaneously (only safe if you have two people)

**Afterwards:**
- Separate both dogs and allow to calm down (at least 30 minutes)
- Check both dogs for injury
- Identify the trigger and manage it going forward
- If fights are happening more than once, get professional help — don't leave dogs unsupervised until the issue is resolved

## Signs the Introduction Is Going Well

Most introductions progress gradually over 2–8 weeks. Signs you're on the right track:

✅ Both dogs relax in the same room without tension  
✅ Play that's mutual and reciprocal  
✅ Voluntary physical proximity — choosing to lie near each other  
✅ Greeting each other happily when reunited  
✅ Eating and sleeping normally (stress often suppresses appetite)  
✅ Neither dog hiding from the other

## Breed-Specific Considerations

| Breed Type | Notes |
|------------|-------|
| **Terriers** | Strong prey drive + high energy; can be persistent with other dogs. Best matched with similarly bold breeds. |
| **Herding breeds (Border Collie, GSD)** | May try to herd other dogs. Can stress low-energy dogs. Benefit from shared tasks/training. |
| **Sighthounds** | May have strong chase instinct but often gentle and non-confrontational. Usually integrate well. |
| **Guarding breeds (Rottweiler, Cane Corso)** | Confident dogs who may not back down — early socialisation crucial. Adult-to-adult introduction benefits from professional guidance. |
| **Toy breeds** | Can be injured during rough play. Don't rely on size difference to "sort itself out." |
| **Labradors/Golden Retrievers** | Generally sociable and easy to introduce. Energy levels can overwhelm more reserved dogs. |

## When to Get Professional Help

Call in a qualified behaviourist if:

- Fights are happening regularly
- One dog is showing prolonged stress (not eating, hiding, trembling)
- The introduction has stalled after 6 weeks with no improvement
- Either dog has a known history of dog aggression

Find ABTC-accredited professionals at **abtcouncil.org.uk**, or contact **Dogs Trust Dog School** for group classes that can help with socialisation.

## FAQs

**Q: How long does it take for two dogs to get along?**  
A: Most dogs settle into a comfortable relationship within 2–8 weeks, though full integration — sharing beds, playing freely — can take 3–6 months. Rescue dogs may need longer.

**Q: My resident dog growled at the new dog. Should I be worried?**  
A: Not necessarily. Growling is normal canine communication. Watch the context: a brief growl that the new dog responds to by backing off is healthy communication. Repeated, escalating growling or snapping needs management — give more space, slow the introduction, and consult a behaviourist if it continues.

**Q: Should I let them "sort it out" themselves?**  
A: No. Unsupervised conflicts can create lasting negative associations. Managed, positive interactions build relationships; unmanaged conflict damages them.

**Q: My older dog seems depressed since the new dog arrived. What can I do?**  
A: This is common in the first few weeks. Prioritise daily one-on-one time with your older dog, keep their routine consistent, ensure they have a space the new dog can't enter, and visit the vet if appetite is affected for more than a week.

**Q: Is it better to get a puppy or an adult dog as a second dog?**  
A: Both have pros and cons. Puppies are socially malleable but exhausting and require careful management around older dogs. Adult dogs have known personalities (especially if from rescue with a behaviour assessment) but may take longer to settle. There's no universal right answer — it depends on your resident dog.

**Q: Do dogs always prefer dogs of the opposite sex?**  
A: As a general rule, opposite-sex pairings tend to have fewer dominance conflicts — but individual temperament matters far more than sex. Some same-sex pairs get along brilliantly; some opposite-sex pairs clash. Focus on personality and energy compatibility over sex alone.

## Summary

Introducing two dogs is rarely instant — but it's absolutely achievable with patience, structure, and the right approach.

The golden rules:
1. **Neutral ground first** — never at home
2. **Parallel walking builds trust** — use it every time
3. **Manage resources** — food, toys, beds away until stable
4. **Separate when unsupervised** — for weeks, not days
5. **Give your resident dog priority** — they were there first
6. **Don't rush** — most failures come from going too fast

Get the introduction right and you'll have two dogs who make each other's lives better. It's worth taking the time.
